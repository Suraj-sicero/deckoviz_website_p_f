## Introduction
Nature doesn't build for us.

That's the first thing to understand. The Grand Canyon wasn't carved to be looked at. The aurora borealis doesn't perform on a schedule. A humpback whale breaching the Atlantic surface isn't doing it for the photograph. And yet these things stop us cold. They reach past language and logic into something older, the part of us that has been standing at the edge of something vast and feeling small and grateful for it for as long as we've existed.

That feeling is exactly what a wall should hold.

Not a screensaver. Not a postcard. A piece of art that carries the actual emotional weight of a place or creature or phenomenon, the quality of light on a specific morning, the scale of something that took a million years to form, the color of a living thing that evolution spent an eternity arriving at. Vizzy can pull from our curated library of nature photography, some of the finest images of the natural world ever taken. It can generate original artworks that translate a landscape into paint or abstraction. It can create graphic nature posters in the tradition of the great natural history illustrators. Or all three, for the same wonder, on the same frame, cycling through different moods.

Here are a hundred of nature's greatest achievements. What they are, why they matter, and what Vizzy can put on your wall to bring them home.

## The Great Wonders

### 1. The Aurora Borealis - Arctic Circle
The Northern Lights are caused by charged particles from the sun colliding with gases in the Earth's atmosphere, which is the least magical accurate description of something in the natural world.
What you actually see: curtains of green, violet, and white light moving across the sky in real time, folding and shifting like something alive, above a frozen landscape that has gone completely silent. People who see it for the first time frequently cry without knowing why. It happens in seconds, without warning, and it is different every time.

### 2. The Grand Canyon - Arizona, USA
446 kilometres long, 29 kilometres wide, 1.8 kilometres deep, carved by the Colorado River over five to six million years through rock that is itself 1.8 billion years old.
Numbers don't help. The Grand Canyon is one of the things in nature that the human eye simply cannot process all at once. You stand at the south rim and your brain refuses to accept the scale, keeps trying to reduce it to something manageable, and keeps failing. The light changes the canyon every hour: dawn turns it pink, noon bleaches it pale, sunset turns the entire gorge amber and red, and the shadows in the side canyons go so deep they become their own color.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The south rim at sunrise, the canyon filling with first light from the east, the Colorado River a thin silver thread 1.8 kilometres below. 🖼️ Poster - A WPA national parks-style Grand Canyon poster: the layered strata in bold flat color bands, ochre and rust and deep red and pale cream, the canyon depth suggested through receding planes. 🎨 Artwork - A geological abstract: the canyon's rock strata as a painting, the layers of time rendered as horizontal color bands from ancient deep purple to pale limestone at the top, the river a thin blue line at the base.

### 3. The Great Barrier Reef - Queensland, Australia
The largest living structure on Earth, visible from space.
Over 2,300 kilometres of coral reef built by tiny polyps over millions of years, supporting more species of fish than any other marine ecosystem on the planet. The reef's colors underwater, the impossibly saturated blues and greens and the coral in every shade from white to deep orange to violet, are colors that seem to exceed what biological systems should be able to produce. It is currently under severe threat from ocean warming, and large sections have bleached in recent years. Seeing it now, at full health, is increasingly urgent.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - An underwater image looking up through a coral garden, fish moving in shafts of blue-green light, the surface visible above as a fractured mirror. 🖼️ Poster - A natural history-style reef poster: the coral formations and their fish rendered in the meticulous illustration style of 19th-century marine biology, scientific and beautiful. 🎨 Artwork - An abstract underwater painting: the reef's colors without its forms, pure saturated blue and turquoise and coral orange in fluid, layered paint, the sensation of being underwater translated into pure color.

### 4. Mount Everest - Nepal/Tibet
8,849 metres above sea level. The highest point on Earth's surface.
Everest is not the most technically demanding climb in the world, but it is the most extreme environment a human being can inhabit even briefly: at the summit, there is one third of the oxygen available at sea level, temperatures can reach minus sixty degrees Celsius, and winds can exceed 280 kilometres per hour. The mountain's south face, the Khumbu Icefall, the Hillary Step: these have become as mythologized as any landscape feature in history. Above the Death Zone, the human body begins consuming itself to stay alive.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - Everest from Kala Patthar at dawn, the mountain's south face catching first light above a sea of cloud, the summit plume of ice crystals visible against a deep blue sky. 🖼️ Poster - A classic mountaineering expedition poster: Everest's silhouette in bold graphic form above the Nepalese foothills, deep blue and white and the warm orange of high altitude sunlight. 🎨 Artwork - An abstract expressionist mountain painting: Everest's form suggested through slabs of white and blue-grey, the violence of the weather visible in the paint's texture, no romanticism, just the mountain as it actually is.

### 5. The Amazon Rainforest - South America
The largest tropical rainforest on Earth, covering 5.5 million square kilometres across nine countries.
The Amazon produces 20 percent of the world's oxygen, absorbs an enormous quantity of the world's carbon, and contains an estimated 10 percent of all species on Earth, many of which have never been catalogued. The forest canopy viewed from above is a landscape of its own: an undulating green surface that stretches to the horizon in every direction, broken only by the river system below. The Amazon River carries more water to the sea than the next seven largest rivers combined.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - An aerial view of the Amazon canopy at dawn, mist rising from the forest in columns, the river visible as a dark curve in the distance. 🖼️ Poster - A biodiversity poster in the style of the great botanical illustrators: a cross-section of the forest from canopy to floor, every layer populated with its specific species, intricate and alive. 🎨 Artwork - A dense figurative jungle painting in the tradition of Henri Rousseau: the forest interior rendered with the hyper-real clarity of a dream, every leaf precise, the light filtering green and gold.

### 6. Victoria Falls - Zambia/Zimbabwe
The world's largest waterfall by combined width and height.
Named by David Livingstone in 1855 after the British queen, but known locally as Mosi-oa-Tunya: The Smoke That Thunders. The name is more accurate. The falls produce a mist column visible from 50 kilometres away, a permanent cloud generated by the Zambezi River dropping 108 metres into a narrow basalt gorge below. During peak flow, 500 million litres of water per minute pass over the edge. The roar is audible from 40 kilometres. The spray creates a rainforest on the gorge's rim that exists nowhere else in the surrounding savanna.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The falls from the Zimbabwean side, the full width of the curtain visible, the spray rising into a permanent rainbow, the gorge below invisible in the mist. 🖼️ Poster - A bold Africa travel poster: the falls in silhouette, the spray column rising above them, a deep indigo sky, the graphic simplicity of the most dramatic waterfall on Earth. 🎨 Artwork - A mist and motion painting: the falls rendered not as a static image but as pure movement, the water and the spray and the light through the mist as a composition of white and blue and the faintest green.

### 7. The Serengeti Migration - Tanzania/Kenya
Every year, 1.5 million wildebeest, 400,000 zebra, and 200,000 gazelle move in a clockwise circuit across the Serengeti and Masai Mara in what is the largest overland migration of animals on Earth.
The Mara River crossing is the migration's most dramatic moment: the wildebeest must cross the crocodile-filled river, and they spend days on the bank before the first animal commits, at which point the herd follows in a mass that can last hours. The dust clouds raised by 1.5 million animals moving across the savanna are visible from altitude. The migration has been happening for roughly a million years.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Mara River crossing at peak flow, thousands of wildebeest in the water simultaneously, crocodiles visible at the edges, dust and chaos and the primal scale of it. 🖼️ Poster - A Serengeti silhouette poster: the wildebeest migration at dusk, the animals rendered as dark shapes against an orange and red sky, the acacia trees above, Africa at its most graphic. 🎨 Artwork - A vast figurative painting of the migration on open savanna, the herd stretching to the horizon, dust rising, the scale of the movement making the individual animals almost invisible in the whole.

### 8. The Northern Lights (Aurora Australis) - Antarctica
The Southern Lights, the aurora australis, seen from the Antarctic continent, have an advantage over their Northern counterpart: no light pollution, no trees, no competing horizon.
The aurora seen from the Antarctic plateau, at 3,000 metres altitude in the clearest air on Earth, above an unbroken white landscape that extends to every horizon, is arguably the most complete experience of the phenomenon available. Scientists working at Antarctic stations have described it as the closest thing to a religious experience they have ever had, and these are people who came to Antarctica specifically to study physics.

### 9. The Galápagos Islands - Ecuador
The living laboratory where Charles Darwin's observations contributed to the theory of natural selection, and where evolution is still visibly operating on a human timescale.
The Galápagos finches, thirteen species descended from a single ancestor, each adapted to a different food source and a different island. The marine iguanas, the only sea-going lizards in the world. The giant tortoises, which can live for over 170 years. The blue-footed boobies, whose foot color is the determining factor in mate selection. The animals of the Galápagos have no learned fear of humans and will approach and observe you with as much curiosity as you bring to them.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - A blue-footed booby in its mating display, the improbable turquoise of the feet foregrounded, the volcanic island and the Pacific behind it. 🖼️ Poster - A natural history expedition poster in the style of 19th-century scientific illustration: the Galápagos finch species arranged by beak shape, Darwin's tree of life in the background, meticulous and revelatory. 🎨 Artwork - A figurative painting of a giant tortoise in the highland mist of Santa Cruz Island, ancient and unhurried, the mist softening everything except the animal itself.

### 10. The Sahara Desert - North Africa
The largest hot desert on Earth: 9.2 million square kilometres, larger than the continental United States.
The Sahara is not, as commonly imagined, mostly sand dunes. Only about 25 percent is erg, the great sand seas of rolling dunes. The majority is reg, gravel plains, and hammada, bare rock plateaus. The Erg Chebbi dunes in Morocco, the Erg of Bilma in Niger, the Ubari Sand Sea in Libya: these are the Sahara of the imagination, and they are as extraordinary as imagined. The silence of the deep Sahara at night, with no wind and no human noise, is one of the most complete silences on Earth.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Erg Chebbi dunes at dawn, the shadows still in the valleys between the dunes and the crests catching the first warm light, a landscape of pure form. 🖼️ Poster - A graphic desert poster: the dune crests as simple bold curves against a gradient sky from deep blue to burnt orange, minimal and elemental. 🎨 Artwork - An abstract sand study: the dune's curves and shadows rendered as a composition of warm ochre, deep amber, and cool blue shadow, the desert as pure light and form.

### 11. The Himalayas - Asia
The youngest major mountain range on Earth, still rising by approximately five millimetres per year as the Indian subcontinent continues its collision with the Eurasian plate.
The Himalayas contain all fourteen of the world's mountains above 8,000 metres, including Everest, K2, Kangchenjunga, and Lhotse. The range stretches 2,400 kilometres across six countries and is the source of ten of the world's major river systems. The view from a high pass, with the peaks arranged across the horizon in every direction, is a scale of landscape that the human visual system was not evolved to process.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Himalayan panorama from the Thorong La pass in Nepal, the peaks of the Annapurna range visible on three sides, the altitude and the scale in the same frame. 🖼️ Poster - A Himalayan mountaineering poster in the tradition of the great British expedition graphics: the peaks in profile against a starfield, the names of the summits lettered in the style of vintage expedition cartography. 🎨 Artwork - A Japanese ink wash-influenced Himalayan painting: the peaks rendered in spare, precise brushstrokes against an empty ground, the mountains as pure form rather than physical fact.

### 12. The Great Blue Hole - Belize
A marine sinkhole 300 metres across and 125 metres deep in the middle of the Belize Barrier Reef.
Formed during the last ice age when sea levels were much lower and the sinkhole was an above-ground cave system, it flooded as the glaciers melted. Jacques Cousteau declared it one of the top ten diving sites in the world in 1971. Seen from above, it is a perfect circle of deep indigo blue in the middle of the turquoise shallows of the reef, the color difference between shallow and deep water so extreme that it looks artificial. Stalactites from its cave formation still hang intact in its lower chambers.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - An aerial view of the Great Blue Hole, the perfect circle of deep indigo surrounded by the turquoise reef, one of the most graphically striking natural formations on Earth. 🖼️ Poster - A deep-sea exploration poster: the hole's circular form from above, rendered in concentric rings of color from turquoise at the rim to midnight blue at the center, graphic and hypnotic. 🎨 Artwork - A pure color-field painting: concentric circles of blue from pale turquoise to near-black, the geometry of the sinkhole as abstract art.

### 13. Mount Fuji - Japan
Japan's highest mountain at 3,776 metres, a nearly perfect stratovolcano that has been the subject of Japanese art, poetry, and religion for over a thousand years.
Hokusai's Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji made it one of the most reproduced natural images in history, and the mountain has been earning that representation ever since. Its symmetry is not quite perfect, which is why it is beautiful rather than simply correct. Fuji is an active volcano, though it has not erupted since 1707. Climbing it is a right of passage for millions of Japanese and the mountain's silhouette, visible from Tokyo on clear days 100 kilometres to the east, is a daily reminder of the natural world that the city exists inside.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - Mount Fuji reflected in Lake Kawaguchiko at dawn, the perfect cone above its own reflection, cherry blossoms on the near shore, the quintessential Japanese landscape. 🖼️ Poster - A direct Hokusai homage or a contemporary woodblock-style Fuji poster: the mountain in the visual language of ukiyo-e, the great wave or a winter snow or a clear summer day, the most illustrated mountain in the world in its natural medium. 🎨 Artwork - A minimalist contemporary painting of the mountain: the cone in pale grey above a pale blue sky above a pale reflection, the entire composition in near-whites and cool greys, the mountain barely there and completely present.

### 14. The Monarch Butterfly Migration - Mexico
Every autumn, approximately 100 million monarch butterflies travel up to 4,500 kilometres from their summer grounds in Canada and the northern United States to their winter roosts in the oyamel fir forests of central Mexico.
No individual butterfly makes the round trip: the return journey takes several generations. The butterflies navigate using a time-compensated sun compass combined with the Earth's magnetic field, and they return to the same forests, often the same trees, as their great-grandparents, without ever having been there before. In the roosting forests of Michoacán, the trees are orange with butterflies, millions of them clustered on every branch, and when the morning sun warms them enough to fly, the forest fills with wings.

### 15. The Bioluminescent Bays - Puerto Rico/Maldives
In certain bays around the world, the water glows blue at night.
The phenomenon is caused by dinoflagellates, single-celled organisms that emit a flash of blue light when disturbed. In Mosquito Bay in Vieques, Puerto Rico, the dinoflagellate concentration is so high that anything moving through the water leaves a glowing trail: a hand dragged through it streams blue light, a fish below the surface is visible as a moving glow, a wave breaking on the shore turns luminous. Kayaking through bioluminescent water in complete darkness is one of the most completely singular experiences the natural world offers.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - A kayak wake in Mosquito Bay at night, the bioluminescent trail glowing electric blue against the black water, the stars above doubling the light. 🖼️ Poster - A night-sea poster: the bioluminescent wave in bold blue against a deep black sea, a crescent moon above, the visual of natural light at its most magical. 🎨 Artwork - An abstract deep-blue painting: the bioluminescent flashes rendered as points and streaks of electric blue on a near-black ground, the ocean at night as a field of living light.

### 16. The Redwood Forests - California, USA
The tallest living things on Earth.
Coast redwoods can reach 115 metres in height and live for over 2,000 years. The largest individual trees contain more wood than any other organism that has ever lived. Walking through a mature redwood forest is a spatial experience unlike any other in nature: the trees are so tall that the canopy is invisible, lost in the mist above, and the scale of the trunks makes a human being feel approximately the size of a moth. The light in the forest, diffused through the fog and the high canopy, has the specific quality of light in a cathedral.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - Looking up from the forest floor at the redwood canopy, the trunks converging toward a distant green ceiling, the light filtering down in columns, the scale communicated in the vanishing point. 🖼️ Poster - A California state parks poster: a single redwood trunk filling the frame from base to crown, the human figure at its base for scale, deep forest greens and the warm rust-red of the bark. 🎨 Artwork - A figurative forest interior painting: the redwood trunks as vertical structures in deep red-brown, the forest floor in soft greens, the light from above golden and diffuse, the cathedral quality made visible.

### 17. The Saharan Silver Ant
Cataglyphis bombycina, the Saharan silver ant, is the fastest ant in the world relative to body size and one of the most remarkable animals in the desert.
It ventures onto the Saharan sand surface, where temperatures can reach 70 degrees Celsius, to scavenge the bodies of insects that have died in the heat. It can only operate for windows of around ten minutes before its own body temperature becomes lethal. It navigates back to its nest by counting its own steps and tracking the angle of the sun, a feat of biomechanical precision that has inspired robotics research. Its silver color is produced by hairs that reflect sunlight and dissipate heat through a process that materials scientists are still studying.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - A silver ant on the sand surface, its metallic sheen catching the desert light, a single extraordinary creature in a vast, inhospitable landscape. 🖼️ Poster - A natural history insect poster in the style of Victorian scientific illustration: the silver ant rendered in precise detail against a sand-colored background, its silver hairs visible, the surrounding desert implied in the color. 🎨 Artwork - A close-up abstract of the ant's silver surface texture, the metallic hairs rendered as a shimmering field of reflected light, the micro made monumental.

### 18. Patagonia - Argentina/Chile
The southernmost region of South America, where the Andes meet the sea at the continent's tip and the landscapes take on a scale and a ferocity that feels like the edge of the world because it is close to being exactly that.
Torres del Paine, the three granite towers that rise 2,500 metres from the Patagonian steppe, are among the most dramatic mountain formations on Earth. The Perito Moreno Glacier advances three metres per day and calves icebergs the size of buildings into the turquoise Lake Argentino below. The wind in Patagonia is a physical force: gusts regularly exceed 150 kilometres per hour and have been known to knock trekkers off their feet.

### 19. The Humpback Whale
The humpback whale, up to 16 metres long and 30 tonnes, is capable of producing the most complex songs of any non-human animal on Earth.
Male humpbacks sing continuously for hours or days at a time during breeding season, producing sequences that last up to 20 minutes and then repeat. The songs evolve over time as individual whales adopt innovations from others, meaning the song heard in the Pacific this year is different from the song heard ten years ago. They breach with a physical energy that seems impossible for something their size, launching their entire body clear of the water's surface. Watching a humpback breach is one of those experiences that resets your understanding of what biological systems can do.

### 20. The Northern Boreal Forest (Taiga) - Russia/Canada
The largest terrestrial biome on Earth, the boreal forest covers approximately 17 million square kilometres across Russia, Canada, Alaska, and Scandinavia.
It contains more than a third of the world's forest cover and more fresh water than anywhere else on land. The Russian taiga alone is twice the size of the Amazon. In winter, it is silent in a way that has no analogy in any other landscape: the snow absorbs sound completely, the cold stills everything, and the forest, which is vast beyond comprehension, makes no sound at all. The light in winter, at 60 degrees north, barely clears the horizon at noon and turns everything gold for an hour before disappearing again.

### 21. The Nile River - Africa
The longest river in the world, stretching 6,650 kilometres from its source in the Rwenzori Mountains to the Mediterranean.
The Nile has been the organizing principle of human civilization in northeast Africa for 5,000 years. The annual flood that deposited rich silt across the Egyptian floodplain was the foundation of the ancient Egyptian civilization, a reliable gift from the highlands of Ethiopia that made agriculture possible in a desert. The river passes through eleven countries, supports 300 million people, and sustains 70 percent of Egypt's population in the narrow green corridor along its banks. From space, the Nile's green thread through the Eastern Sahara is the most vivid illustration of what a river means to a desert.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Nile from altitude, the green agricultural corridor perfectly defined against the ochre desert on both sides, the geometry of a civilization that exists only because of one river. 🖼️ Poster - An Egyptian travel poster in the Art Deco tradition: felucca sailboats on the Nile at sunset, the palm trees and temple columns behind them, the classic imagery of the river at its most romantic. 🎨 Artwork - A landscape painting of the Nile at dawn near Luxor, the river catching the first light, the temple columns on the bank, the desert pink and golden behind them, everything still before the heat arrives.

### 22. The Snow Leopard - Central Asia
One of the most rarely seen large mammals on Earth, the snow leopard inhabits the high mountain ranges of Central and South Asia at altitudes between 3,000 and 5,500 metres.
It is so rarely observed that biologists spent decades working in its range without a confirmed sighting, gathering only tracks and occasional livestock kills as evidence. Its tail is nearly as long as its body, used for balance on rocky terrain. Its paws are proportionally the largest of any big cat, acting as snowshoes. Its eyes, pale green or grey, are adapted to the low light of the mountain dawn. Wildlife photographers have waited weeks at known territories and left without seeing one. It is a ghost that leaves prints.

### 23. The Dead Sea - Jordan/Israel
The lowest point on Earth's land surface, 430 metres below sea level, and the saltiest large body of water in the world, at nearly ten times the salinity of the ocean.
Nothing lives in the Dead Sea. The salt concentration prevents any form of animal life, and the few microorganisms that survive there represent some of the most extreme life known on Earth. Floating in it is an experience of complete counterintuition: you cannot sink, regardless of what your body does. You simply sit in the water at a 45-degree angle, unable to submerge, reading a newspaper if you like. The mineral deposits on the shoreline, white and crystalline, look like snow in the middle of the desert.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Dead Sea shoreline, white salt crystals forming abstract sculptures at the water's edge, the Jordanian mountains mauve in the haze across the water. 🖼️ Poster - A mineral poster: the Dead Sea salt formations and the surreal blue of the water, rendered in clean graphic form, the visual of the impossible made factual. 🎨 Artwork - A salt crystal abstract: the geometric formations of Dead Sea salt translated into a crystalline painting, white and pale blue and the pale gold of desert light, the chemistry of extreme salinity made beautiful.

### 24. The Wolf Pack - Yellowstone
When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995 after a 70-year absence, they triggered what ecologists call a trophic cascade.
The wolves changed the behavior of the deer, who avoided grazing in valleys where they could be cornered. The valleys recovered. The rivers changed course, their banks stabilized by new vegetation. Songbird populations increased. Beaver populations returned. The wolves, by being themselves, restructured the entire ecosystem from the top down. This is now one of the most studied examples of how predators create the conditions for life rather than simply diminishing it, a complete reversal of the previous management assumption.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - A Yellowstone wolf pack moving through the Lamar Valley in winter, the animals dark against the snow, the valley's recovered willows and the mountains behind them. 🖼️ Poster - A Yellowstone wildlife poster: the pack in silhouette against a winter moon, the mountain and the geyser steam in the background, the visual of restored wilderness. 🎨 Artwork - A figurative winter landscape: the wolf pack crossing the snow-covered valley floor, the animals painted in dark greys against the white, the mountain ridgeline above, the ecosystem's wholeness implied in the scene.

### 25. The Matterhorn - Switzerland/Italy
The most immediately recognizable mountain in the world, its near-perfect pyramidal form rising 4,478 metres above the Swiss-Italian border.
The Matterhorn is geologically distinctive: its peak is composed of African rock, material from the Apulian Plate that was pushed northward and upward as Africa collided with Europe, meaning the summit of this most Swiss of mountains is, technically, African. The north face, sheer and consistently iced, was one of the last great Alpine challenges to be climbed and remains one of the most demanding. Zermatt below it has been photographed from its surrounding hills so many times that there are specific spots worn into the grass by generations of photographers standing in the same place.
**Deckoviz inspo:** 📷 Photo - The Matterhorn reflected in the Riffelsee at dawn, the near-perfect pyramidal form above its own reflection, alpenglow on the north face, the most composed natural image in the Alps. 🖼️ Poster - A classic Swiss tourism poster: the Matterhorn in the bold flat color tradition of Swiss graphic design, the mountain in blue-grey above Zermatt's rooftops, clean and iconic. 🎨 Artwork - A Cézanne-influenced painting of the mountain in different lights: the form studied from multiple angles in the same composition, the geometry of the peak as an obsessive subject, the mountain as a problem of painting.

### 26. The Great Migration of Christmas Island Crabs
Every year, the 45 million red crabs of Christmas Island migrate from the forest to the coast to breed, covering the island's roads, paths, and buildings in a moving red carpet.
The Australian government closes roads for them. Special crab bridges and underpasses have been built. They flow down cliffs and across beaches in masses so dense that the ground appears to move. ### 32. The Platypus

### 40. The Peacock

## Closing thoughts
Nature didn't build for us, and it doesn't care whether we're watching.
That's the first thing. The aurora wasn't made to be beautiful. The peacock's tail didn't evolve to move us. The mantis shrimp doesn't see sixteen color channels for our benefit. The morning light rakes across the desert whether anyone is there to see it or not.

And yet we have been watching all of it for as long as we have been capable of watching. The oldest cave paintings are pictures of animals. The oldest poetry is about the sea and the stars. The sense that the natural world is worth attending to, worth representing, worth bringing inside in some form, is as old as the human impulse to make anything at all.

Deckoviz is a way of bringing that attention home.

A photograph of the Serengeti migration on your wall is a daily reminder that 1.5 million animals are out there right now, moving in a circuit they have been making for a million years, indifferent to your awareness of them. A painting of the aurora in your living room is the light that fell over a frozen lake in Finland last winter, translated into art and placed somewhere you'll look at it while you drink your morning coffee.
