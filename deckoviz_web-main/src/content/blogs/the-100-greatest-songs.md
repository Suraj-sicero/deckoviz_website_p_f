# The 100 Greatest Songs
## A DASPort Library

<div className="author-pub-info opacity-70">
  <span>By Deckoviz Editorial</span> | <span>30 min read</span>
</div>

## Introduction
A song is the smallest complete world in art. Three minutes, sometimes less, and a great one manages to do what novels spend hundreds of pages attempting: put you somewhere specific, make you feel something precise, and leave you changed in a way you can't quite account for afterward. The best songs feel inevitable, as if they always existed and someone simply found them, which is of course the exact opposite of how they were made.

This list cuts across a century of recorded music and reaches back before that. It doesn't pretend to be definitive, no list of a hundred songs can be, but every entry here changed something: a genre, a generation, a conversation about what music could do or say or mean. Some of them are obvious. Some will surprise you. All of them have a visual world worth living in.

That's the Deckoviz angle on this list. A great song is almost always a great image too: the neon of a particular city, the texture of a particular heartbreak, a specific quality of light at a specific time of day. Vizzy can translate that into ambient art that plays on your wall the way a song plays in your head, not loudly, but persistently, reshaping the mood of whatever room it's in.

One entry per artist to keep the range wide. Here are a hundred songs worth listening to and looking at.

## The Great Library

### 1. Strange Fruit — Billie Holiday (1939)
Originally a poem by a Jewish schoolteacher named Abel Meeropol, set to music and recorded by Holiday for a small New York label after Columbia refused to touch it. The song's depiction of lynching, rendered through the image of Black bodies hanging from Southern trees like strange and bitter fruit, was the most confrontational piece of protest music American audiences had encountered. Holiday reportedly ended every set she performed it with no encore and the lights staying dark afterward. It changed what a song was allowed to say.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Southern magnolia tree against a dusk sky, heavy with foliage, the branches rendered in dense shadow, the beauty of the image and what it implies held in deliberate, uncomfortable tension.

### 2. What's Going On — Marvin Gaye (1971)
Motown chief Berry Gordy initially refused to release this, believing its anti-war, pro-ecology, social justice themes were uncommercial. Gaye threatened to never record again. It became the best-selling Motown single to that point and the title track of what many consider the greatest soul album ever made. Gaye had been radicalized by his brother's return from Vietnam, and the song's layered, conversational arrangement, voices overlapping like a crowded room, was as revolutionary as its content.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Detroit street in the early 1970s, families on stoops and children playing, the ordinary texture of a community asking questions of itself, warm and documentary in tone.

### 3. Bohemian Rhapsody — Queen (1975)
Six minutes long at a time when radio programmers refused to play anything over three, recorded across three studios simultaneously to build its layered operatic sections, and impossible to categorize even now. Freddie Mercury reportedly told his bandmates "I've written a song, darlings, and you're going to love it" and largely refused to explain what it meant. Radio DJ Kenny Everett played it in defiance of his station's guidelines and the phones didn't stop ringing. The song essentially rewrote what a rock single could be.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A theatrical stage at the moment before a performance, red curtain lit from below, an ornate empty spotlight, the visual anticipation of something that can't be categorized.

### 4. Respect — Aretha Franklin (1967)
Otis Redding wrote and originally recorded it as a man asking his woman to respect him when he comes home. Franklin flipped the gender, added the iconic R-E-S-P-E-C-T spelling and the "sock it to me" bridge, and transformed a decent soul record into an anthem of feminine and Black self-determination that landed at exactly the moment the civil rights and women's movements needed it. Redding reportedly said on hearing her version that she had stolen his song. He was right. She made it entirely her own.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A recording studio with the booth glass separating engineer and vocalist, a woman at the microphone in a moment of absolute command, warm studio amber lighting.

### 5. Smells Like Teen Spirit — Nirvana (1991)
Kurt Cobain wrote the central riff in ten minutes and considered it a fairly obvious Pixies pastiche. The song's success bewildered and eventually horrified him. It arrived at a cultural moment when mainstream rock had become glossy and performatively masculine, and its combination of quiet verses and explosive choruses, its deliberately slurred and inarticulate lyrics about a generation that didn't know what it wanted to say, articulated something a generation hadn't known it was feeling. Grunge didn't exist as a mainstream category before this song. After it, nothing else on radio sounded right.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A high school gymnasium strewn with confetti and streamers after a dance, the lights half-down, the sense of something that should feel celebratory but doesn't quite, pale and slightly wrecked.

### 6. Like a Rolling Stone — Bob Dylan (1965)
Originally over twenty minutes long, eventually cut to six, which was still double the length of anything on pop radio. Dylan had been the voice of the folk protest movement and this was his declaration of war on everything comfortable and familiar, a scorching, relentless address to someone whose privilege had collapsed, delivered with contempt and something that might have been sympathy. Rolling Stone magazine later called it the greatest rock song ever made. It redefined what a lyric could do by treating song as literature without apology.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A wide empty American highway stretching toward a horizon with no destination marked, flat light and long shadows, the visual sensation of having nowhere particular to go.

### 7. God Save the Queen — Sex Pistols (1977)
Released during Queen Elizabeth's Silver Jubilee weekend on a label that was formed specifically to release it after every major label refused, played on a boat on the Thames outside the official celebrations until police boarded and arrested the band's manager. It reached number two on the UK charts despite being banned by the BBC and most major retailers. Whether it was truly number one and was suppressed is debated still. Its image of the Queen with a safety pin through her lip became the defining visual of punk.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A London street on Jubilee weekend, Union flags decorating houses, but the colors slightly wrong or the image slightly off, the celebration and its disruption occupying the same frame.

### 8. Superstition — Stevie Wonder (1972)
Written by Wonder in a matter of hours after sitting down at the drum kit in Jeff Beck's studio, given to Beck as a gesture of collaboration, then reclaimed when Wonder's Motown deal came up for renegotiation and he retained the right to record it himself. The clavinet riff that opens it is one of the most recognizable in popular music. Wonder was 22 when he wrote it, at the beginning of a five-album run that represents one of the most concentrated bursts of creative genius in popular music history.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dimly lit apartment in Detroit, candles burning and a rabbit's foot on the table, the visual texture of superstition as both cultural practice and lived anxiety.

### 9. A Day in the Life — The Beatles (1967)
The closing track of Sgt. Pepper, assembled from two unfinished Lennon and McCartney songs joined by a planned orchestral crescendo that producer George Martin described as a "Niagara of sound," the full orchestra instructed to climb from their lowest to highest note in 24 bars. It ends on a single E major chord played simultaneously on three pianos, left to decay for 45 seconds. The BBC banned it for drug references. It is widely considered the greatest song the Beatles ever recorded, which is a sentence that carries considerable weight.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A London morning newspaper spread on a kitchen table, coffee cup nearby, a window showing grey city light, the ordinary domestic detail through which extraordinary events arrive.

### 10. Johnny B. Goode — Chuck Berry (1958)
The foundational text of rock and roll guitar, built on a blues shuffle that Berry sharpened into something new: fast, precise, and unmistakably electric. The song's narrative of a country boy making good on his guitar laid down the mythology that rock and roll would spend the next several decades retelling. Carl Sagan included it on the Voyager Golden Record as one of the representatives of human music sent into interstellar space. It is currently somewhere beyond the solar system.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A roadside juke joint in Louisiana at night, neon sign reflecting in rain puddles on the dirt road, warm light from inside against the dark country beyond.

### 11. Yesterday — The Beatles (1965)
Paul McCartney woke up with the melody fully formed, was convinced he'd unconsciously reproduced something he'd already heard, and spent weeks asking musicians if they recognized it before concluding it was his. Originally recorded with McCartney alone and a string quartet, over no other Beatle's contribution, it became the most covered song in history by most counts. That a song this formally simple, a single voice and a few strings, became the most reproduced piece of popular music ever is itself a kind of argument.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An empty room in warm morning light, a guitar leaning against the wall, the suggestion of someone who just left, simple and slightly melancholic.

### 12. Waterloo Sunset — The Kinks (1967)
Ray Davies wrote it about the bridge over the Thames near where he grew up, watching couples meet at the end of the day, happy to observe from the outside. It's a song about contentment with a particular place and a particular view, which was an unusual subject for pop music in 1967. Brian Wilson called it his favorite song. Its combination of specific London geography with a universal feeling of quiet happiness makes it one of the great place songs in pop.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Waterloo Bridge at sunset, the Thames below turning orange and gold, figures crossing without hurrying, London rendered in warm dusk tones, unhurried and peaceful.

### 13. Purple Rain — Prince (1984)
The title track of the film and album that announced Prince to a mainstream audience that had already been warned about him and wasn't ready. Eight minutes long, built on a guitar solo that Prince later said he only half-planned, and capable of filling an arena with something that felt like collective religious experience. The song earned Prince an Academy Award for Best Original Song Score, which is not the category you'd invent for it but captures something about its scale.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A concert stage in purple-tinted rain, a single spotlight on a solitary figure, the crowd beyond the light in darkness, theatrical and slightly sacred.

### 14. I Will Always Love You — Dolly Parton (1974)
Written by Parton as a goodbye to her mentor Porter Wagoner when she left his show to pursue a solo career, a real farewell to a real person that somehow became one of the most universal expressions of loving departure ever recorded. Elvis Presley wanted to record it; Parton's manager Colonel Tom Parker told her Elvis always took half the publishing rights, and Parton walked away. Whitney Houston's 1992 version is what most people know, but Parton's original, delicate and specific, is the greater achievement.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Tennessee mountain road in early morning, two figures parting at a crossroads, the landscape wide and forgiving, warm and slightly heartbroken.

### 15. (I Can't Get No) Satisfaction — The Rolling Stones (1965)
Keith Richards recorded the opening riff on a cassette at 2am, then went back to sleep, and was unaware he'd done it until the tape ran out. The band initially planned it as a placeholder, a demo idea to come back to, and only decided to release it after an American concert audience went wild for a rough version. The fuzz guitar riff played through a fuzz box was designed to approximate a horn section until a proper brass arrangement could be recorded. It never was. The song's dissatisfaction with commercialism became, inescapably, one of the most commercially successful records of its era.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A motel room somewhere in the American Midwest in 1965, a television on in the corner, the curtains not quite closed on a parking lot, restless and slightly sour.

### 16. God Only Knows — The Beach Boys (1966)
Brian Wilson and Tony Asher wrote it in an afternoon, Wilson later said he was disappointed with it. Carl Wilson, who sings the lead, nearly had to be persuaded to perform a song that opened with the conditional "I may not always love you." Paul McCartney called it the greatest song ever written. It was one of the first pop songs to use the word "God" in its title, which concerned the label. Its orchestration, shifting time signatures and unconventional structure, represents the summit of Wilson's baroque pop ambitions.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A California beach at late afternoon in 1966, golden light turning the sand orange, a small group of people gathered loosely, the quality of light that only exists for about twenty minutes and then is gone.

### 17. Redemption Song — Bob Marley (1980)
Recorded with just Marley and an acoustic guitar, unusual for a reggae track and apparently not the plan initially, from his final studio album, released months before his death from cancer at 36. The lyrics drew on Marcus Garvey's speeches, and the central image of emancipating yourself from mental slavery, the chains being in the mind, has given the song a life beyond any particular political moment. It is sung at funerals and revolutions and graduation ceremonies with equal appropriateness.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A single acoustic guitar on a hillside in Jamaica, the sea visible in the distance, warm afternoon light, uncomplicated and indelible.

### 18. Heartbreak Hotel — Elvis Presley (1956)
Elvis's first number one on the national charts, based on a newspaper report about a suicide note that read "I walk a lonely street." The production was so spare and the atmosphere so bleak that RCA initially doubted it would sell. It stays at number one for seven weeks. Elvis's vocal on it is unlike anything before it in popular music: intimate, slightly threatening, sliding between registers in a way that had never been on the radio before. The world that popular music inhabited before this record and after it are genuinely different places.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dimly lit hotel corridor at midnight, a single lamp at the far end, worn carpet and empty coat hangers, the specific loneliness of a place that's meant to be temporary.

### 19. Imagine — John Lennon (1971)
Lennon called it "Working Class Hero with chocolate coating," by which he meant it packaged radical ideas in a form palatable enough for mainstream consumption. He was being self-critical. The song's vision of a world without nations, religion, or private property, sung over a gentle piano from a white room in his mansion, became the most famous peace anthem of the 20th century, which does not entirely escape the irony he was pointing at.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A white room entirely empty except for a grand piano, natural light through tall windows, the deliberate blankness of a space that contains only possibility.

### 20. Strange — Celeste (2019)
A British singer of Jamaican heritage who grew up in Brighton, her voice sits somewhere between jazz, soul, and classic torch singing, and this song about the particular sadness of losing touch with someone without anything dramatic happening gave her a following years before her mainstream breakthrough. Its restraint is its power: nothing swells unnecessarily, nothing resolves falsely.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A late-night café, two cups of coffee on a table, one person sitting alone, the city dark outside the window, quiet and slightly unresolved.

### 21. Halo — Beyoncé (2008)
Written in twenty minutes by Ryan Tedder after Beyoncé played him a track she liked and asked for something in that spirit, this is a power ballad that earns every one of its soaring moments by the time it reaches them. Beyoncé's vocal control is the argument the song is making: she holds back for three minutes before the full range arrives, and when it does, the release is architectural.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A rooftop at dawn, the city below still mostly dark, warm light arriving at the edges of the sky, the visual of a private revelation in a sleeping world.

### 22. Lose Yourself — Eminem (2002)
Written for the film 8 Mile during breaks in filming, partly on a napkin, this is Eminem's most compact and disciplined performance, three verses that build a self-contained narrative of risk and consequence without losing momentum for a single line. It won the Academy Award for Best Original Song, the first rap track to do so. That the verses are as precisely engineered as the beat they ride over was not, at the time, something the Academy typically recognized.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Detroit parking lot at dawn, a single figure preparing alone before a competition, the visual stillness before a decisive moment.

### 23. No Woman, No Cry — Bob Marley (1974)
Credited to Vincent "Tata" Ford, a friend who ran a soup kitchen in Trenchtown, as a gesture of financial support that would provide Ford with royalties for his community work. The live version recorded at the Lyceum in London in 1975 is the definitive recording, Marley's vocal riding the rhythm section with a looseness the studio version doesn't quite match. It is a song of comfort about poverty, written from inside it, and its warmth is more remarkable for that.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Trenchtown yard in Kingston at evening, a fire going, people gathered, the warm light of community in a place that doesn't have much else.

### 24. Someone Like You — Adele (2011)
Written at a piano the morning after a conversation with an ex-partner who had moved on and got engaged, the rawness of the production, just voice and piano, was a deliberate choice against the more produced tracks on 21. The live performance at the BRIT Awards, which made an entire arena cry in real time, introduced the song to a mainstream that had already made the album a record-breaker. Its emotional directness is its entire technique.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A piano in a bare room at morning, curtains open on grey London sky, the specific atmosphere of processing something difficult through the act of playing.

### 25. Born to Run — Bruce Springsteen (1975)
Took six months to record, cost more than the entire first two Springsteen albums combined, and nearly bankrupted Columbia Records' patience in the process. Springsteen wanted it to sound like Roy Orbison singing Phil Spector with a street gang, and the result is a four-and-a-half minute wall of sound about escape and whether escape actually gets you anywhere. Time magazine and Newsweek ran cover stories on Springsteen the same week it was released, which had never happened before to a rock musician.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A highway on-ramp at night, headlights blurring past, New Jersey industrial horizon glowing in the background, the visual of getting out and not being sure where out leads.

### 26. Blue in Green — Miles Davis (1959)
A short, slow modal piece from Kind of Blue, widely considered the best-selling jazz album ever recorded, made in two sessions with almost no rehearsal, the musicians handed scales rather than chord changes and told to improvise. Whether Davis or Bill Evans wrote this particular track has been debated for decades without resolution. At barely five minutes it is one of the most concentrated expressions of melancholy in recorded music.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A jazz club in 1959, near-empty in the small hours, a single trumpet player finishing a set for five remaining people, blue-green light through cigarette smoke.

### 27. Take Five — Dave Brubeck (1959)
Written in 5/4 time, a time signature that common wisdom said couldn't support a groove people would actually dance or tap their feet to. Paul Desmond's saxophone melody proved otherwise and it became the best-selling jazz single of all time. Brubeck and Desmond reportedly argued for years over who wrote it. Its sophistication is invisible: it swings despite every formal reason it shouldn't.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A mid-century modern living room, a record player in the corner, geometric furniture and a clean aesthetic, the late 1950s rendered as a style rather than a decade.

### 28. Jolene — Dolly Parton (1973)
Parton says she wrote it after a red-haired bank teller kept flirting with her husband, combining that experience with the name of a young fan she'd met on the road. The song's unusual quality is that the narrator never expresses anger at Jolene, only admiration for her beauty and a helpless, honest plea. The power dynamic in it is entirely inverted from the expected: the wife is supplicating, not accusing. That vulnerability is what makes it last.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small-town bank in 1973, afternoon light through venetian blinds casting stripe shadows, a moment charged with unspoken meaning between strangers.

### 29. What a Wonderful World — Louis Armstrong (1967)
Written by Bob Thiele and George David Weiss for Armstrong when he was in his mid-sixties, partly as a gesture of warmth toward an artist they admired and partly as a counterweight to the turbulence of the era. ABC Records chief Larry Newton reportedly hated it and refused to promote it, which is why it was a minor hit in America but a number one in the UK, where it found its audience first. Its sentimentality is total and undefended and this is, in the end, the only way a song this simple could work.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A city park in spring, trees in bloom, children and old people sharing the same space under a blue sky, the world rendered at its most generous and uncomplicated.

### 30. Nights in White Satin — The Moody Blues (1967)
Written by 19-year-old Justin Hayward in an hour on the white satin sheets of his new bed, which he'd bought to mark leaving home for the first time. The orchestral arrangement was added later as part of the Days of Future Passed album, which positioned the band's rock sound against a full symphony orchestra. The song was initially dismissed as overwrought and only became a classic after it was re-released years later, by which point its emotional grandeur had found the audience that suited it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A bedroom window at night, white curtains moving in a light breeze, moonlight on the floor, the visual of being nineteen and feeling things for what seems like the first time.

### 31. Ring of Fire — Johnny Cash (1963)
Written by June Carter Cash and Merle Kilgore about June's falling in love with Johnny Cash while he was still married to someone else, the fire being simultaneously desire and the hellish consequences of desire. The mariachi horns were Cash's idea, arriving to him in a dream, which the band and producer initially resisted. The song became his signature alongside "I Walk the Line," a love song from someone who knew exactly what loving the wrong person cost.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A desert at dusk, mountains on the horizon catching the last red light, a ring of fire burning in the foreground, elemental and slightly ominous.

### 32. God's Gonna Cut You Down — Johnny Cash (2006)
Recorded for American Recordings VI: American IV: The Man Comes Around as Cash was dying, the song is a traditional spiritual taken back to its bones and performed by a man for whom mortality was not metaphorical. Rick Rubin's production gives it space that amplifies rather than fills: just Cash's voice, a sparse arrangement, and the moral certainty of a man who had spent decades running from things and knew how those stories ended.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A lone figure walking down a long dirt road toward a distant crossroads, the sky vast and darkening, black and white tones, the visual of reckoning as geography.

### 33. Hallelujah — Leonard Cohen (1984)
Cohen spent years writing and rewriting this, reportedly producing over 80 verses and performing different combinations at different concerts. The song was largely ignored on its original release, only becoming famous through John Cale's 1991 cover and then Jeff Buckley's 1994 recording, which became the definitive version for most listeners. Cohen reportedly once said his version was a reflection on religion and sex, and Buckley's was about heartbreak, and both were correct.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A cold, empty church in late afternoon, winter light through stained glass falling on bare stone floors, the architecture of a spiritual life without its congregation.

### 34. Grace — Jeff Buckley (1994)
The title track of Buckley's only studio album, its guitar tuned down and its central image, going to meet death as if it were a lover, delivered with a vocal range and emotional commitment that defines the record. Buckley died in 1997, swimming in the Mississippi River at 30, and the album has grown in reputation every year since, partly through the tragedy and partly because no one who hears it forgets it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A wide river at dusk, still water reflecting the last light, a figure visible on the far bank, the visual of crossing over rendered with beauty rather than dread.

### 35. Fast Car — Tracy Chapman (1988)
Written in Chapman's college years and largely unchanged when it was recorded, the song tells a story of escape from poverty and a difficult family situation that turns into a different kind of trap. Its specificity, the job at the convenience store, the checkout girl dreams, the man who works too much and drinks, is what makes it universal. Chapman performed it at Nelson Mandela's 70th Birthday Tribute concert to 600 million television viewers and the song went to number one the following week.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A convenience store at night, fluorescent light and near-empty shelves, a car in the parking lot with the engine running, the visual of wanting out and not quite leaving.

### 36. Killing Me Softly — Roberta Flack (1973)
The song was written by Lori Lieberman and Charles Fox after Lieberman heard Don McLean perform "Empty Tables" live and was moved by the sensation that he was somehow singing about her own experience. Flack heard Lieberman's recording on a flight and asked to record her own version. Her arrangement, slower and more intimate, became definitive. Lauryn Hill's 1996 Fugees version introduced it to a new generation with an entirely different feel but the same emotional core.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A concert venue mid-performance, the audience still, a single performer on stage lit by a spotlight that makes the audience invisible, the intimacy of public vulnerability.

### 37. A Whiter Shade of Pale — Procol Harum (1967)
Its Bach-derived keyboard phrase is one of the most recognized in pop history, and the lyrics, which include references to Miller's tale, vestal virgins, and the ocean road, have been argued over since 1967 without resolution. Lyricist Keith Reid has said there's no single meaning. The song spent six weeks at number one in the UK in the summer of 1967 and remains the most played track in UK public performance history.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A formal dance at the end of the evening, the room half-emptied, couples still moving slowly to something they can barely hear, candlelight and the visual of a night that's extending beyond its natural end.

### 38. Gold Digger — Kanye West (2005)
Built on a Ray Charles sample (Genius from Georgia) with an interpolation that Charles reportedly loved before his death, this was the song that confirmed West's pop instincts were as sharp as his critical ones. The production is meticulous underneath what sounds casual, and Jamie Foxx's hook delivery manages to be simultaneously ironic and totally committed. It spent ten weeks at number one and announced that rap's most interesting producer had also become its most commercially dominant artist.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Chicago block in the mid-2000s, summer heat and street energy, warm asphalt tones and the visual texture of a music that emerged from and then transcended a particular place.

### 39. Clair de Lune — Claude Debussy (1905)
Not a song in the popular sense but a piano piece so widely beloved and so cinematically deployed that it belongs on any list of music that has shaped how people hear. Written in the impressionist style Debussy was developing, the piece evokes moonlight not by describing it but by creating a musical texture that produces the same quality of diffused, shifting light. It has been used in films, television, and advertising more than almost any other piece of classical music.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A garden at night under a full moon, the light filtering through trees, shadows and illuminated patches shifting as clouds move, the visual of light as fluid rather than fixed.

### 40. Blackbird — The Beatles (1968)
McCartney wrote it in response to the civil rights movement in America, the blackbird being a Black woman finding the freedom she'd been denied, though for years it was received simply as a gentle acoustic guitar piece about nature. Its fingerpicking pattern is one of the first things guitarists of a certain generation learn. The recording includes ambient birdsong and McCartney's foot tapping, elements that no one edited out and that give the track its particular warmth.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A garden at first light, a blackbird on a branch, dew on the grass, the moment just before the world fully wakes, rendered in soft greens and early morning grey.

### 41. Fake Plastic Trees — Radiohead (1995)
Recorded in a single take after Thom Yorke heard producer John Leckie's acoustic version and, according to the story, cried on the way back from the vocal booth. The song's subject is a woman whose life is constructed from surfaces and a man who can't love her for it, and the swelling strings arrive exactly when the emotional logic requires them. It announced that British rock had somewhere more interesting to go than Britpop.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A supermarket at closing time, fluorescent lights flickering slightly, a shopping cart full of plastic-wrapped food, the visual of abundance rendered as emptiness.

### 42. I Put a Spell on You — Nina Simone (1965)
Screamin' Jay Hawkins wrote and originally recorded it as a rave-up novelty in 1956. Simone turned it into something genuinely threatening, slowing the tempo and delivering the possessive, irrational love in the lyrics with a controlled ferocity that makes the original sound like a joke. Simone's version is one of the great examples of a cover that retroactively makes the original sound like a rough draft.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A late-night club stage, a single piano lit from above, the performer at the keys commanding a dark room, the visual of someone holding an audience in place through sheer force of presence.

### 43. Heroes — David Bowie (1977)
Recorded in a single day at Hansa Studios in Berlin, close enough to the Wall that Tony Visconti's vocal microphones picked up the wind at the border. Bowie saw two members of the production team kissing in the shadow of the Wall and built the song around them, the wall and the guns above it part of the visual from the start. The song's recording technique, Brian Eno's experimental Fripp guitar processed through three microphones at different distances, gives it a texture that simply didn't exist before it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Two figures standing at the Berlin Wall at dusk, the Wall stretching in both directions, the guards visible in watchtowers above, the visual of ordinary human courage inside a divided city.

### 44. Creep — Radiohead (1992)
Radiohead's first hit and a song they came to resent for defining them in their own minds as "the creep band." Thom Yorke has said the song's narrator is someone he finds more pathetic than sympathetic. The quiet-loud dynamic was partly influenced by The Pixies and partly by guitarist Jonny Greenwood deliberately playing aggressive chords to disrupt what he felt was an overly gentle song. Those crunching chords before each chorus are the sound of internal sabotage that made the song.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A crowded college party, one figure standing at the edge watching everyone else, the warm social glow making the margins colder by contrast.

### 45. Stairway to Heaven — Led Zeppelin (1971)
Never released as a single, never had a music video, was famously overplayed on rock radio to the point of satire, and is still the most requested song in the history of rock radio. Jimmy Page's ascending guitar line, Robert Plant's mythological lyric that was partly written in the room where it was recorded, and the song's escalation from folk ballad to hard rock within eight minutes did something no previous song had done in quite that way.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An ancient stone staircase disappearing into mist on a hillside, lantern light from below, the destination invisible, the architecture of an ascent that doesn't promise arrival.

### 46. Dreams — Fleetwood Mac (1975)
From Rumours, recorded while the band was falling apart in multiple directions simultaneously, this is Stevie Nicks's response to her breakup with Lindsey Buckingham, recorded in one sitting in Sly Stone's mobile studio. Buckingham recorded his own breakup response on the same album. They both had to perform both songs every night for years. The song's second life in 2020, driven by a TikTok video of a man skateboarding and drinking cranberry juice, introduced it to a generation who heard it fresh.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A recording studio where two people are working on opposite sides of a glass partition, making music about each other and not speaking, the visual architecture of creative intimacy and personal rupture.

### 47. Eleanor Rigby — The Beatles (1966)
A string octet, no other instruments, and two characters who never meet, an old woman who cleans up rice after weddings and a priest who practices his sermon in front of a mirror. McCartney has said he doesn't know where Eleanor Rigby's name came from and that the character assembled herself. George Martin's arrangement was based on Bernard Herrmann's Psycho score. The song's question about where lonely people belong had no answer then and has none now.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A church after a wedding, confetti and rice on stone steps, everyone gone, a woman bent over with a dustpan, the party's aftermath rendered as the subject rather than the afterthought.

### 48. Strange Fruit (revisited): Ma Rainey's Black Bottom — Ma Rainey (1927)
The song that gave August Wilson's play its title and captured the early blues idiom at its most raw and performatively confrontational. Ma Rainey, the "Mother of the Blues," recorded over 100 songs in the 1920s and her recordings were among the first to capture the Black Southern musical tradition for a wider audience. Her vocal authority and the unvarnished quality of her arrangements influenced every blues and gospel singer who came after her.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Chicago recording studio in the 1920s, a large woman in feathers and sequins commanding a room of male musicians, the visual of a creative authority that was not asked for but simply asserted.

### 49. Summertime — Billie Holiday (1936)
Originally written by George Gershwin for Porgy and Bess, this was one of Holiday's early recordings and already showed the quality that would define her: the ability to take a song written by someone else and make it sound like a personal memory. Holiday's timing was distinctive from the start, her tendency to sing slightly behind the beat creating a sensation of emotional weight that singers have tried to replicate for nearly a century.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Southern porch in summer evening, a woman in a rocking chair, fireflies beginning to appear in the long grass, warm and heavy with heat.

### 50. The Times They Are A-Changin' — Bob Dylan (1964)
Written as a deliberate anthem, Dylan said later, something that would communicate the spirit of a particular moment in as direct and universal language as possible. Its address to senators, congressmen, mothers and fathers, and critics makes it unusual among his songs for the specificity of its audience. It was already slightly prophetic when released and arrived two months before Kennedy's assassination, after which it became something else again.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A protest march on a Washington street, a sea of hand-lettered signs above a moving crowd, the visual of collective conviction in motion.

### 51. Hotel California — Eagles (1977)
Don Henley has described it as a song about a journey into the dark heart of the American dream, excess and hedonism and the trap of not being able to leave once you've arrived. The twin guitar solo by Don Felder and Joe Walsh that closes it was arranged by drawing two lines and having both players fill them in separately, the intertwining heard for the first time on the finished recording. The nameless hotel became one of rock's defining images.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A desert highway at dusk, a large Spanish colonial hotel at the end of it, warm amber windows against a darkening sky, the architecture of arrival that conceals departure's difficulty.

### 52. Thriller — Michael Jackson (1984)
A fourteen-minute short film attached to a seven-minute single, directed by John Landis with a horror movie budget unprecedented in music video history. Jackson reportedly had to get a religious exemption from the Jehovah's Witnesses to participate. MTV played it in rotation for months. It is credited with saving the music video format and, by some accounts, saving MTV itself. The choreography, the Vincent Price monologue, the zombie procession: its images became the visual grammar of an era.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A foggy graveyard at midnight, figures rising from the ground in a loose, unhurried procession, moonlight and mist, the visual of menace made strangely graceful.

### 53. Billie Jean — Michael Jackson (1983)
Jackson reportedly played the demo seventy-eight times on a drive from Los Angeles to Las Vegas. Quincy Jones initially wanted to cut it from Thriller. The bassline, Jones later admitted, was the finest he'd ever heard. The song's paranoid narrative of false paternity accusations drew on Jackson's own experiences with fans, and he performed it at the Motown 25 television special with the first public moonwalk, a moment television audiences hadn't processed before the news about it was already everywhere.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A city sidewalk at night with illuminated squares that light up underfoot, the visual of someone moving through a world that responds to their presence, electric and slightly unstable.

### 54. Cactus Tree — Joni Mitchell (1968)
From Mitchell's debut album, a song about a woman who loves many people and belongs to none of them, the cactus tree being a place of beauty that nothing touches. Mitchell was twenty-four when she wrote it and it already showed the harmonic complexity and the unflinching self-examination that would define her career. Her ability to be simultaneously the subject and the analyst of her own emotional life is here from the very beginning.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A desert landscape at golden hour, a tall saguaro cactus alone against a wide sky, the visual of something that is beautiful, solitary, and armed.

### 55. Both Sides Now — Joni Mitchell (1969)
Written on a plane when Mitchell was reading Henderson the Rain King and looked out at the clouds from above for the first time, she realized she'd only ever seen clouds from below and the song came from that. The original 1969 recording is full of youthful brightness. Her 2000 re-recording with an orchestra, done at fifty-six, is something different entirely: the same words delivered by someone who has actually lived them. Both recordings are the same song and completely different experiences.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Clouds viewed from above on a flight, the sun above the cloud layer turning everything gold and white, the visual of a familiar thing made unfamiliar by a change of perspective.

### 56. Ne Me Quitte Pas — Jacques Brel (1959)
One of the great songs about desperate love, or the wreckage left where love was, performed by a Belgian singer-songwriter who was already a major figure in French cabaret. Its central plea, don't leave me, spirals into increasingly extravagant promises that the narrator knows will not be kept. Scott Walker and Nina Simone both covered it in English under the title "If You Go Away," but Brel's original, performed as if the words are physically tearing him, is its own category of performance.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Paris apartment at night, one person at the door with a coat already on, the other at a table still, the visual of a goodbye that hasn't been accepted yet.

### 57. La Vie en Rose — Édith Piaf (1947)
Piaf wrote the lyrics in a café, reportedly on a paper bag, after her music collaborator Louiguy showed her the melody. She claimed in an interview that the whole thing took fifteen minutes. The song became the soundtrack to post-war Parisian optimism, the idea of seeing the world through rose-colored glasses as a form of resilience rather than denial. No recording of it has ever replaced the quality of Piaf's own voice, which seemed constitutionally unable to be casual about anything.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Paris street in early evening, café lights beginning to glow, the city viewed through a window, the warm haze of urban life turned gentle and golden.

### 58. Casta Diva — Bellini (1831)
An aria from the opera Norma, sung by a Druid priestess to the moon, its bel canto vocal line requiring extraordinary breath control and range to execute. Maria Callas's recording turned it into a touchstone for a generation's understanding of what the human voice could be. The aria's beauty is inseparable from its context: it is sung by a woman about to betray everything she believes in for love, and the music doesn't judge her.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A moonlit stone temple in an ancient forest, a solitary figure with arms raised toward a full moon, the visual of private devotion and private conflict held simultaneously.

### 59. Nessun Dorma — Puccini (1926)
From the opera Turandot, Puccini died before finishing it and the final act was completed by another composer. Nessun Dorma, "None shall sleep," is the tenor aria in which the Prince stakes everything on revealing his name at dawn. Its final "Vincerò" (I will win) became globally famous through Pavarotti's performance at the 1990 World Cup. The aria's combination of melodic beauty and raw emotional declaration makes it one of the most replicated moments in classical music.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Beijing's Forbidden City at the moment before dawn, torches burning, the sky beginning to lighten at the horizon, the visual of a city holding its breath before an announcement that will change everything.

### 60. The Four Seasons (Spring) — Vivaldi (1723)
The most frequently performed of Vivaldi's concerti, written with descriptive sonnets that correspond to specific passages in the music, making it one of the earliest examples of programmatic composition. Its opening theme has been used in so many advertisements, films, and public spaces that it now has a double life as both serious art and ambient wallpaper. Returning it to its proper context, the particular quality of Italian spring Vivaldi was describing, requires some effort.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An Italian garden in early April, blossom falling from a tree onto a stone path, birds visible in the branches, the season rendered as something just arrived and worth noticing.

### 61. So What — Miles Davis (1959)
The opening track of Kind of Blue, built on two modal scales rather than chord changes, giving the improvisers space that bebop's complex harmonic movement had never provided. The bass plays the theme, the horns respond, and the arrangement is over in a few seconds before the improvisation begins. Davis said he wanted something the musicians could play with their eyes closed, which is not the same as saying something simple.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A jazz club between sets, musicians talking at the edge of the stage, the audience murmuring, the specific anticipation of the moment before music starts again.

### 62. Ain't No Sunshine — Bill Withers (1971)
Withers was working in an aircraft factory when he wrote this, making airplane toilets, and he recorded it partly because a friend convinced him he should do something with his songs before it was too late. The song's repeated "I know, I know, I know" section was a placeholder he expected to replace with actual lyrics, and the producer told him to leave it. It won a Grammy and Withers quit the factory. He went on to make some of the most quietly essential American music of the decade.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An apartment in the afternoon with the curtains half-closed, one person sitting very still, the light suggesting absence rather than presence, the quality of a room where someone used to be.

### 63. Lean on Me — Bill Withers (1972)
Written in one sitting after Withers moved to Los Angeles from a small West Virginia town and felt the absence of the community he'd grown up in. It became the first Withers song to reach number one on the pop chart and has since been recorded by more artists than almost any other contemporary song. Its premise is so undefended in its offer of mutual support that performing it requires trusting the audience not to find it naïve, which audiences consistently do not.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A front porch in a small American town, neighbors gathered on a summer evening, the visual of community as something casual and ordinary rather than ceremonial.

### 64. Alright — Kendrick Lamar (2015)
Released in the midst of the Black Lives Matter movement, this became its unofficial anthem, chanted at protests across America, which Lamar reportedly watched with a complicated mix of pride and concern. The song's production by Pharrell Williams, bright and almost celebratory, sits against lyrics about police violence and systemic racism in a tension that captures exactly the emotional complexity the movement was navigating. The refrain "we gon' be alright" is an act of faith that knows exactly what it's refusing to look away from.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A street scene where a large crowd is gathered, the energy between grief and determination visible in the faces, warm afternoon light and hand-raised fists.

### 65. Mr. Tambourine Man — Bob Dylan (1965)
Recorded in one take after a long night, the jangly acoustic guitar and the surreal, hallucinatory images of the lyric became the foundation of the folk-rock sound that the Byrds then electrified into the number one version most casual listeners know. Dylan's original is more fragile and stranger than the Byrds' version, and the tambourine of the title has been interpreted as everything from drugs to art to God to a real person Dylan knew.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An empty beach at first light, a figure in the distance dancing to something no one else can hear, the visual of private joy that doesn't require an audience.

### 66. Blue — Joni Mitchell (1971)
Mitchell has described the Blue album as a transparent record, one in which she tried to achieve a complete vulnerability in the writing, and this title track may be the most exposed moment on it. Its address to a color, and through the color to a lover and a state of mind, is exactly the kind of lyrical strategy that sounds precarious in description and feels inevitable in the song itself.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A single blue object in a white room, the color as presence rather than backdrop, the visual of a mood given form.

### 67. The Sound of Silence — Simon & Garfunkel (1964)
Recorded unplugged and largely unnoticed in 1964, then overdubbed with electric instruments by producer Tom Wilson without Simon and Garfunkel's knowledge and re-released in 1965. Simon was furious when he heard it. It went to number one. The song's image of people talking without speaking and hearing without listening was partly about television and mass media, but the generation that made it famous heard something more personal in it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A crowded neon city street at 4am seen from above, light trails from cars below, people walking quickly and separately, the visual of density and isolation as the same thing.

### 68. Space Oddity — David Bowie (1969)
Released ten days before the Apollo 11 moon landing and with obvious intent, though Bowie said later he wrote it while watching 2001: A Space Odyssey on acid. Major Tom's detachment from his capsule and his calm acceptance of it became one of Bowie's recurring themes across his career, and the song's production, using a Stylophone bought from Rolf Harris, gave it a lo-fi domestic quality that made the cosmic setting stranger rather than grander.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A capsule floating in deep space, the Earth visible and receding in the porthole, the interior lit with instrument panel glow, solitude rendered as something almost peaceful.

### 69. Running Up That Hill — Kate Bush (1985)
Bush said she wanted to make a deal with God to switch places with a lover, to understand each other by inhabiting each other's experience, and the song's central proposition is that empathy requires something impossible. Its second life in 2022, driven by its appearance in Stranger Things, introduced it to a generation that heard it as a song about personal struggle rather than interpersonal exchange, which suggests its emotional vocabulary is wider than its specific subject.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A hill under a heavy sky, two figures running from opposite ends toward the same point at the top, the visual of convergence and the distance still remaining.

### 70. Waterloo — ABBA (1974)
The song that won Eurovision and launched ABBA's international career was deliberately designed to be a Eurovision winner: catchy, in English, with a historical hook and a melody that worked even if you didn't understand the words. It succeeded beyond any calculation. The use of Waterloo as a metaphor for surrendering to love while framing it as a military defeat is both knowing and completely unironic, which is the ABBA mode that nobody has successfully replicated.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A 1970s television performance set, glitter and platform boots, four performers in matching outfits, the aesthetic of pop as costume and spectacle.

### 71. The Way You Look Tonight — Fred Astaire (1936)
Written by Jerome Kern and Dorothy Fields for the film Swing Time, in which Astaire sings it to Ginger Rogers while she is washing her hair, entirely unaware she's being serenaded. Fields reportedly cried when she first heard Kern play the melody. It won the Academy Award for Best Original Song and has been recorded by more artists than almost any other standard, which is saying something given the competition of the Great American Songbook.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A 1930s Hollywood soundstage between takes, two performers in costume sitting apart with coffee, the glamour of the film world in its ordinary moments rather than its performed ones.

### 72. Over the Rainbow — Judy Garland (1939)
Almost cut from The Wizard of Oz twice on the grounds that it slowed the opening of the film. Harold Arlen reportedly wrote the melody while sitting outside Schwab's drug store and initially thought it was too important a tune for a children's film. The octave leap on the first word "somewhere" was considered by the studio to be undignified for a major production. Garland performed it for the rest of her life at every concert, and it became the song that made the most demands on her emotionally as she aged.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Kansas farmyard under a stormy grey-green sky, a rainbow visible at the edge of the weather, a young figure looking toward it from a dusty porch.

### 73. My Generation — The Who (1965)
Pete Townshend wrote the lyric's stammer as a way of expressing inarticulate anger rather than the drugs explanation that circulated at the time. Roger Daltrey's performance made the stammer dangerous rather than comic. The wish to die before getting old, delivered by people in their early twenties, was both a genuine generational statement and a provocation that time has made its own kind of commentary. Many of them didn't die before getting old, and the song has aged with them.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A mid-1960s concert with the band destroying their equipment at the end, smoke and splintered wood, the audience's reaction frozen at the moment between shock and exhilaration.

### 74. Strawberry Fields Forever — The Beatles (1967)
Two incompatible recordings at two different tempos and in two different keys, spliced together at the join because Lennon preferred the beginning of one and the end of the other and asked George Martin to make it work. Martin sped up one recording and slowed down the other until the keys matched at the join: it is visible to a trained ear at about the one-minute mark. The song's image of a real place in Liverpool where Lennon played as a child, made into a surrealist interior landscape, is one of the most fully realized moments in the Beatles' catalog.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Liverpool garden overgrown with long grass, trees creating a canopy, everything slightly too green and vivid, the visual of a childhood memory that may or may not have looked like this.

### 75. What'd I Say — Ray Charles (1959)
Improvised at the end of a dance gig when Charles and his band ran out of prepared material and had time left to fill. The audience responded with such force that Charles asked his band to remember what they'd played and they reconstructed it for the recording session. Its call-and-response structure, borrowed from gospel and applied to explicitly secular content, was scandalous to some and irresistible to nearly everyone else, and the combination of the two qualities is precisely why it matters.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dance hall in 1959 during the last song of the night, the crowd loose and warm, stage lights reflecting off a polished floor, the communal release of three hours of dancing arriving at its peak.

### 76. Good Vibrations — The Beach Boys (1966)
Took six months and ninety-six hours of studio time across four different studios to record, then the most expensive single ever made. Brian Wilson called it a "pocket symphony" and assembled it from recordings made in different sessions and states of mind, editing them together in a way that the technology of the time made enormously difficult. Its theremin hook, its shifting sections, and Wilson's harmonic ambitions in a three-minute package were a feat of production that contemporaries, including Paul McCartney, studied with something close to desperation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A California beach in 1966 with young people in the water and on the sand, the light catching every wave, the specific quality of West Coast optimism at the moment before it became aware of its own complications.

### 77. House of the Rising Sun — The Animals (1964)
A traditional American folk song of disputed origin, performed by the Animals as a single take in one three-hour recording session that cost less than five pounds. The organ line Eric Burdon brought to it transformed an Appalachian folk ballad into something that sounded like it had come from a specific city at a specific moral low point. Bob Dylan recorded it before the Animals, heard their version, and reportedly gave up playing it himself on the grounds that their recording was simply better.
**Deckoviz inspo:** New Orleans at dawn, the French Quarter empty, iron balconies and faded shutters, the visual of a city that has spent the night doing things it won't quite explain.

### 78. Ode to My Family — Cranberries (1994)
Dolores O'Riordan's song about homesickness and the specific sadness of growing up, and the impossibility of returning to what you were before the world started asking things of you. The Cranberries were from Limerick and their success had been rapid and disorienting, and the song's question about whether anyone else feels the way you do when home is somewhere you can no longer fully return to captured something that has kept it in circulation for decades.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An Irish rural landscape in autumn, a family farmhouse in the middle distance, the view of a place you grew up in from slightly too far away to make out the details.

### 79. One — U2 (1991)
Written in fifteen minutes during a tense session for Achtung Baby, at a point when the band was nearly broken up following Bono and The Edge's disagreement about musical direction. The song's lyrics about unity and coming together were written in a state of active disunity, and that tension is audible. It has since become one of the most performed songs at funerals and memorial services, which suggests its emotional range extends considerably beyond where it began.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Berlin studio in 1991, equipment everywhere and the four members of the band in different corners of the room, the visual of a creative partnership in the middle of its own crisis.

### 80. Under the Bridge — Red Hot Chili Peppers (1992)
Anthony Kiedis wrote the lyrics originally as a poem, partly about his experience of heroin addiction and the specific loneliness of being in Los Angeles feeling like the city was his only companion. Producer Rick Rubin found the poem and suggested it become a song. The melody arrived when Kiedis was humming it to himself walking to a writing session with Frusciante, who built the guitar part around what he heard. The song's combination of the confessional and the geographical gave it a specificity that prevented it from becoming merely a drug song.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The Los Angeles river channel at dusk, concrete banks and the highway above, a city visible in both directions but no one visible at ground level, the urban landscape as a kind of intimate loneliness.

### 81. Comfortably Numb — Pink Floyd (1979)
The Wall was Roger Waters's concept and he and David Gilmour disagreed about almost everything during its recording, which is visible in the song's structure: Waters wrote the verses, Gilmour the choruses, and the emotional register of each is completely different. Gilmour's guitar solos, particularly the second one, are frequently cited in musicians' polls as the greatest of all time. The song's image of a childhood illness as the last unguarded moment before adult numbness settled in is Waters at his most autobiographically raw.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A backstage dressing room before a major concert, a performer staring at the mirror, the crowd noise audible from beyond the walls, the private moment before the public one.

### 82. Purple Haze — Jimi Hendrix (1967)
The opening note is a dissonant tritone interval that classical music theory had called "the devil in music" and avoided for centuries. Hendrix used it as a welcome mat. He described writing the song after a dream about walking underwater, but its combination of psychedelic imagery and blues structure placed it at the exact intersection of the two traditions that shaped him. Guitar players learn it early and spend careers understanding it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A concert stage in purple light, a guitarist playing with his back to the audience, the visual of a performance that's also a private act, the crowd receiving something not entirely directed at them.

### 83. Lose You to Love Me — Selena Gomez (2019)
Written about the end of her relationship with Justin Bieber, which had been one of the most publicly documented romantic situations of the decade, the song's choice to use that subject as raw material for something spare and undefended was a different kind of move than the more produced breakup anthems the moment might have suggested. Its directness, combined with a production that strips everything back to voice and minimal arrangement, gives it a quality her earlier work had not reached.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An empty hotel room after a checkout, clothes gone from the wardrobe, a window showing an unfamiliar city, the visual of absence in a space that was only briefly occupied.

### 84. River — Joni Mitchell (1971)
A Christmas song about wishing there were a river she could skate away on, written while Mitchell was in California missing Canadian winters and processing a relationship ending during the holiday season. The song's use of the most communal time of year as a backdrop for profound personal isolation gives it a specificity that has made it one of the most emotionally requested tracks during December, which Mitchell has described as something she finds both touching and ironic given that she wrote it in despair.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A frozen river in a Canadian winter, bare trees on either bank, grey sky, a pair of skates on the ice near the bank, the image of an escape route that isn't quite ready.

### 85. This Must Be the Place (Naive Melody) — Talking Heads (1983)
David Byrne wrote it as a love song to his then-girlfriend, which he considered at the time to be against his natural instincts as a songwriter. The song's arrangement, bass and keyboards carrying the weight while the guitar plays simple melodic phrases, was built by the band starting separately and joining together, each adding a layer. Its warmth in the context of an album and band usually associated with controlled cerebral distance makes it land harder than it would have from a more consistently warm source.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A domestic interior made strange by the intensity of feeling in it, familiar furniture in warm lamplight, the home as a place that has become the center of the world because of who's in it.

### 86. No Scrubs — TLC (1999)
Written by Kandi Burruss and Tameka "Tiny" Cottle, this was a response to male rappers' habit of listing criteria for their ideal women, and its criteria for what TLC wouldn't accept became a defining statement of feminine self-respect in late 90s pop. Its video, spacesuits and floating cars, made the song's attitude visual in a way that amplified rather than distracted from the lyric. It has been covered, sampled, and referenced more than almost any other pop track of its era.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A late 1990s city sidewalk, three women walking past a man leaning against a parked car, the body language of complete indifference rendered in the specific visual language of the era.

### 87. Toxic — Britney Spears (2003)
Written by four Swedes, the song's central achievement is that its Bond-theme-meets-Bollywood production sounds inevitable rather than bizarre in retrospect. It was recorded during the period of Spears's career when she was exercising the most creative control, and her vocal on it, processed and layered in ways that make the artifice part of the point, represents a precise understanding of production as persona. It won her first Grammy and has been reassessed repeatedly in the years since as one of the defining pop tracks of its decade.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An airplane in flight at night, the cabin half-lit and mostly empty, a single figure moving through it as if through a dream, the visual of glamour and danger in the same body.

### 88. Formation — Beyoncé (2016)
Released the night before the Super Bowl halftime show in which she performed it, with a video that referenced Hurricane Katrina, Black Southern culture, and police violence with imagery too specific and deliberate to be accidental. The performance, with dancers in Black Panther-inspired uniforms, created a response so outsized that it confirmed the song had touched something the culture wasn't ready to have touched. Its production, Houston hip-hop aesthetics applied to a global pop context, was as much a statement as its visual.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A New Orleans street scene post-Katrina, a bouncing car surrounded by water but the occupants still dancing, the image of joy as defiance rather than ignorance.

### 89. Redbone — Childish Gambino (2016)
Donald Glover wrote it as a homage to the P-Funk and soul sound of the 1970s but played the tempo slow enough that the groove becomes unsettling rather than inviting. Its lyric about staying woke delivered in the voice of a 70s R&B track is either a stylistic exercise or a structural argument about whether style and political consciousness can occupy the same space without compromising each other, and the fact that the question remains open is part of the song's achievement.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A 1970s Los Angeles apartment at 2am, the television on with the sound off, the window open to a warm night, the visual of being awake past the hour when most people have decided to trust the dark.

### 90. Yesterday Once More — Carpenters (1973)
Karen Carpenter's voice is one of the great instruments in popular music and this is one of its most characteristic showcases: a song about the power of radio to return you to emotional states you thought were finished, delivered in a tone of gentle, unironic warmth. The Carpenters' reputation suffered for decades from being associated with uncoolness, and the reassessment of their recordings, which were technically exceptional, has been gathering pace ever since.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A 1970s kitchen, a transistor radio on the counter, a woman pausing in the middle of some household task to listen, the private moment of music stopping time.

### 91. Africa — Toto (1982)
Lyricist David Paich wrote it about a young man in America watching a documentary about Africa and feeling the strange longing of somewhere he's never been. The song's production is meticulous in the way that early 80s session musician work is meticulous: everything is in exactly the right place and the result sounds simultaneously effortless and engineered. Its second life in internet culture and its adoption as an ironic anthem have paradoxically deepened rather than diminished its emotional hold on people.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The East African savanna at dusk, Kilimanjaro visible in the far distance, rain approaching from the horizon, the visual of a landscape imagined from thousands of miles away and somehow recognized.

### 92. Take Me to Church — Hozier (2013)
Andrew Hozier-Byrne wrote it partly as a response to the Catholic Church's institutional role in Irish life and partly as a celebration of physical love as its own form of devotion. The gospel imagery is used rather than mocked, and the song's ability to make a secular act feel sacred without irony is its main formal achievement. Its release coincided with Ireland's debate on marriage equality, and the video's explicit depiction of a gay couple under attack ensured the song's political dimension was unmissable.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A rural Irish church at dusk, the stained glass lit from inside, the graveyard outside in long shadow, the architecture of religious authority rendered as both beautiful and enclosing.

### 93. Chandelier — Sia (2014)
Sia wrote the song about her years of alcohol abuse during a period of depression, the chandelier being the image of swinging high before the crash. She then gave the visual language of the song to Maddie Ziegler to perform in the video, dissociating the physical performance from herself in a way that became as discussed as the song itself. The vocal performance, which Sia has described as something she could only access by not looking at herself in the glass of the vocal booth, is technically extraordinary.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A large empty house at night, a single chandelier blazing above a bare floor, a figure below it in motion, the visual of excess and release in a space that's too big for one person.

### 94. Where Is the Love? — Black Eyed Peas (2003)
Released in the aftermath of September 11, the song's litany of social failures, racism, gang violence, terrorism, environmental destruction, was unusual territory for a commercial hip-hop pop act and it became their biggest hit to that point. will.i.am has said it was the song that changed how he thought about what popular music was for. Its earnestness was at odds with the prevailing irony of early 2000s culture and succeeded precisely because of that.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A world map seen from above at night, city lights clustered on the coasts and along rivers, the dark spaces between them as conspicuous as the light.

### 95. Feeling Good — Nina Simone (1965)
Written for the British musical The Roar of the Greasepaint, The Smell of the Crowd, where it was performed by a character called The Negro as an anthem of new beginnings. Simone's recording transformed it from a show tune with a political subtext into something that sounds constitutive of freedom rather than merely celebratory of it. Her arrangement, slower and more deliberate than the original, makes each image, the new dawn, the dragonfly, the butterfly, the feeling good, arrive with the weight of something that has been earned.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A summer morning with sunlight breaking through trees, birds visible in flight above a river, the natural world rendered as something that belongs to the person looking at it, warm and open.

### 96. Pyramid Song — Radiohead (2001)
Thom Yorke based the piano rhythm on the irrational meter of an old spiritual called "Amazing Grace," which gives the song its sense of being rhythmically unmoored. Its lyric describes death as a journey to a place where everyone is together and nothing is owned, a vision of the afterlife as a kind of communism that Yorke has said he can't quite believe in but can't stop wanting. Johnny Greenwood's string arrangement arrives late and turns the song into something different from what it was building toward, which is the whole point.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An ancient pyramid at night reflected in a still black river, stars visible in both the sky and the reflection, the visual of time as something that doesn't run in a single direction.

### 97. Glycerine — Bush (1994)
Gavin Rossdale wrote this in three minutes with an acoustic guitar the night before it was needed for the recording session. Its ambiguous lyric about love and disappointment and the specific phrase "I'm never alone, I'm alone all the time" has been parsed by fans for thirty years without settling into a single interpretation. The song's directness and its emotional exposure, written so quickly there was no time to intellectualize it, is part of why it has lasted.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A London flat in winter, a guitar against the wall, someone sitting on the floor with the curtains open on a grey day, the particular atmosphere of being both inside and shut out.

### 98. Landslide — Fleetwood Mac (1975)
Stevie Nicks wrote it at 26, sitting alone in Aspen, Colorado, facing the decision of whether to continue pursuing music or accept a more conventional life. It's a song about standing at a threshold with no certainty about which side to step toward. She has performed it at different stages of her life and said each time it means something different, which is the property of good songs: they age with the people who carry them.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A mountain slope in early winter, the first snow making the landscape unfamiliar, a lone figure halfway up looking back down at where they came from, quiet and suspended.

### 99. Everybody Hurts — R.E.M. (1992)
Written by drummer Bill Berry at a point in the band's tour cycle when everyone was close to exhaustion and wondering aloud whether to continue. Michael Stipe's vocal is deliberately gentle, even tentative, because the song's address to someone at the edge of endurance needed to not feel like performance. It became one of the most requested tracks on crisis lines and grief counseling playlists, which the band received as the most serious possible compliment.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A traffic jam on an empty stretch of highway, cars stopped with no visible reason, a figure sitting on the roadside with their head down, the visual of being halted when you most need to keep moving.

### 100. Here Comes the Sun — The Beatles (1969)
George Harrison wrote it in Eric Clapton's garden after truanting from a particularly difficult Abbey Road business meeting, picking up a guitar and feeling the relief of being somewhere else doing something else. It was recorded near the end of the Beatles' final recording sessions, with a band that was barely speaking to each other, and contains no audible trace of the circumstances of its making. It is purely a song about warmth arriving after a long cold time, and it is the most frequently played Beatles track on streaming services, which suggests that people still need it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A garden at the exact moment the sun comes out after a long grey morning, the quality of light that changes everything without doing anything dramatic, green and warm and suddenly different from what it was ten seconds ago.

## Closing thoughts
A great song is unreasonably ambitious for its duration. It asks to be remembered, to reshape how you feel about something, to become the soundtrack to moments you won't choose it for consciously but which it will arrive at anyway: a particular drive at a particular hour, a loss, a reunion, a morning that feels different from other mornings without the reason being immediately clear. The songs on this list all achieved that, for at least some people, in some era.

For Deckoviz, the visual life of music is the part that usually gets left behind. You hear a song, you feel its world, and then the feeling fades because there's nowhere for it to live outside the listening. Vizzy changes that proposition. The cotton fields of Ma Rainey's era, the Berlin Wall in Bowie's recording session, Joni Mitchell's frozen Canadian river, the Liverpool garden that became Strawberry Fields, these aren't just album art or music video imagery. They're environments. They belong on your wall, shifting with the time of day and the light in the room, the same way a song shifts with your mood when you put it on.
