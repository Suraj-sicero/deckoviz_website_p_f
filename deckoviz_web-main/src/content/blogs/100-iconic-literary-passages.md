## Introduction
Some sentences are doors. You read them and you are no longer where you were — you are inside a fog-bound London street, a Russian ballroom, a whale's open mouth, a child's bedroom on the night everything changed. A great passage does this instantly, without warning, and it does not let you go even after you have finished reading it.

This collection gathers 100 of the most iconic passages in literature — openings, closings, and the moments in between that readers have carried with them for decades or centuries. Many of these are old enough to belong to everyone: the great public-domain works of the 18th and 19th centuries, where we can give you the actual words, in full, exactly as they were written. For more recent works, still protected by copyright, we describe the passage, its context, and why it matters, with a single short phrase quoted directly — enough to point you toward the book itself, where the full passage is waiting.

What unites all 100 is a quality of arrival. Each one is a moment where a writer found, after however many drafts, exactly the right words for something that had never quite been said that way before — and the result outlived its author, its century, and its original readers, becoming part of the permanent furniture of the literate mind.

**On your Deckoviz DASPort:** Long-form text is one of the most striking and underused forms of frame art. A full passage — Dickens's opening to Bleak House, Melville's meditation on the colour white, the final paragraph of The Great Gatsby — rendered as typographic art, in a serif face on a textured cream background, or white text on deep midnight blue, becomes something closer to a manuscript page than a quote poster. It rewards a second and third reading in a way a single line cannot.

Use this guide as a brief for Vizzy sessions building literary passage art: full-page typographic treatments for the public-domain passages, paired with moodscapes that match their settings — London fog for Dickens, Russian ballroom candlelight for Tolstoy, the green light across the bay for Fitzgerald, the open ocean for Melville. For the modern works, use the short quoted phrase as an anchor for poster art, paired with imagery of the book's world. Build a rotating "first lines and last lines" collection — the openings and closings of the books that have meant the most to you, displayed as the literary monuments they are.

## Part One: The Public Domain Classics (Full Passages)

### 1. A Tale of Two Cities — Charles Dickens (1859) Opening
"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair, we had everything before us, we had nothing before us, we were all going direct to Heaven, we were all going direct the other way—in short, the period was so far like the present period, that some of its noisiest authorities insisted on its being received, for good or for evil, in the superlative degree of comparison only."
The most famous opening sentence in English literature — a single, sustained antithesis that captures the contradictions of revolutionary France and, by extension, every era that believes itself uniquely chaotic. 
**Deckoviz:** The full passage as typographic art against a Parisian dawn — half in shadow, half in light, the visual antithesis matching the textual one.

### 2. Pride and Prejudice — Jane Austen (1813) Opening
"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife. However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered the rightful property of some one or other of their daughters."
The wittiest opening in English fiction — a sentence that announces, in its first clause, the entire social comedy that follows, and undercuts itself in its second. 
**Deckoviz:** The opening line in elegant serif typography against a Regency drawing room moodscape — soft cream and sage palette.

### 3. Moby-Dick — Herman Melville (1851) The Whiteness of the Whale
"Is it that by its indefiniteness it shadows forth the heartless voids and immensities of the universe, and thus stabs us from behind with the thought of annihilation, when beholding the white depths of the milky way? Or is it, that as in essence whiteness is not so much a colour as the visible absence of colour, and at the same time the concrete of all colours; is it for these reasons that there is such a dumb blankness, full of meaning, in a wide landscape of snows—a colourless, all-colour of atheism from which we shrink?"
Melville's meditation on the colour white as the most terrifying colour of all — a passage that turns from natural history into pure metaphysical dread within a single paragraph. 
**Deckoviz:** White-on-white typographic art — text barely visible against a field of snow or sea-foam, the "colourless, all-colour" made literal.

### 4. Moby-Dick — Herman Melville (1851) Opening
"Call me Ishmael. Some years ago—never mind how long precisely—having little or no money in my purse, and nothing particular to interest me on shore, I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world. It is a way I have of driving off the spleen, and regulating the circulation."
Three words that constitute one of the most recognisable openings in world literature — an invitation into a narrator's confidence that has never worn out. 
**Deckoviz:** "Call me Ishmael" as standalone typographic art against deep ocean blue — minimal, immediate, instantly recognisable.

### 5. Great Expectations — Charles Dickens (1861) Opening
"My father's family name being Pirrip, and my Christian name Philip, my infant tongue could make of both names nothing longer or more explicit than Pip. So, I called myself Pip, and came to be called Pip."
A small, tender, and slightly comic opening — a boy naming himself before the story of his life can properly begin. 
**Deckoviz:** "So, I called myself Pip" in warm typography against a misty marsh moodscape — the opening of one of fiction's great coming-of-age stories.

### 6. Anna Karenina — Leo Tolstoy (1878) Opening
"Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."
The most economical and most quoted opening sentence in the Russian novel — eleven words containing an entire theory of domestic life. 
**Deckoviz:** The opening line in Cyrillic-inspired typography against a Russian winter palette — deep blues, candlelit interiors.

### 7. War and Peace — Leo Tolstoy (1869) The Comet
"Pierre felt as though, that night, an immense vault, full of stars, had spread itself out above him."
This brief image — taken from the larger meditation on the great comet of 1812, which appears throughout the novel as an omen — captures Tolstoy's recurring device of using the night sky to register the smallness and wonder of individual human consciousness against the scale of history. 
**Deckoviz:** A vast star-filled sky as moodscape, with the line displayed small at the bottom of the frame — scale as the visual point.

### 8. Jane Eyre — Charlotte Brontë (1847) Opening
"There was no possibility of taking a walk that day. We had been wandering, indeed, in the leafless shrubbery an hour in the morning; but since dinner (Mrs. Reed, when there was no company, dined early) the cold winter afternoon had grown so dark with cloud and rain that further outdoor exercise was now out of the question."
The grey, confined opening of one of literature's great accounts of a confined girlhood — weather as imprisonment from the very first line. 
**Deckoviz:** Grey winter window-pane moodscape with rain, the passage in cool typography — the visual equivalent of being shut indoors.

### 9. Wuthering Heights — Emily Brontë (1847) Opening
"1801.—I have just returned from a visit to my landlord—the solitary neighbour that I shall be troubled with. This is certainly a beautiful country! In all England, I do not think that I could have fixed on a situation so completely removed from the stir of society."
A deceptively calm opening — a stranger arriving at a remote house, unaware of the storm of feeling he is about to encounter. 
**Deckoviz:** The Yorkshire moors in mist, the passage in weathered typography — the calm before the novel's ferocity.

### 10. The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn — Mark Twain (1884) Opening
"You don't know about me without you have read a book by the name of The Adventures of Tom Sawyer; but that ain't no matter. That book was made by Mr. Mark Twain, and he told the truth, mainly."
The first great American novel written entirely in vernacular voice — the opening establishes, in a single sentence, the colloquial honesty that Hemingway later said all American literature descends from. 
**Deckoviz:** Mississippi River raft moodscape at dawn, the passage in hand-lettered, rough-edged typography matching Huck's voice.

### 11. A Christmas Carol — Charles Dickens (1843) Opening
"Marley was dead: to begin with. There is no doubt whatever about that. The register of his burial was signed by the clergyman, the clerk, the undertaker, and the chief mourner. Scrooge signed it. And Scrooge's name was good upon 'Change, for anything he chose to put his hand to."
A blunt, almost comic opening for a ghost story — establishing the fact of death with bureaucratic certainty before the supernatural arrives. 
**Deckoviz:** "Marley was dead: to begin with" as winter typographic art against a Victorian London snowscape — a natural Deckoviz Christmas collection centrepiece.

### 12. A Christmas Carol — Charles Dickens (1843) The Ghost of Christmas Present
"There are some upon this earth of yours," returned the Spirit, "who lay claim to know us, and who do their deeds of passion, pride, ill-will, hatred, envy, bigotry, and selfishness in our name, who are as strange to us and all our kith and kin, as if they had never lived. Remember that, and charge their doings on themselves, not us."
One of the moral centres of the book — the Spirit's rebuke of those who invoke goodness while practising its opposite. 
**Deckoviz:** Warm candlelit Victorian interior moodscape with the passage in gold typography — a piece for the Deckoviz wisdom and conscience collection.

### 13. The Picture of Dorian Gray — Oscar Wilde (1890) Preface
"The artist is the creator of beautiful things. To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim. The critic is he who can translate into another manner or a new material his impression of beautiful things."
The opening of Wilde's preface — an aesthetic manifesto in miniature, setting out the philosophy that the novel itself will test to destruction. 
**Deckoviz:** The preface as typographic art against a deep emerald or crimson background — Wilde's aestheticism rendered in colour and serif type.

### 14. The Picture of Dorian Gray — Oscar Wilde (1890) Closing
"As he entered he saw lying on the floor a portrait of their master, as they had last seen him, in all the wonder of his exquisite youth and beauty. Lying on the floor was a dead man, in evening dress, with a knife in his heart. He was withered, wrinkled, and loathsome of visage. It was not till they had examined the rings that they recognized who it was."
The novel's final reversal — the portrait restored to youth, and the man himself revealed as the true image of his corruption. 
**Deckoviz:** A portrait-and-mirror visual concept — youth and decay as two halves of a single composition.

### 15. The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde — Robert Louis Stevenson (1886) Closing
"Here then, as I lay down the pen and proceed to seal up my confession, I bring the life of that unhappy Henry Jekyll to an end."
The last line of Jekyll's own statement, ending the novel — a quiet, almost administrative close to one of the great horror stories of duality. 
**Deckoviz:** A sealed letter and candle moodscape, the line in formal Victorian typography — the closing of a confession as visual art.

### 16. Frankenstein — Mary Shelley (1818) Opening of the Creature's narrative
"It is with considerable difficulty that I remember the original era of my being: all the events of that period appear confused and indistinct. A strange multiplicity of sensations seized me, and I saw, felt, heard, and smelt at the same time; and it was, indeed, a long time before I learned to distinguish between the operations of my various senses."
The Creature's account of his own awakening — one of literature's first attempts to imagine consciousness from absolute zero, before language, before identity. 
**Deckoviz:** An Arctic-white moodscape with the passage in stark, spaced-out typography — consciousness arriving as pure sensation.

### 17. Dracula — Bram Stoker (1897) Opening
"3 May. Bistritz.—Left Munich at 8:35 P.M., on 1st May, arriving at Vienna early next morning; should have arrived at 6:46, but train was an hour late. Buda-Pesth seems a wonderful place, from the glimpse which I got of it from the train and the little I could walk through the streets."
The diary-entry opening that grounds the novel's supernatural events in the mundane texture of 19th-century European rail travel — the ordinary as the doorway to the uncanny. 
**Deckoviz:** A railway timetable aesthetic transitioning into Transylvanian mountains — the ordinary becoming strange.

### 18. Middlemarch — George Eliot (1871–72) Finale
"Her finely touched spirit had still its fine issues, though they were not widely visible. Her full nature, like that river of which Cyrus broke the strength, spent itself in channels which had no great name on the earth. But the effect of her being on those around her was incalculably diffusive: for the growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts; and that things are not so ill with you and me as they might have been, is half owing to the number who lived faithfully a hidden life, and rest in unvisited tombs."
The closing meditation on Dorothea Brooke — and one of the most quietly devastating tributes to ordinary, unrecorded goodness in the English language. 
**Deckoviz:** "Unhistoric acts" as the centrepiece of an autumn English countryside moodscape.

### 19. The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — Arthur Conan Doyle (1892) A Scandal in Bohemia
"To Sherlock Holmes she is always THE woman. I have seldom heard him mention her under any other name. In his eyes she eclipses and predominates the whole of her sex. It was not that he felt any emotion akin to love for Irene Adler. All emotions, and that one particularly, were abhorrent to his cold, precise but admirably balanced mind."
The opening of the first Holmes short story — establishing, in Watson's voice, the central tension of the character. 
**Deckoviz:** Foggy Baker Street moodscape with the passage in classic detective-fiction serif typography.

### 20. The Time Machine — H.G. Wells (1895) Closing
"He, I know—for the question had been discussed among us long before the Time Machine was made—thought but cheerlessly of the Advancement of Mankind, and saw in the growing pile of civilization only a foolish heaping that must inevitably fall back upon and destroy its makers in the end. If that is so, it remains for us to live as though it were not so."
The novel's final philosophical turn — an early articulation of the idea that meaning must be chosen even if the cosmos offers none. 
**Deckoviz:** "Live as though it were not so" as typographic art against a deep-time landscape.

### 21. The Importance of Being Earnest — Oscar Wilde (1895) Act I
"ALGERNON: Did you hear what I was playing, Lane? LANE: I didn't think it polite to listen, sir. ALGERNON: I'm sorry for that, for your sake. I don't play accurately—anyone can play accurately—but I play with wonderful expression. As far as the piano is concerned, sentimentality is my forte. I keep science for Life."
The opening exchange of Wilde's comic masterpiece — wit deployed as a complete philosophy of living. 
**Deckoviz:** Drawing-room comedy aesthetic — pale green and gold, the dialogue as theatrical typographic art.

### 22. Vanity Fair — William Makepeace Thackeray (1848) Closing
"Ah, Vanitas Vanitatum! which of us is happy in this world? Which of us has his desire? or, having it, is satisfied?—come, children, let us shut up the box and the puppets, for our play is played out."
The narrator's direct address to the reader at the novel's close — the entire story revealed as a puppet show. 
**Deckoviz:** A theatre curtain closing on miniature figures — the puppet-box as visual metaphor for the novel's vast social panorama.

### 23. The Scarlet Letter — Nathaniel Hawthorne (1850) Closing
"ON A FIELD, SABLE, THE LETTER A, GULES."
The novel's final line — the heraldic motto on Hester Prynne's tombstone, reducing an entire novel of shame, love, and redemption to a single emblem. 
**Deckoviz:** A single scarlet "A" on black as minimalist typographic art.

### 24. Walden — Henry David Thoreau (1854) Conclusion
"I learned this, at least, by my experiment: that if one advances confidently in the direction of his dreams, and endeavors to live the life which he has imagined, he will meet with a success unexpected in common hours."
The passage from which the most famous (and most paraphrased) Thoreau quote derives. 
**Deckoviz:** Walden Pond at dawn, the full passage in restrained typography.

### 25. Leaves of Grass — Walt Whitman (1855) Song of Myself
"I celebrate myself, and sing myself, / And what I assume you shall assume, / For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you. / I loiter and invite my soul, / I lean and loafe at my ease observing a spear of summer grass."
The opening of American poetry's founding long poem — the self declared as both subject and shared property. 
**Deckoviz:** "For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you" as typographic art over an open American landscape.

### 26. The Awakening — Kate Chopin (1899) Closing
"She thought of the bird with the broken wing beating, beating, beating its disabled wings down, down to the water. ... The voice of the sea is seductive; never ceasing, whispering, clamoring, murmuring, inviting the soul to wander for a spell in abysses of solitude."
Edna Pontellier's final swim — written in prose that turns the sea into both invitation and dissolution. 
**Deckoviz:** Open ocean horizon at dusk, the passage in pale typography.

### 27. The Wonderful Wizard of Oz — L. Frank Baum (1900) Closing
"'My darling child!' she cried, folding the little girl in her arms and covering her face with kisses. 'Where in the world did you come from?' 'From the Land of Oz,' said Dorothy, gravely. 'And here is Toto, too. And oh, Aunt Em! I'm so glad to be at home again!'"
The quiet domestic ending after the spectacle of Oz — home as the destination of every fantastic journey. 
**Deckoviz:** A Kansas farmhouse doorway with golden light — "I'm so glad to be at home again" as a homecoming typographic concept.

### 28. Heart of Darkness — Joseph Conrad (1899) Closing
"The brown current ran swiftly out of the heart of darkness, bearing us down towards the sea with twice the speed of our upward progress; and Kurtz's life was running swiftly, too, ebbing, ebbing out of his heart into the sea of inexorable time. ... The offing was barred by a black bank of clouds, and the tranquil waterway leading to the uttermost ends of the earth flowed sombre under an overcast sky—seemed to lead into the heart of an immense darkness."
The novel's closing image — the Thames itself transformed into the river of the title. 
**Deckoviz:** A river at dusk under heavy cloud, the passage in dark grey typography.

### 29. The Call of the Wild — Jack London (1903) Closing
"When the long winter nights come on and the wolves follow their meat into the lower valleys, he may be seen running at the head of the pack through the pale moonlight or glimmering borealis, leaping gigantic above his fellows, his great throat a-bellow as he sings a song of the younger world, which is the song of the pack."
The final image of Buck, fully returned to wildness. 
**Deckoviz:** A wolf silhouette against the aurora borealis.

### 30. Anne of Green Gables — L.M. Montgomery (1908) Closing
"'God's in his heaven, all's right with the world,' whispered Anne softly."
Anne's quiet final line — a quotation from Browning that closes the novel on a note of complete, hard-won contentment. 
**Deckoviz:** A Prince Edward Island sunset over orchards — the line as a gentle evening typographic concept.

### 31. The Secret Garden — Frances Hodgson Burnett (1911) Closing
"And, oh, the things they found in that garden! ... they were so absorbed in cricket and watching the new flowers spring up that they never noticed Mary, sitting on the grass and watching them too."
The garden's transformation as the children's own transformation. 
**Deckoviz:** An English walled garden in spring bloom — renewal and growth as a Deckoviz seasonal concept.

### 32. Dubliners — James Joyce (1914) The Dead
"A few light taps upon the pane made him turn to the window. It had begun to snow again. ... His soul swooned slowly as he heard the snow falling faintly through the universe and faintly falling, like the descent of their last end, upon all the living and the dead."
The closing of Joyce's greatest short story — snow falling over all of Ireland, living and dead together. 
**Deckoviz:** Snow falling over a dark Irish landscape at night.

### 33. The Voyage Out — Virginia Woolf (1915) Opening
"As the streets that lead from the Strand to the Embankment are very narrow, it is better not to walk down them arm-in-arm. If you persist, lawyers' clerks will have to make flying leaps into the mud; young lady typists will have to fidget behind you."
Woolf's first novel opens with the texture of London street life. 
**Deckoviz:** Edwardian London street scene at dusk.

### 34. The Souls of Black Folk — W.E.B. Du Bois (1903) Opening
"Between me and the other world there is ever an unasked question: unasked by some through feelings of delicacy; ... How does it feel to be a colored man? ... I answer seldom a word."
The opening of Du Bois's foundational essay collection. 
**Deckoviz:** A typographic concept in deep indigo and gold.

### 35. The Souls of Black Folk — W.E.B. Du Bois (1903) Of Our Spiritual Strivings
"It is a peculiar sensation, this double-consciousness, this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others, of measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity."
The most quoted passage in the book. 
**Deckoviz:** A mirror-within-a-mirror visual concept.

### 36. The Yellow Wallpaper — Charlotte Perkins Gilman (1892) Closing
"'I've got out at last,' said I, 'in spite of you and Jane! And I've pulled off most of the paper, so you can't put me back!'"
The story's chilling final lines — a descent into madness rendered as a kind of liberation. 
**Deckoviz:** A faded yellow wallpaper texture as background.

### 37. My Ántonia — Willa Cather (1918) Closing
"Whatever we had missed, we possessed together the precious, the incommunicable past."
The novel's final line — a single sentence that captures Cather's recurring theme. 
**Deckoviz:** Nebraska prairie at golden hour.

### 38. O Pioneers! — Willa Cather (1913) Closing
"Fortunate country, that is one day to receive hearts like Alexandra's into its bosom, to give them out again in the shining eyes of a younger generation!"
The closing tribute to Alexandra Bergson. 
**Deckoviz:** Golden prairie wheat fields at sunset.

### 39. The Age of Innocence — Edith Wharton (1920) Closing
"He sat for a long time on the bench in the thickening dusk, his eyes fixed on the balcony. ... At length a light shone through the windows... At that, as if it had been the signal he waited for, Newland Archer got up slowly and walked back alone to his hotel."
The novel's final, quietly devastating image. 
**Deckoviz:** A lit balcony window at dusk, seen from a park bench.

### 40. This Side of Paradise — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1920) Closing
"He stretched out his arms to the crystalline, radiant sky. 'I know myself,' he cried, 'but that is all—'"
Fitzgerald's first novel ends with its young protagonist's claim to self-knowledge. 
**Deckoviz:** Outstretched figure against a star-filled sky.

### 41. The Great Gatsby — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925) Closing
"Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us. It eluded us then, but that's no matter—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch out our arms farther. ... And one fine morning— So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."
The most famous closing passage in American fiction. 
**Deckoviz:** The green light across the bay at dusk.

### 42. The Great Gatsby — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925) Opening
"In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since. 'Whenever you feel like criticizing any one,' he told me, 'just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had.'"
The novel's opening — Nick Carraway's inherited code of tolerance. 
**Deckoviz:** A father-and-son silhouette against a Midwestern landscape.

### 43. The Sun Also Rises — Ernest Hemingway (1926) Closing
"'Oh, Jake,' Brett said, 'we could have had such a damned good time together.' Ahead was a mounted policeman in khaki directing traffic. He raised his baton. The car slowed suddenly, pressing Brett against me. 'Yes,' I said. 'Isn't it pretty to think so?'"
The novel's final exchange. 
**Deckoviz:** A Madrid taxi at night, two silhouettes.

### 44. The Sound and the Fury — William Faulkner (1929) Closing
"The broken flower drooped over Ben's fist and his eyes were empty and blue and serene again as cornice and façade flowed smoothly once more from left to right, post and tree, window and doorway and signboard, each in its ordered place."
The novel's final image — the world restored to its proper, indifferent order. 
**Deckoviz:** A Southern street scene flowing past a carriage window.

### 45. To the Lighthouse — Virginia Woolf (1927) Closing
"Yes, she thought, laying down her brush in extreme fatigue, I have had my vision."
The novel's final line — Lily Briscoe completing her painting. 
**Deckoviz:** A finished canvas on an easel, seaside light through a window.

"When the candles are all burnt out, when the dance is over, the boys must go home to bed. ... You have a wonderfully beautiful face, Mr. Gray. Don't frown. You have. And Beauty is a form of Genius—is higher, indeed, than Genius, as it needs no explanation."

### 48. The Wind in the Willows — Kenneth Grahame (1908) The Piper at the Gates of Dawn
"'O, never, never, never. Not in this world, did anything smell so good before.' ... 'This is the place of my song-dream, the place the music played to me,' whispered the Rat, as if in a trance."
One of the most lyrical and mystical passages in children's literature.

### 49. Peter Pan — J.M. Barrie (1911) Closing
"When Wendy grew up she had a daughter, and this is she, Jane ... and thus it will go on, so long as children are gay and innocent and heartless."
The novel's closing lines. 
**Deckoviz:** A nursery window open to the night sky over London.

### 50. Little Women — Louisa May Alcott (1868) Closing
"'Oh, my girls, however long you may live, I never can wish you a greater happiness than this!'"
Marmee's closing benediction over her daughters and grandchildren. 
**Deckoviz:** A warm domestic interior at dusk, family gathered.



### 51. Tess of the d'Urbervilles — Thomas Hardy (1891) Closing
"'Justice' was done, and the President of the Immortals (in Aeschylean phrase) had ended his sport with Tess."
Hardy's bitterly ironic closing line — invoking Greek tragedy to frame Tess's death as the cruel amusement of indifferent gods. 
**Deckoviz:** A Wessex landscape at dawn under heavy sky — the line as a stark, tragic typographic concept.

### 52. The Mayor of Casterbridge — Thomas Hardy (1886) Closing
"...happiness was but the occasional episode in a general drama of pain."
The novel's closing philosophical statement — Hardy's bleak summary of the human condition, delivered with characteristic plainness. 
**Deckoviz:** A muted English landscape at dusk — the line as a contemplative, unflinching typographic piece for the Deckoviz Deep Thought collection.

### 53. Treasure Island — Robert Louis Stevenson (1883) Closing
"Oxen and wain-ropes would not bring me back again to that accursed island; and the worst dreams that ever I have are when I hear the surf booming about its coasts, or start upright in bed, with the sharp voice of Captain Flint still ringing in my ears: 'Pieces of eight! Pieces of eight!'"
Jim Hawkins's closing reflection — adventure remembered not with longing but with the lingering terror of what it cost. 
**Deckoviz:** A parrot silhouette against a tropical sunset, ship in the distance — "Pieces of eight!" as a classic adventure typographic concept.

### 54. Robinson Crusoe — Daniel Defoe (1719) Opening
"I was born in the year 1632, in the city of York, of a good family, though not of that country, my father being a foreigner of Bremen, who settled first at Hull."
The plain, matter-of-fact opening of the first great English novel. 
**Deckoviz:** A weathered ship's log aesthetic — the opening as the foundation-stone of the novel form, displayed in period typography.

### 55. Robinson Crusoe — Daniel Defoe (1719) The Footprint
"I happen'd one day about noon going towards my boat, I was exceedingly surpriz'd with the print of a man's naked foot on the shore, which was very plain to be seen in the sand."
One of the most famous single images in the history of the novel. 
**Deckoviz:** A single footprint on an empty beach — minimalist, instantly recognisable, perfect for a Deckoviz Solitude collection.

### 56. Gulliver's Travels — Jonathan Swift (1726) A Voyage to Lilliput
"I lay down to rest on the grass, which was very short and soft, where I slept sounder than ever I remember to have done in my life, and, as I reckoned, above nine hours; for when I awaked, it was just daylight. I attempted to rise, but was not able to stir: for as I happened to lie on my back, I found my arms and legs were strongly fastened on each side to the ground."
The famous awakening scene — Gulliver discovering himself bound by the tiny Lilliputians. 
**Deckoviz:** A giant figure bound by tiny ropes on a green field.

### 57. Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Lewis Carroll (1865) Opening
"Alice was beginning to get very tired of sitting by her sister on the bank, and of having nothing to do: once or twice she had peeped into the book her sister was reading, but it had no pictures or conversations in it, 'and what is the use of a book,' thought Alice, 'without pictures or conversations?'"
The opening that leads, within paragraphs, to the rabbit hole.

### 58. Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Lewis Carroll (1865) The Cheshire Cat
"'Well! I've often seen a cat without a grin,' thought Alice; 'but a grin without a cat! It's the most curious thing I ever saw in my life!'"
One of the most quoted single images in children's literature.

### 59. Through the Looking-Glass — Lewis Carroll (1871) The Walrus and the Carpenter

The opening of the nonsense poem recited by Tweedledee.

### 60. Emma — Jane Austen (1815) Opening
"Emma Woodhouse, handsome, clever, and rich, with a comfortable home and happy disposition, seemed to unite some of the blessings of existence; and had lived nearly twenty-one years in the world with very little to distress or vex her."
The opening that sets up everything Emma will need to learn.

### 61. Persuasion — Jane Austen (1817) Closing
"...the dread of a future war all that could dim her sunshine. She gloried in being a sailor's wife, but she must pay the tax of quick alarm for belonging to that profession which is, if possible, more distinguished in its domestic virtues than in its national importance."
Austen's last completed novel ends with a clear-eyed acknowledgment that happiness coexists with anxiety.

### 62. Sense and Sensibility — Jane Austen (1811) Closing
"...and among the merits and the happiness of Elinor and Marianne, let it not be ranked as the least considerable, that though sisters, and living almost within sight of each other, they could live without disagreement between themselves, or producing coolness between their husbands."
Austen's dry final wit — closing a novel about excessive feeling with a joke about how rarely sisters actually get along.

### 63. North and South — Elizabeth Gaskell (1855) Closing
"'Mother!' said Mr. Thornton, in a voice of tender reproach, 'you don't know how I love her!'"
The closing scene of Gaskell's industrial novel.

### 64. North and South — Elizabeth Gaskell (1855) The Strike
"He did not speak. He thought speech would be of no use. He folded his arms and stood looking out of the window, as if perfectly indifferent to what was going on; but in reality fiercely stirred up by the contest going on in his own heart."
A passage capturing the novel's central tension.

### 65. Bleak House — Charles Dickens (1852–53) Opening
"London. Michaelmas term lately over, and the Lord Chancellor sitting in Lincoln's Inn Hall. Implacable November weather. The famous fog-bound opening of Bleak House.

### 66. David Copperfield — Charles Dickens (1850) Opening
"Whether I shall turn out to be the hero of my own life, or whether that station will be held by anybody else, these pages must show."
Dickens's most personal novel opens with a question about narrative authority itself.

### 67. Oliver Twist — Charles Dickens (1837–39) "Please, sir, I want some more"
"Child as he was, he was desperate with hunger and reckless with misery. He rose from the table; and advancing to the master, basin and spoon in hand, said, somewhat alarmed at his own temerity—'Please, sir, I want some more.'"
One of the most famous single requests in English fiction.

### 68. The Pickwick Papers — Charles Dickens (1836–37) Opening
"The first ray of light which illumines the gloom, and converts into a dazzling brilliancy that obscurity in which the earlier history of the public career of the immortal Pickwick would appear to be involved, is derived from the perusal of the following entry in the Transactions of the Pickwick Club."

### 69. The Mill on the Floss — George Eliot (1860) Closing
"Nature repairs her ravages—but not all. The uptorn trees are not rooted again; the parted hills are left scarred... To the eyes that have dwelt on the past, there is no thorough repair."
Eliot's closing meditation after the novel's catastrophic flood.

### 70. Adam Bede — George Eliot (1859) Opening
"With a single drop of ink for a mirror, the Egyptian sorcerer undertakes to reveal to any chance comer far-reaching visions of the past. This is what I undertake to do for you, reader."
Eliot's opening conceit — the novel as a drop of ink that becomes a mirror onto an entire vanished world. 
**Deckoviz:** A single ink drop on white paper, rippling outward into a landscape.

### 71. Far from the Madding Crowd — Thomas Hardy (1874) Closing
"'I should hardly think you would remember it, Gabriel,' she murmured. ... 'That, however, is a matter I have not yet learnt to do without your help,' she said."
The quiet, unsentimental final exchange between Bathsheba and Gabriel.

### 72. Jude the Obscure — Thomas Hardy (1895) Closing
"'Done because we are too menny.'"
The note left by Little Father Time — six words, including a single misspelling, that constitute one of the most shocking moments in Victorian fiction.

### 73. Silas Marner — George Eliot (1861) Closing
"'O father,' said Eppie, 'what a pretty home ours is! I think nobody could be happier than we are.'"
The novel's gentle closing line.

### 74. The Hound of the Baskervilles — Arthur Conan Doyle (1902) Opening

### 75. The War of the Worlds — H.G. Wells (1898) Opening

### 76. A Study in Scarlet — Arthur Conan Doyle (1887) Opening

### 77. The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde — Robert Louis Stevenson (1886) Opening

The novel's opening character sketch.

### 78. Daniel Deronda — George Eliot (1876) Opening

### 79. Wuthering Heights — Emily Brontë (1847) Closing

### 80. Jane Eyre — Charlotte Brontë (1847) Closing

Five words constituting one of the most famous and most direct sentences in English fiction.

## Part Two: Modern Works (Described, with Short Quotation)

### 81. Ulysses — James Joyce (1922) Opening
The novel opens with Buck Mulligan emerging onto the tower roof, "stately, plump," carrying a bowl of lather — a mock-heroic entrance that immediately establishes the novel's blend of the epic and the everyday. 
**Deckoviz:** The Martello tower at dawn over Dublin Bay.

### 82. Mrs Dalloway — Virginia Woolf (1925) Opening
The novel begins with Clarissa Dalloway stepping out into a June morning in London, the famous opening establishing her resolve to buy flowers herself — the small domestic decision that launches a day-long meditation on time, memory, and mortality. 
**Deckoviz:** A London flower stall in morning light.

### 83. The Sound and the Fury — William Faulkner (1929) Opening
The novel opens inside Benjy Compson's consciousness — a sequence of fragmented sensory impressions, golfers seen through a fence, with no conventional narrative signposting at all, immersing the reader directly in a mind that experiences time non-sequentially. 
**Deckoviz:** A fence-line view of a golf course at dusk, slightly distorted.

### 84. As I Lay Dying — William Faulkner (1930) Closing
The novel ends with the Bundren family's journey to bury Addie complete, and Faulkner's closing chapters deliver one of fiction's most quietly devastating final images of a family moving on, almost indifferently, from grief. 
**Deckoviz:** A wagon on a dusty Southern road at dusk.

### 85. Brave New World — Aldous Huxley (1932) Closing
The novel ends with Bernard Marx's exile and the Savage's final, devastating act of despair — a closing that turns the novel's satirical comedy into something genuinely tragic, the cost of a world that has eliminated suffering by eliminating meaning. 
**Deckoviz:** A lighthouse on a barren coastline.

### 86. Of Mice and Men — John Steinbeck (1937) Closing
The novella ends on the riverbank where it began, with George and Lennie's shared dream of a small farm — "a little house, an' a couple of acres" — dissolving into the novel's most heartbreaking final act. 
**Deckoviz:** A small farmhouse silhouette against golden California hills.

### 87. The Grapes of Wrath — John Steinbeck (1939) Closing
The novel ends with Rose of Sharon and Ma Joad in a barn during a flood, in an image of quiet human solidarity and continuance that closes the Joad family's journey on a note of resilience rather than despair. 
**Deckoviz:** A barn interior during rain, warm light inside.

### 88. The Hobbit — J.R.R. Tolkien (1937) Opening
The novel's famous opening line establishes Bilbo Baggins's comfortable hobbit-hole before the adventure that will transform him — one of the most recognisable openings in fantasy literature, instantly evoking an entire cosy, green world. 
**Deckoviz:** A round green door set into a hillside, golden afternoon light.

### 89. The Lord of the Rings — J.R.R. Tolkien (1954–55) Closing
The trilogy ends with Sam Gamgee returning to Bag End, sitting down to dinner with his family, and speaking the novel's famous final line — "Well, I'm back," — closing an epic of cosmic stakes with the smallest possible domestic gesture. 
**Deckoviz:** A lit cottage window at dusk after a long journey.

### 90. Nineteen Eighty-Four — George Orwell (1949) Opening
The novel's opening line establishes the wrongness of its world through a single, quiet detail — "the clocks were striking thirteen" — before any of the regime's machinery is described, time itself already corrupted. 
**Deckoviz:** A clock face showing an impossible hour against a grey concrete skyline.

### 91. The Catcher in the Rye — J.D. Salinger (1951) Opening
Holden Caulfield's narration begins by refusing the conventions of autobiography altogether — dismissing his "lousy childhood" and any interest in his family history, establishing his voice of alienated, defensive honesty from the first sentence. 
**Deckoviz:** A lone red hunting cap against a grey New York skyline.

### 92. Lolita — Vladimir Nabokov (1955) Opening
The novel's opening paragraph is built around the repeated, incantatory sound of its title character's name — "light of my life, fire of my loins" — prose of extraordinary musicality in service of a deeply troubling narrator, establishing the novel's central tension between beauty of language and horror of subject from the very first lines. 
**Deckoviz:** The novel's American road-trip visual palette (sun-bleached motels, highway at dusk) as inspiration for art.

### 93. One Hundred Years of Solitude — Gabriel García Márquez (1967) Opening
The novel's celebrated opening sentence moves across decades in a single breath — a man facing execution remembering, years earlier, his first encounter with ice — establishing the book's signature collapse of linear time and its blend of the mythic and the mundane. 
**Deckoviz:** A block of ice in tropical heat, surrounded by a crowd.

### 94. Beloved — Toni Morrison (1987) Opening
The novel opens with the single word "124" — the address of the haunted house — described as "spiteful," establishing from the first sentence that the house itself is a character, alive with the weight of what happened there. 
**Deckoviz:** A weathered house number on a door, slightly askew, in cold blue light.

### 95. The Road — Cormac McCarthy (2006) Closing
The novel's final paragraph turns away from the father and son entirely to describe trout in a mountain stream before the world ended — a closing image of a lost natural order, rendered in prose of unexpected lyrical tenderness after the book's relentless bleakness. 
**Deckoviz:** Trout in a clear mountain stream, sunlight through water.

### 96. The Kite Runner — Khaled Hosseini (2003) Closing
The novel's final scene takes place at a park in California, with Amir running a kite for Hassan's son — the closing image returning to and redeeming the kite-flying of the novel's opening, decades and continents away. 
**Deckoviz:** A kite against a clear sky, seen from below.

### 97. Life of Pi — Yann Martel (2001) Closing
The novel ends with Pi's quiet question to the investigators — which of his two stories about his survival they prefer — leaving the reader to choose between the literal and the mythic, a closing that turns the entire novel into a meditation on the nature of storytelling itself. 
**Deckoviz:** A lifeboat on calm water at dawn, two versions of the same scene overlaid faintly.

### 98. The Book Thief — Markus Zusak (2005) Opening
The novel is narrated by Death, who introduces himself and his subject — a young book thief in Nazi Germany — with a tone that is unexpectedly gentle, even tender, establishing the novel's distinctive voice from its first pages. 
**Deckoviz:** Falling snow over a small German town, seen from above.

### 99. The Alchemist — Paulo Coelho (1988) Closing
The novel ends with Santiago returning to the ruined church where his journey began, and discovering — in the last lines — that what he sought was there from the start, closing the circle the novel opened. 
**Deckoviz:** A ruined Andalusian church at dawn, treasure half-buried beneath sycamore roots.

### 100. The Little Prince — Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (1943) Closing
The narrator's final words describe the desert where he met the Little Prince and ask the reader, if they ever pass that way, to look up and remember — a closing of extraordinary tenderness that turns the entire book into a kind of letter to anyone who reads it. 
**Deckoviz:** A single star over an empty desert, a small golden-haired figure barely visible on a dune.

## Outro
One hundred passages. Some of them you could recite from memory before you ever read this list. Some of them you may be encountering for the first time. All of them are moments where language stopped being merely functional and became something closer to a held breath — the sentence that a reader returns to, years later, and finds exactly as powerful as the first time.

What is striking, gathering these together, is how often the most enduring passages are the simplest. Five words on a tombstone. A single footprint in sand. "Reader, I married him." The grandest novels often distil themselves, in memory, down to a handful of words — and it is usually those words, rather than the surrounding hundreds of pages, that end up living in a reader's mind permanently.

That is precisely what makes literature suited to a frame in your home. A passage you love is not something you need to finish — you have already finished it, possibly many times. What you want now is to live alongside it: to have it catch your eye on an ordinary Tuesday morning and feel, again, that small shock of recognition.
