## Introduction
History is not a list of dates. It is a sequence of ruptures — moments when the world was one thing before and something irrevocably different after. Most of human time passes without such moments: seasons change, generations are born and die, the ordinary work of life continues. And then something happens that redraws every map, physical and conceptual, that human beings have made.
This guide gathers 100 of those moments — the events, discoveries, revolutions, and turning points that made the world as we know it. Some are military and political. Some are scientific and technological. Some are philosophical and spiritual. Some are artistic. Some are catastrophic and some are triumphant, and some are so large that they contain both catastrophe and triumph simultaneously, inseparable from each other.
A word about selection. The 100 greatest events in human history is not a neutral list. Every such selection reflects a set of values — about what constitutes significance, about which cultures and which centuries deserve emphasis, about whether the invention of a writing system matters more or less than the fall of an empire. We have tried to be as global and as long-ranging as the record allows, reaching back to the emergence of our species and forward to the technologies transforming the present moment. We have given weight to events that changed the conditions of life for the largest number of people, to ideas that shifted how human beings understand themselves, and to moments of creation and destruction so large that their effects are still unfolding.
The result is not a ranking so much as a portrait — of the species that made fire, wrote poems, built cities, waged wars, crossed oceans, abolished slavery (in some places), split atoms, and walked on the moon. A species that has caused enormous suffering and produced enormous beauty, often simultaneously. A species still, always, in the middle of its own story.

## The 100 Events

### 1. The Emergence of Homo Sapiens (c. 300,000 BCE)
Modern humans evolved in Africa, inheriting a lineage of hominids stretching back millions of years. Around 300,000 years ago, the fossil record shows the emergence of Homo sapiens — anatomically modern humans with larger brains, flatter faces, and the physical capacity for the complex language that would change everything. It is the event from which every other event in this guide follows.
What made us different was not muscle or size but cognition — the ability to think in abstractions, to plan across time, to cooperate with strangers through shared belief, to accumulate and transmit knowledge across generations. We are the only animal that can do all of these things simultaneously, and it is this combination that has made us capable of everything in this list, for better and for worse.
**On Deckoviz:** The African savanna at dawn — the world before history. Human silhouette against an ancient landscape. The emergence concept as abstract art. 'We are the only animal that knows it will die' as typographic poster art.

### 2. The Cognitive Revolution (c. 70,000 BCE)
Something happened around 70,000 years ago — possibly a genetic mutation affecting language, possibly a sufficient density of population, possibly both — that dramatically accelerated human cognitive capacity and cultural complexity. Cave paintings, musical instruments, jewellery, religious artefacts: suddenly the archaeological record shows a species making meaning, not just surviving.
Yuval Noah Harari called this the Cognitive Revolution — the point at which humans began living not just in physical reality but in shared imaginative reality, cooperating in vast numbers through common myths, religions, and stories. Every human institution from then to now — every religion, every nation, every currency, every company — exists because of this capacity.
**On Deckoviz:** Cave painting art in ochre and charcoal. The handprint — the oldest self-portrait in human history. Fire in darkness. The Cognitive Revolution concept as poster art.

### 3. The Cave Paintings of Lascaux and Altamira (c. 17,000 BCE)
Deep in caves in southern France and northern Spain, our ancestors painted — horses, bison, deer, aurochs, the occasional human hand — with a skill and grace that still astonishes us. These are not primitive drawings. They are the work of artists with complete mastery of form, perspective, and the use of the cave's natural contours to create three-dimensional figures that seem to move in firelight.
They are the oldest art in the world. They are also evidence that the drive to make images — to represent the world and one's experience of it — is as ancient and as fundamental as any other human impulse.
**On Deckoviz:** Cave painting art on your frame — the world's first art on the world's most contemporary display technology. Bison and horse in ochre on dark stone. The handprint series. Firelit prehistoric world moodscape.

### 4. The Agricultural Revolution (c. 10,000 BCE)
Humans stopped moving and started farming. In multiple locations independently — the Fertile Crescent, China, Mesoamerica, sub-Saharan Africa — wild plants were domesticated, animals were herded, and settlements became permanent. The village became the city. Surplus became possible. Specialisation became possible. Writing, law, religion, art, and war at scale all followed.
The Agricultural Revolution is the most consequential change in the conditions of human life ever made. It was also, arguably, one of the worst decisions in terms of individual human wellbeing — studies of skeletal remains show that early farmers were shorter, sicker, and worked harder than the hunter-gatherers they replaced. But it set in motion everything that followed.
**On Deckoviz:** The wheat field as the origin of civilisation concept art. The transition from movement to settlement visual. Ancient Fertile Crescent landscape. The first village at dawn.

### 5. The Invention of Writing (c. 3200 BCE)
In Sumer, in Mesopotamia, clay tablets were pressed with a stylus to record grain inventories. The first use of writing was administrative — a tool for tracking who owed what to whom. From this entirely practical origin, humans developed the capacity to externalise memory, to transmit knowledge across time, to write laws, poetry, philosophy, and history.
Without writing, there is no accumulation of knowledge across generations. Without accumulation of knowledge across generations, there is no science, no philosophy, no recorded history. Writing is the technology that makes everything else in this list possible. The moment a scribe pressed a reed into wet clay in ancient Sumer and made a mark that meant something, the modern world began.
**On Deckoviz:** Cuneiform tablet art — the first writing as visual art. The clay tablet as a concept. Ancient script typography. 'In the beginning was the word' quote art in ancient script aesthetic.

### 6. The Building of the Pyramids (c. 2560 BCE)
The Great Pyramid of Khufu at Giza is the only surviving member of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. Built over twenty years by tens of thousands of workers, with a precision of alignment and construction that continues to astonish engineers and architects, it is simultaneously a monument to human organisational capacity, a tomb, a cosmological statement, and the most dramatic demonstration in history of what becomes possible when a large society directs its surplus toward a single purpose.
For 3,800 years it was the tallest structure on earth.
**On Deckoviz:** The pyramid at dawn against the desert sky. The Sphinx at night. Ancient Egypt in gold and ochre. The pyramid as a geometric concept poster. Hieroglyphic typography art.

### 7. The Bronze Age (c. 3300–1200 BCE)
The discovery that copper and tin, combined, produce a metal harder and more versatile than either — this was the first great materials revolution. Bronze tools, bronze weapons, bronze ships' fittings: the Bronze Age enabled the first true urban civilisations and the first large-scale warfare. Trade networks spanning thousands of miles developed to move the raw materials. Writing, law, and monumental architecture all flourish in the Bronze Age.
**On Deckoviz:** Bronze Age warrior art. Ancient trade network map concept. Metalwork and fire as visual concept. The sword as symbol of the Bronze Age.

### 8. The Invention of the Wheel (c. 3500 BCE)
First used not for transport but for pottery — the wheel's rotational principle appearing in the potter's wheel before the cart wheel. The wheeled vehicle appears slightly later, revolutionising transport, trade, and warfare. With the wheel comes the axle, the gear, and eventually every machine that rotates — which is most machines.
**On Deckoviz:** The wheel as the origin of all mechanical motion — concept poster art. The potter's wheel. Ancient trade cart on a dusty road. Motion concept art.

### 9. The Greek Golden Age (c. 480–323 BCE)
In a relatively small city-state of perhaps 300,000 people, over approximately 150 years, something happened that has never quite been replicated. Democracy (however limited). Philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle). Drama (Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes). History (Herodotus, Thucydides). Mathematics (Euclid, Pythagoras). Architecture (the Parthenon). Medicine (Hippocrates).
The Greek Golden Age invented the intellectual framework within which most subsequent Western thought has operated. We are still, in many ways, living inside a structure that fifth-century Athenians built.
**On Deckoviz:** The Parthenon at dawn. Greek philosophical dialogue concept art. The Theatre of Dionysus. 'The unexamined life' quote art. Classical Greek sculpture portrait. Athens acropolis silhouette.

### 10. The Life and Death of the Buddha (c. 563–483 BCE)
Siddhartha Gautama, born a prince in what is now Nepal, renounced his wealth and position to seek an answer to the problem of human suffering. His enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, his first sermon at Deer Park, and the spread of his teaching across Asia constitute one of the great spiritual and philosophical events in human history.
Buddhism changed the conditions of life for hundreds of millions of people — offering a sophisticated and psychologically acute account of suffering and its causes, a method for liberation, and an ethical framework of extraordinary compassion. It remains, 2,500 years later, one of the world's great living traditions.
**On Deckoviz:** The Bodhi tree in golden light. The seated Buddha in meditation. Deer Park first sermon concept art. Buddhist mandala and lotus art. 'The mind is everything' quote art.

### 11. The Life and Teaching of Confucius (551–479 BCE)
Confucius — Kong Qiu — was a Chinese philosopher whose teachings on social harmony, filial piety, ritual, and ethical conduct shaped Chinese civilisation more profoundly than any other single individual. The Analects, compiled by his disciples, contain his conversations and teachings. For over two millennia, Confucian thought was the philosophical foundation of Chinese governance, education, and family life.
**On Deckoviz:** Chinese ink painting of a scholar in contemplation. The Analects as typographic art. Chinese classical garden and pavilion art. 'Choose a job you love' and other Confucius quote art.

### 12. The Persian Wars (499–449 BCE)
The Greek city-states, massively outnumbered, defeated the Persian Empire at Marathon (490 BCE), Thermopylae and Salamis (480 BCE), and Plataea (479 BCE). The consequences of a Persian victory — the absorption of the Greek world into the Persian Empire — are incalculable. The Athens that produced Socrates, Plato, drama, and democracy might never have developed as it did.
Thermopylae, where 300 Spartans held the pass against the Persian army for three days, became one of history's defining stories of sacrifice and courage.
**On Deckoviz:** The Battle of Marathon as classical art. Thermopylae — the 300 Spartans — as warrior concept art. The naval battle of Salamis. 'Tonight we dine in hell' quote concept art.

### 13. The Conquests of Alexander the Great (336–323 BCE)
By the age of 32, Alexander of Macedon had conquered Greece, Egypt, Persia, Central Asia, and reached the borders of India — creating the largest empire in the ancient world and, in doing so, spreading Greek language, art, philosophy, and culture across an enormous swath of the world. Hellenism — the fusion of Greek and local cultures — transformed the ancient world from the Mediterranean to the Hindu Kush.
**On Deckoviz:** Alexander in battle — the Alexander Mosaic aesthetic. The march to the East concept art. Hellenistic portrait art. 'There is nothing impossible to him who will try' quote art. The route of Alexander's campaign as map art.

### 14. The Library of Alexandria (c. 300–30 BCE)
The greatest library of the ancient world — founded in Alexandria, Egypt, under the Ptolemies — attempted to collect every scroll written in every language. At its height it may have contained 700,000 scrolls. Scholars from across the Mediterranean world came to work there, and the library was the intellectual centre of the ancient world for centuries.
Its destruction — gradual, over centuries, by fire, war, and neglect — remains one of history's great losses. We do not know what we lost.
**On Deckoviz:** The great library as a visual concept — the most beautiful library in history. Scrolls and columns. The burning of knowledge as a devastating visual. 'What we do not know' quote art.

### 15. The Life, Death, and Resurrection of Jesus Christ (c. 4 BCE–30 CE)
Whatever one's theological position, the historical impact of this life is among the largest in human history. The religion that formed in its wake — Christianity — became the most widely practised in the world, shaping the history of Europe, the Americas, Africa, and Asia for two millennia. The calendar most of the world uses is dated from the estimated year of his birth.
**On Deckoviz:** The Nativity in classical art aesthetic. The Sermon on the Mount. The Crucifixion and Resurrection as sacred art concepts. Byzantine icon-style portraiture. 'Love one another' as typographic quote art.

### 16. The Fall of the Roman Empire (476 CE)
The Western Roman Empire did not fall in a single dramatic moment — it dissolved over decades, the last emperor deposed by a Germanic chieftain with barely a historical footnote. But the end of Roman political unity in the West marked the beginning of a new world: the medieval period, the rise of the Catholic Church as the dominant organising institution of European life, and the long, slow process of rebuilding the civilisation that Rome had been.
Gibbon spent six volumes diagnosing what happened. We are still arguing about it.
**On Deckoviz:** Roman ruins in golden light. The last emperor. The barbarian at the gate. Roman architectural beauty. 'All empires end' typographic concept art. The decline and fall as civilisational concept poster.

### 17. The Life and Teaching of Muhammad (570–632 CE)
The Prophet Muhammad received the revelations that became the Quran over 23 years and founded a religious community that would, within a century of his death, stretch from Spain to Central Asia. Islam — the world's second-largest religion, with nearly 2 billion adherents — transformed the political, cultural, and intellectual landscape of half the world. Islamic civilisation preserved and extended Greek philosophy, invented algebra, and produced art, architecture, and literature of extraordinary beauty.
**On Deckoviz:** Islamic geometric art as sacred concept. The Quran as typographic art. Mosque architecture — the great mosques of Mecca, Medina, Istanbul, Córdoba. Desert and revelation concept. Calligraphic art.

### 18. The Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE)
China's golden age — the most populous, most prosperous, and arguably most sophisticated empire on earth for nearly three centuries. The Tang dynasty produced poetry (Li Bai, Du Fu), painting, ceramics, and music of extraordinary refinement, and the Silk Road under Tang expansion connected China to the Islamic world, Byzantium, and India in the most globally connected trading network the ancient world produced.
**On Deckoviz:** Tang dynasty painting aesthetics. Silk Road map art. Chinese imperial city. Li Bai's moonlit poetry as visual concept. Tang ceramic art.

### 19. The Islamic Golden Age (c. 786–1258 CE)
While Europe was in its early medieval period, the Islamic world — centred in Baghdad's House of Wisdom — was the intellectual capital of the earth. Muslim scholars translated, preserved, and extended Greek philosophy, invented algebra (al-Khawarizmi), made fundamental advances in astronomy, optics (Ibn al-Haytham), medicine (Ibn Sina / Avicenna), and philosophy (Ibn Rushd / Averroes). The knowledge they preserved and extended eventually flowed back to Europe and made the Renaissance possible.
**On Deckoviz:** Baghdad's House of Wisdom as architectural concept art. Astrolabe and astronomical instrument art. Islamic geometric tile art. 'Seek knowledge, even as far as China' quote art. Medieval Islamic library.

### 20. The Norman Conquest of England (1066)
William the Conqueror's victory at Hastings on 14 October 1066 transformed England permanently — introducing Norman French as the language of the ruling class, reshaping English law, architecture, and culture, and making England part of a European network of power rather than a Scandinavian periphery. Modern English is the hybrid language that resulted from the collision of Anglo-Saxon and Norman French over the following centuries.
**On Deckoviz:** The Bayeux Tapestry — one of the great works of medieval art — as visual concept. Norman castle and cathedral art. The Battle of Hastings as historical composition.

### 21. The Crusades (1095–1291)
Two centuries of military expeditions from Christian Europe to the Holy Land — launched, ultimately failed, and leaving a complex, bloody, and still-contested legacy. The Crusades caused enormous suffering. They also intensified cultural contact between the Islamic world and Europe, bringing back mathematical, philosophical, and medical knowledge that transformed European intellectual life. They are one of history's most morally complex chapters.
**On Deckoviz:** Crusader castle in desert landscape. The medieval knight. Jerusalem as a city of three faiths concept art. Historical map of the Crusade routes.

### 22. The Magna Carta (1215)
King John of England, forced by his barons to sign a charter limiting royal power at Runnymede, produced a document of 63 clauses, most of which concerned the specific grievances of 13th-century English nobles. Its importance was retrospective and grew over centuries — Magna Carta became the founding precedent for constitutional government, the rule of law, and the principle that even sovereigns are subject to law.
**On Deckoviz:** The Magna Carta document as typographic art. Runnymede in summer light. The charter seal. 'No man shall be imprisoned without lawful judgment' as typographic concept. Rule of law concept art.

### 23. Genghis Khan and the Mongol Empire (1206–1368)
From the steppes of Central Asia, a confederation of nomadic tribes under Temüjin — who became Genghis Khan — created the largest contiguous land empire in human history. From Korea to Poland, from Siberia to Persia, the Mongol Empire connected the world in ways not seen before — the Pax Mongolica enabled trade, communication, and cultural exchange across Eurasia. It also caused catastrophic destruction: the siege of Baghdad in 1258, which ended the Islamic Golden Age, was among the most devastating events in medieval history.
**On Deckoviz:** The Mongol steppe and horseman silhouette. The silk road under Mongol protection as map art. The siege of Baghdad. The scale of the Mongol Empire as concept art.

### 24. The Black Death (1347–1353)
The bubonic plague killed between 30 and 60 percent of Europe's population — perhaps 50 million people — in six years. It transformed European society at every level: labour became scarce and valuable, which undermined feudalism; the authority of the Church was questioned when prayer failed to halt the dying; art became preoccupied with mortality in ways that fed into the Renaissance. The plague changed who Europe was and who it would become.
**On Deckoviz:** The Dance of Death — the medieval artistic tradition of danse macabre — as visual art. The Black Death as civilisational rupture concept. Mortality and renewal visual concept. 'Remember you must die' as typographic art.

### 25. The Renaissance (c. 1300–1600)
A rebirth of classical learning, humanist philosophy, and artistic ambition that transformed Italy first and then Europe. The Renaissance produced Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael, Botticelli, Dante, Petrarch, Machiavelli, and Erasmus. It invented the individual as the central value of Western culture. It recovered and extended Greek and Roman knowledge. It made beauty a moral and civic value. It created the visual language that Western art has drawn on ever since.
**On Deckoviz:** Renaissance portrait art of extraordinary beauty. The Vitruvian Man. Michelangelo's Sistine ceiling. The Renaissance city — Florence, Rome, Venice. 'Art is never finished, only abandoned' Leonardo quote art.

### 26. Johannes Gutenberg and the Printing Press (c. 1440)
Before Gutenberg, a Bible took a monk years to copy by hand. After Gutenberg, 500 copies could be printed in the time it took to copy one. The printing press democratised knowledge, made the Reformation possible (Luther's 95 Theses spread across Germany in weeks), created the conditions for the Scientific Revolution, and ultimately made mass literacy — and therefore modern democracy — possible.
It is the most transformative communications technology in human history until the internet.
**On Deckoviz:** The printing press as a visual concept. The Gutenberg Bible as typographic art. The spread of knowledge as a map. 'The press is the greatest weapon in the armoury of the modern commander' concept. Books as civilisation poster art.

### 27. Columbus and the European Discovery of the Americas (1492)
On 12 October 1492, Christopher Columbus landed in the Bahamas, believing he had reached Asia. What he had actually discovered — from the European perspective — was a hemisphere that European civilisation did not know existed. The consequences were both catastrophic and transformative: the destruction of indigenous civilisations, the transatlantic slave trade, and the creation of a new world order in which Europe became the dominant global power.
**On Deckoviz:** The Santa María on the open ocean. The meeting of worlds — Old and New — as visual concept. The 1492 map. The Americas before Columbus concept art. The complexity of discovery concept.

### 28. The Protestant Reformation (1517)
Martin Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the door of Wittenberg's Castle Church on 31 October 1517 — or so the legend goes; the evidence is disputed. What is not disputed is that Luther's challenge to the Catholic Church, enabled by the printing press, unleashed a religious revolution that split Western Christianity permanently, produced a century of religious warfare, and ultimately created the conditions for both religious liberty and the secular state.
**On Deckoviz:** Luther at the church door. The 95 Theses as typographic art. The Reformation as rupture concept. Wittenberg cathedral art. 'Here I stand, I can do no other' quote poster.

### 29. The Copernican Revolution (1543)
Nicolaus Copernicus published On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres in the year of his death, proposing that the earth revolved around the sun rather than vice versa. This heliocentric model was not simply an astronomical theory — it was a conceptual revolution. Earth was no longer the centre of the universe. Humanity was no longer at the cosmic centre. Everything that followed in science — Galileo, Kepler, Newton, Einstein — built on this displacement.
**On Deckoviz:** The heliocentric model — planets orbiting the sun — as geometric art. Copernicus's diagram. 'The earth is not the centre' as visual concept. The cosmos from a new perspective.

### 30. The Life and Work of Shakespeare (1564–1616)
William Shakespeare wrote 37 plays and 154 sonnets over roughly 25 years — and in doing so created the modern conception of the human being. His characters think in ways no fictional characters had thought before: contradicting themselves, surprising themselves, understanding themselves and failing to understand themselves simultaneously. He invented more than 1,700 words in common use today. He is the most translated and most performed writer in human history.
**On Deckoviz:** The Globe Theatre. Shakespeare portrait art. 'To be or not to be' as typographic art. Elizabethan theatre aesthetic. The complete works as a concept.

### 31. The Scientific Revolution (c. 1543–1687)
From Copernicus to Newton — the century and a half in which the method of empirical observation, mathematical description, and experimental verification replaced theological authority as the basis for understanding the natural world. Galileo, Kepler, Descartes, Harvey, Boyle, and Newton — the Scientific Revolution produced not just knowledge but the means of producing knowledge. It is the most important intellectual achievement in human history.
**On Deckoviz:** Newton and the apple as a concept poster. Galileo's telescope and the moons of Jupiter. The scientific method as visual concept. 'Hypotheses non fingo' — Newton on not speculating — quote art.

### 32. The Thirty Years' War and the Peace of Westphalia (1618–1648)
The most devastating European war before the 20th century — fought across the German states, killing a third of Germany's population and involving every major European power. The Peace of Westphalia that ended it established the principle of national sovereignty — that each state has authority within its own borders and other states should not interfere in its internal affairs. This remains the foundational principle of the modern international order.
**On Deckoviz:** The ruined German landscape of the Thirty Years War. The peace treaty document as typographic art. National sovereignty concept. European map in 1648.

### 33. The English Civil War and the Execution of Charles I (1649)
For the first time in modern European history, a king was tried and executed by his own subjects. Charles I was beheaded on 30 January 1649 — and the precedent it set was enormous. The sovereignty of the people, however contested, had been physically demonstrated. The Glorious Revolution of 1688 and the Bill of Rights that followed established constitutional monarchy and parliamentary government as the English model — a model that influenced the American and French revolutions.
**On Deckoviz:** The execution of a king as historical turning point concept. The Bill of Rights as typographic art. Parliamentary democracy concept. The crown and its limits.

### 34. Isaac Newton and the Principia Mathematica (1687)
Newton's Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica is the most important scientific book ever written. In it, Newton formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation — a unified mathematical account of how objects move and how gravity works, from falling apples to orbiting planets. He also invented calculus (simultaneously with Leibniz). The Newtonian worldview — a deterministic, mathematically governed universe — dominated science for 200 years.
**On Deckoviz:** Newton's apple. The orbit of the planets. The equation for universal gravitation as mathematical art. 'Standing on the shoulders of giants' quote poster. The Principia as a typographic concept.

### 35. The Atlantic Slave Trade (1500–1900)
Between 12 and 13 million Africans were forcibly transported across the Atlantic to the Americas in the largest forced migration in human history. Millions more died during the Middle Passage. The wealth generated by enslaved labour built the economies of Britain, France, Portugal, Spain, and the United States — and shaped every subsequent political, cultural, and economic development in the Atlantic world. Its consequences — in inequality, trauma, and unresolved historical injustice — are still present in the world today.
**On Deckoviz:** The Slave Ship — Turner's painting of a slave ship in a storm — as the most important painting about this history. The Middle Passage route as map concept. The abolition movement. 'Never forget' memorial concept art.

### 36. The Glorious Revolution and the Birth of Liberal Democracy (1688)
The peaceful deposition of James II and the accession of William and Mary in exchange for acceptance of the Bill of Rights established the constitutional framework for English governance that survives, in evolved form, today. The principle that government derives its authority from the consent of the governed — that there are limits to royal and state power — became the foundation of liberal political thought.
**On Deckoviz:** The Bill of Rights document as typographic art. Parliamentary debate concept. Constitutional monarchy as a visual concept.

### 37. The Enlightenment (c. 1685–1815)
The age of reason — in which European thinkers applied rational analysis to every domain of human life: politics, religion, economics, society, science. Locke, Voltaire, Rousseau, Hume, Kant, Adam Smith — the Enlightenment produced the ideas that made the American and French revolutions and every subsequent liberal democracy possible. Its core conviction — that human reason, not tradition or religion, is the proper guide to the organisation of human life — is still the most consequential idea in modern political history.
**On Deckoviz:** The Enlightenment salon — philosophers around a candlelit table. Voltaire portrait. 'Dare to know' (Kant's 'Sapere Aude') as typographic art. The light of reason concept poster.

### 38. The American Revolution (1775–1783)
'We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal' — the Declaration of Independence articulated a political philosophy that became the template for democratic revolutions across the world. The American experiment was not perfect (the men who wrote 'all men are created equal' many of them owned slaves), but it demonstrated that a modern republic, governed by elected representatives and protected by a written constitution, was possible.
**On Deckoviz:** The Declaration of Independence as typographic art. The Revolutionary War as historical composition. The Founding Fathers at Independence Hall. 'We hold these truths' quote poster. The American flag as a graphic concept.

### 39. The French Revolution (1789–1799)
Liberté, égalité, fraternité — and the guillotine. The French Revolution destroyed the Ancien Régime and attempted to rebuild French society from its foundations on Enlightenment principles. It produced constitutional government, the Declaration of the Rights of Man, and the metric system — and also the Terror, the guillotining of thousands, and eventually Napoleon. It was simultaneously the most idealistic and the most violent political experiment of the 18th century.
**On Deckoviz:** The storming of the Bastille — one of history's most iconic images. The guillotine. Marianne as the embodiment of the Republic. 'Liberty, Equality, Fraternity' typographic art. The tricolore.

### 40. Napoleon Bonaparte (1799–1815)
Napoleon Bonaparte seized power in France at 30 and by 40 had remade the map of Europe. He codified French law (the Napoleonic Code, still the basis of law in France, Louisiana, and Quebec), exported Enlightenment ideas at the point of a sword, and demonstrated that a man of extraordinary individual ability could reshape history. He also caused the deaths of millions. The contradiction is irresolvable, and history has never agreed on how to weigh it.
**On Deckoviz:** Napoleon at the St. Bernard Pass — David's great equestrian portrait. The battle maps of Europe. The Napoleonic Code as typographic art. 'Impossible is a word found only in the dictionary of fools' quote art.

### 41. The Industrial Revolution (c. 1760–1840)
Steam power, the factory, the cotton gin, the railway — the transformation of an agricultural world into an industrial one, beginning in Britain and spreading across the world over the following century. The Industrial Revolution produced unprecedented wealth, unprecedented inequality, the modern city, the working class, child labour, environmental destruction, and the technological civilisation we inhabit today.
**On Deckoviz:** The steam engine as a visual concept. The factory at night. The railway as a symbol of industrial progress. Industrial-era engravings as poster art. 'Dark satanic mills' — Blake's phrase — as typographic concept.

### 42. The Abolition of the Slave Trade (1807) and Slavery (Various, 1833–1865)
The British abolition of the slave trade in 1807 — fought for over decades by abolitionists including Olaudah Equiano, William Wilberforce, and Granville Sharp — was the first major reversal of an enormously profitable global institution through moral argument. The abolition of slavery itself in the British Empire followed in 1833, in the United States after the Civil War in 1865.
It is one of history's rare examples of a moral revolution — a practice accepted across millennia dismantled within a century through the force of argument and political organisation.
**On Deckoviz:** The broken chain as a symbol of abolition. Frederick Douglass portrait. 'Am I not a man and a brother?' — the Wedgwood medallion. The Emancipation Proclamation. Wilberforce typographic portrait.

### 43. The Publication of Darwin's On the Origin of Species (1859)
The most important book in the history of science. Darwin's theory of natural selection provided a complete, coherent, and empirically verifiable account of how the diversity of life on earth arose — without divine creation. It displaced humanity from its special position in the natural order and provided the foundation for the modern biological sciences. Every subsequent advance in genetics, ecology, and medicine builds on it.
**On Deckoviz:** The tree of life as visual concept. Darwin's finches. The Beagle voyage map. 'There is grandeur in this view of life' quote art. Natural selection as a visual concept.

### 44. The American Civil War (1861–1865)
