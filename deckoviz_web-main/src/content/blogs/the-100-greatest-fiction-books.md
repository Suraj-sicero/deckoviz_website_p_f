## A DASPort Library

## Introduction
Every great novel is a room you walk into and don't fully leave. The furniture rearranges something in you, quietly, and you carry it out with you whether you meant to or not. That's the strange power of fiction: it's the one art form that builds entire worlds using nothing but sentences, and asks your imagination to do the construction work.

At Deckoviz, we think your walls should hold some of that. Not posters of book covers bought off a shelf, but living, generative art drawn from the worlds these books built. With Vizzy, you can turn the burnt orange skies of a dystopia, the candlelit drawing rooms of a Russian novel, or the quiet menace of a coastal town into ambient art that shifts your space the way the book itself shifted your mind.

This is our attempt at the 100 greatest works of fiction ever written. Not a definitive ranking (no such thing exists, and we'd be lying if we pretended otherwise), but a working library: books that changed how people think about themselves, about power, about love, about time. For each one, we've dug into the context that makes it worth reading, and we've sketched out the kind of Deckoviz visual that could bring its world into your home.

Treat this as a reading list and a frame list at once. Pick a book, read it, then ask Vizzy to paint its world onto your wall.

## The Great Library

### 1. Don Quixote — Miguel de Cervantes (1605)
Often called the first modern novel, written by a man who'd been a soldier, a slave, and a tax collector before he turned to fiction in his fifties. Its genius is that it's both a parody of chivalric romance and a deeply sincere meditation on idealism versus reality, a tension that still defines half of modern literature.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The windmills scene, rendered as a hazy, golden-hour Spanish plain, Quixote's lance tilted toward giants that are obviously, heartbreakingly, just windmills. Could work as a faded oil-painting style piece, slightly melancholic rather than comic.

### 2. War and Peace — Leo Tolstoy (1869)
Tolstoy's attempt to capture an entire society at a hinge moment in history, following five aristocratic families through the Napoleonic Wars. What makes it endure isn't the scale, it's the intimacy inside the scale: the way Tolstoy zooms from a battlefield to a single character's private doubt without losing either.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A snow-covered Russian estate at dusk, warm windows glowing against a vast cold landscape. Could also do the ballroom scene, candlelight and movement, as a contrast piece for the same household.

### 3. Pride and Prejudice — Jane Austen (1813)
A comedy of manners that's secretly an economic novel: every conversation about love is also a conversation about money, status, and the very narrow set of moves available to women in Regency England. Austen's wit is so precise it still reads as contemporary.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The drawing room at Pemberley, all soft natural light through tall windows, understated elegance. A second option: a rain-soaked garden scene for the Darcy proposal, moody and romantic without being saccharine.

### 4. Moby-Dick — Herman Melville (1851)
A whaling adventure that becomes a meditation on obsession, fate, and the limits of human knowledge. Famously a commercial failure in its time, only recognized as a masterpiece decades after Melville's death. Ahab's hunt for the white whale is one of literature's great studies of monomania.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The Pequod against a churning grey-green sea, Moby Dick's white bulk barely visible beneath the surface. A more abstract option: just the sea itself, vast and indifferent, painted in cold blues and whites.

### 5. Crime and Punishment — Fyodor Dostoevsky (1866)
A murder story that's really a psychological excavation. Raskolnikov's theory that exceptional people are above conventional morality gets tested against the crushing weight of guilt, and Dostoevsky uses the novel to argue, brutally, that conscience doesn't care about your theories.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A cramped St. Petersburg attic room, narrow shaft of light, oppressive and claustrophobic. Alternatively, the bridge over the canal at night, fog and gaslight, a single figure walking.

### 6. One Hundred Years of Solitude — Gabriel García Márquez (1967)
The book that brought magical realism to the world stage, following the Buendía family across seven generations in the fictional town of Macondo. Time loops, ghosts walk through rooms, and a plague of insomnia spreads through town, all narrated with total matter-of-factness.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Macondo itself, a lush, slightly surreal jungle town with yellow butterflies drifting through the air (a direct nod to Mauricio Babilonia's storyline). Rich tropical colors, dreamlike but grounded.

### 7. Frankenstein — Mary Shelley (1818)
Written when Shelley was eighteen, this is often cited as the first true science fiction novel. It's less a horror story about a monster than a tragedy about a creator who abandons his creation, and the loneliness that follows for both of them.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The Arctic ice fields where the novel opens and closes, vast white desolation under a pale sky, two tiny figures in the distance. A second option: the laboratory itself, candlelit and cluttered, more Gothic than gory.

### 8. The Great Gatsby — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925)
A short, glittering novel about the hollowness underneath the American dream, set against the backdrop of Jazz Age excess. The green light across the bay has become one of literature's most enduring symbols of longing for something just out of reach.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The green light itself, viewed across dark water from Gatsby's dock, Art Deco color palette, moody and atmospheric. Alternatively, one of Gatsby's parties in full swing, gold and champagne tones, slightly blurred like a memory.

### 9. Anna Karenina — Leo Tolstoy (1877)
Opens with one of the most famous lines in fiction about happy families all resembling one another, then spends 800 pages proving how varied and specific unhappiness actually is. Anna's story runs parallel to Levin's quieter, more redemptive arc, and the contrast is the whole point.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A train platform in winter, steam and snow, a single figure standing apart from the crowd. For the Levin storyline, a wide shot of Russian farmland at harvest, warm and grounded, the visual opposite of the train scene.

### 10. The Catcher in the Rye — J.D. Salinger (1951)
Holden Caulfield's wandering through New York over a few days became the template for the modern voice of adolescent alienation. What's often missed is how much grief sits underneath the cynicism, the whole book is shadowed by his brother Allie's death.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A snowy New York street at night, neon signs blurred through falling snow, a lone figure in a red hunting cap. Could also do the carousel scene from the end, warmer tones, a rare moment of peace in the book.

### 11. Ulysses — James Joyce (1922)
A single day in Dublin, June 16, 1904, mapped onto the structure of Homer's Odyssey. Joyce's stream-of-consciousness technique was revolutionary, rendering thought itself as messy, associative, and unfiltered as it actually feels from the inside.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Dublin streets at dusk, warm gaslight on wet cobblestones, slightly impressionistic and blurred, as if seen through a wandering mind rather than a steady eye.

### 12. Mrs Dalloway — Virginia Woolf (1925)
Another single-day novel, this one following Clarissa Dalloway through London as she prepares for a party, while a shell-shocked war veteran's parallel day ends in tragedy. Woolf uses the structure to show how ordinary moments carry entire lifetimes of feeling underneath them.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A London townhouse garden in early summer light, flowers being arranged for a party, soft and painterly, with a faint sense of something unspoken in the air.

### 13. To the Lighthouse — Virginia Woolf (1927)
A meditation on time, memory, and loss, centered on a family's visits to a Scottish coastal house over the span of a decade. The lighthouse itself becomes a symbol of everything that's wanted but endlessly deferred.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A lighthouse across grey water, seen from a window with curtains moving in the breeze, muted coastal tones, quiet and contemplative.

### 14. The Sound and the Fury — William Faulkner (1929)
Four perspectives on the decline of a Southern aristocratic family, including one narrated by a man with severe cognitive impairment. Faulkner fractures time and voice so completely that the novel itself becomes a study in how memory distorts experience.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A decaying Southern plantation house, golden late-afternoon light through Spanish moss, beautiful but with an undertone of decline.

### 15. Beloved — Toni Morrison (1987)
A former slave is haunted, literally, by the daughter she killed to save from slavery. Morrison turns trauma into something almost physical, a presence that occupies rooms and shapes lives long after the event itself.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A modest rural house at the edge of a wood, warm interior light against deep blue evening, a single empty chair suggesting an unseen presence.

### 16. Invisible Man — Ralph Ellison (1952)
An unnamed Black narrator moves through mid-century America, encountering institutions and ideologies that all claim to see him while fundamentally failing to. The novel's central metaphor of invisibility remains one of the sharpest images in American literature.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A crowded city street rendered with one figure slightly translucent or out of focus among sharply defined others, urban and stark.

### 17. The Brothers Karamazov — Fyodor Dostoevsky (1880)
Dostoevsky's final and most ambitious novel, using a murder mystery within one family to stage a sprawling debate about faith, doubt, free will, and the existence of God. The "Grand Inquisitor" chapter alone has shaped a century of theological argument.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Russian monastery courtyard at dawn, golden domes against a pale sky, peaceful but with a sense of looming conflict just outside the frame.

### 18. Madame Bovary — Gustave Flaubert (1856)
The story of a provincial doctor's wife whose romantic fantasies, fed by novels, collide disastrously with the dullness of her actual life. Flaubert's precision with language was so exacting that the book's style became as influential as its content.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A French provincial bedroom, lace curtains and faded wallpaper, a woman seated by a window looking out at nothing in particular, soft and slightly melancholic.

### 19. Middlemarch — George Eliot (1871)
A panoramic novel of English provincial life, following multiple interlinked characters as they navigate marriage, ambition, and disappointment. Often cited as the greatest English novel for its psychological depth and its refusal to give anyone an easy ending.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A sunlit English country lane in autumn, hedgerows and distant church spire, warm and pastoral, the kind of view that hides quiet dramas behind every door.

### 20. Jane Eyre — Charlotte Brontë (1847)
A governess's first-person account of poverty, cruelty, love, and independence, radical for its time in giving a plain, poor woman a voice that demanded equal moral standing with anyone else. The "reader, I married him" line is one of literature's great quiet declarations.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Thornfield Hall at night, a single lit window in a dark stone facade, moody Gothic atmosphere with a hint of warmth inside.

### 21. Wuthering Heights — Emily Brontë (1847)
A brutal, obsessive love story set on the Yorkshire moors, structured as a story within a story and populated by characters who are almost uniformly cruel to each other. Its intensity was shocking to Victorian readers and remains startling today.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Windswept moorland under a stormy sky, a lone tree bent by wind, dramatic and almost violent in its weather, deep greens and greys.

### 22. The Picture of Dorian Gray — Oscar Wilde (1890)
A young man stays eternally beautiful while a portrait of him ages and corrupts in his place, absorbing the consequences of his moral decay. Wilde's only novel is also his sharpest meditation on vanity, hedonism, and the price of a life lived without consequence.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A gilded Victorian drawing room with a single covered portrait in the corner, opulent but with something unsettling implied just out of view.

### 23. Heart of Darkness — Joseph Conrad (1899)
A journey up the Congo River becomes a descent into the brutality of European colonialism and the darkness Conrad locates at the center of "civilized" man. Famously the basis for Apocalypse Now, the novella's atmosphere of dread has never quite dated.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A river disappearing into dense jungle under heavy mist, a small boat barely visible, deep greens fading to near-black at the edges.

### 24. Lord of the Flies — William Golding (1954)
A group of British schoolboys stranded on an island descend rapidly from order into savagery. Golding's bleak argument is that civilization is a thin veneer, and what's underneath emerges faster than anyone wants to admit.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A tropical beach at sunset, a fire burning further inland sending up smoke, beautiful surface and ominous undertone in the same frame.

### 25. Brave New World — Aldous Huxley (1932)
A dystopia built not on fear but on pleasure, where citizens are engineered and conditioned into contentment with their own oppression. Huxley's warning, that people might come to love their servitude, feels more relevant with every passing decade.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A sleek, sterile city of pastel towers under a flawless sky, slightly too perfect, with a single figure looking faintly out of place among the crowds.

### 26. Nineteen Eighty-Four — George Orwell (1949)
A totalitarian state controls reality itself through surveillance, propaganda, and the deliberate destruction of language and history. "Big Brother," "doublethink," and "thoughtcrime" have all passed into everyday vocabulary, a rare case of fiction directly shaping how we describe the world.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A grey, monolithic cityscape with a single oversized poster face watching over empty streets, muted tones with one stark red accent.

### 27. Animal Farm — George Orwell (1945)
A farmyard rebellion against a cruel farmer ends with the animals living under a new tyranny, indistinguishable from the old one but for the faces in charge. A short fable that doubles as one of the most precise allegories of revolution ever written.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A farmyard at dusk, animals silhouetted against a barn with a painted slogan on its side, warm rural colors with an uneasy edge.

### 28. The Trial — Franz Kafka (1925)
A man is arrested, prosecuted, and condemned by a legal system that never explains what he's accused of. The nightmare logic of bureaucracy, where the process itself becomes the punishment, gave us the word "Kafkaesque" for a reason.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A vast, dim hallway lined with identical doors, a single figure small against the scale of the space, cold institutional lighting.

### 29. The Metamorphosis — Franz Kafka (1915)
A man wakes up transformed into an insect, and the horror of the story isn't really the transformation, it's how quickly his family adjusts to seeing him as a burden. A compact, devastating study of alienation and the conditional nature of love.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A sparse bedroom seen from a low angle, morning light through a window, slightly distorted perspective to suggest something has fundamentally shifted.

### 30. The Stranger — Albert Camus (1942)
A man kills another man on an Algerian beach, almost casually, and the novel that follows is less about the crime than about a society's discomfort with someone who refuses to perform expected emotions. The foundational text of literary existentialism.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A blinding white beach at midday, harsh sun and stark shadows, almost overexposed, conveying heat as something oppressive rather than pleasant.

### 31. The Plague — Albert Camus (1947)
An Algerian city is sealed off by an outbreak of bubonic plague, and the novel becomes an allegory for how people respond to catastrophe, collectively and individually, with everything from denial to quiet heroism.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An empty Mediterranean city square at dusk, shuttered windows and warm stone, the kind of stillness that suggests absence rather than peace.

### 32. Things Fall Apart — Chinua Achebe (1958)
The story of a Igbo village leader whose life is upended by the arrival of European colonialism and Christian missionaries. Written partly as a response to colonial depictions of Africa, it remains one of the most widely read African novels in the world.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Nigerian village at golden hour, compounds and red earth paths, warm and richly detailed, capturing a world before its disruption.

### 33. The Color Purple — Alice Walker (1982)
Told through letters, this is the story of a Black woman's survival of abuse and her slow journey toward self-respect and love, set in the early 20th-century American South. Its epistolary form makes the gradual shift in the protagonist's voice the real arc of the book.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A rural Georgia porch in late afternoon, hand-stitched quilts drying on a line, warm and textured, suggesting resilience built from small things.

### 34. One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest — Ken Kesey (1962)
Set in a psychiatric ward, the arrival of a rebellious new patient threatens the iron control of the head nurse, and the novel becomes a parable about institutional power versus individual freedom. Narrated by a patient everyone assumes is too far gone to understand what's happening.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A sterile hospital dayroom with a single window showing the world outside, fluorescent and cold inside, warm and out of reach beyond the glass.

### 35. Slaughterhouse-Five — Kurt Vonnegut (1969)
A soldier becomes "unstuck in time" after surviving the firebombing of Dresden, jumping nonlinearly through his own life including an alien abduction subplot. Vonnegut's deceptively simple style carries one of the angriest anti-war arguments in American fiction.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A bombed-out city skyline at night, fires in the distance, rendered with a strange dreamlike calm rather than chaos, as if seen from outside time.

### 36. To Kill a Mockingbird — Harper Lee (1960)
A small Alabama town in the 1930s, seen through a child's eyes, as her father defends a Black man falsely accused of rape. Atticus Finch's closing arguments and the quiet courage of the Finch household made this one of the most assigned novels in American schools for good reason.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small-town courthouse square in summer heat, dust and dappled shade from old oak trees, nostalgic but with a sense of tension underneath.

### 37. The Grapes of Wrath — John Steinbeck (1939)
A family of Dust Bowl farmers travels to California in search of work, only to find exploitation waiting for them there too. Steinbeck's anger at economic injustice is matched by his tenderness toward the people caught inside it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dust-choked highway stretching to the horizon, an overloaded truck small against the landscape, sepia and ochre tones, vast and weary.

### 38. East of Eden — John Steinbeck (1952)
A multigenerational saga set in California's Salinas Valley, loosely retelling the Cain and Abel story through two families bound by rivalry, love, and inherited guilt. Steinbeck considered it his most important work.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The Salinas Valley at sunrise, fields of lettuce stretching toward distant hills, soft golden light, expansive and quietly hopeful.

### 39. The Old Man and the Sea — Ernest Hemingway (1952)
An aging Cuban fisherman's solitary battle with a giant marlin becomes a meditation on dignity, endurance, and the relationship between man and nature. Spare, stripped-down prose that earned Hemingway the Nobel Prize.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small wooden boat alone on open water at dawn, the silhouette of a great fish breaking the surface nearby, calm sea and pale sky, vast solitude.

### 40. A Farewell to Arms — Ernest Hemingway (1929)
An American ambulance driver on the Italian front falls in love with a nurse, and the novel becomes one of the great anti-war love stories, written in Hemingway's signature flat, understated style that makes the emotional blows land harder.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A field hospital tent at dusk, rain falling on a muddy Italian landscape, muted greys and greens with a single warm light inside the tent.

### 41. Lolita — Vladimir Nabokov (1955)
A linguistically dazzling and morally repellent first-person account from a man obsessed with a twelve-year-old girl, written so that the narrator's eloquence constantly fights against the horror of what he's describing. One of the most controversial and most studied novels of the 20th century.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An American roadside motel at dusk, neon sign half-lit, a long empty highway, a visual sense of restlessness and unease rather than anything from the narrative itself.

### 42. Doctor Zhivago — Boris Pasternak (1957)
A physician and poet's life is torn apart by the Russian Revolution and Civil War, his personal loves and losses set against vast historical upheaval. Banned in the Soviet Union, it was published abroad and won Pasternak the Nobel Prize, which Soviet pressure forced him to decline.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A snow-covered Russian railway station, a single train disappearing into a white horizon, cold blues and greys, vast and solitary.

### 43. The Master and Margarita — Mikhail Bulgakov (1967)
The Devil arrives in Soviet Moscow and chaos ensues, in a novel that blends satire, romance, and theology, written in secret over more than a decade and published only after Bulgakov's death. Its surreal energy and biting humor made it an underground sensation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Moscow rooftops at midnight under a full moon, a black cat silhouetted on a chimney, slightly fantastical, warm city lights against deep blue sky.

### 44. One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich — Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn (1962)
A single day in a Soviet labor camp, narrated with almost unbearable matter-of-factness, where small victories like an extra piece of bread become the entire emotional landscape of the book. One of the first works to expose the realities of the Gulag to a wide audience.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A frozen labor camp at dawn, rows of wooden barracks under heavy snow, harsh pale light, stark and unflinching.

### 45. The Tin Drum — Günter Grass (1959)
A narrator who decides at age three to stop growing tells the story of Germany's descent into Nazism and its aftermath, beating a tin drum as his act of protest and witness. Magical realism applied to one of history's darkest periods.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A narrow Danzig street in the 1930s, a small figure with a drum among adults, warm period color with an undertone of menace approaching from the edges.

### 46. The Name of the Rose — Umberto Eco (1980)
A murder mystery set in a medieval Italian monastery, where a Sherlock Holmes-like monk investigates a series of deaths tied to a forbidden book. Eco layers in theology, semiotics, and history without ever losing the pleasure of the mystery itself.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A vast monastery library, shelves rising into shadow, a single candle illuminating an ancient manuscript, rich browns and golds, mysterious and scholarly.

### 47. If on a Winter's Night a Traveler — Italo Calvino (1979)
A novel about the act of reading a novel, opening ten different stories that all get interrupted, addressed directly to "you," the reader. A playful, structurally audacious experiment that's also a love letter to why people read at all.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An open book on a table by a window, the page itself dissolving into the landscape it describes, a visual blending of reality and story.

### 48. The Leopard — Giuseppe di Lampedusa (1958)
An aging Sicilian aristocrat watches his world end as Italy unifies and the old order gives way to new money and new politics. Elegiac and gorgeously written, it captures the specific sadness of being the last of something.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A grand Sicilian villa ballroom in late afternoon light, dust motes visible in the air, faded opulence, warm but slightly melancholic.

### 49. The Magic Mountain — Thomas Mann (1924)
A young man visits a tuberculosis sanatorium in the Swiss Alps for three weeks and ends up staying for seven years, during which the novel becomes a vast debate about European civilization on the eve of World War I.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A snowbound Alpine sanatorium, white peaks under a clear sky, figures on balconies wrapped in blankets, crisp cold light, beautiful and isolating.

### 50. Siddhartha — Hermann Hesse (1922)
A young man in the time of the Buddha leaves home to seek enlightenment, moving through asceticism, worldly pleasure, and finally a kind of quiet wisdom found by a river. A short novel that's become a touchstone for generations seeking spiritual meaning outside organized religion.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A wide river at sunset, a lone figure seated on the bank, warm golds and ambers, calm and meditative.

### 51. Steppenwolf — Hermann Hesse (1927)
A middle-aged man torn between his civilized self and his "wolf" nature drifts through a strange, surreal Magic Theater that forces him to confront the many selves he's been suppressing. A cult favorite for its raw portrayal of alienation and inner fragmentation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dimly lit theater interior with mirrored doors leading in impossible directions, fragmented reflections, moody and slightly psychedelic.

### 52. Dracula — Bram Stoker (1897)
Told through letters, diary entries, and newspaper clippings, this is the novel that codified the modern vampire myth, a story of ancient evil arriving in modern, rational England and the band of unlikely heroes who fight it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Transylvanian castle on a cliff at night, a single lit window, mist curling around the towers, deep blues and blacks with one warm point of light.

### 53. The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde — Robert Louis Stevenson (1886)
A respectable London doctor's experiment to separate his good and evil natures unleashes a violent alter ego he can't control. A short novella that gave the world a permanent shorthand for hidden duality.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A foggy Victorian London street at night, gaslight haloed in mist, a single door slightly ajar, atmospheric and tense.

### 54. Great Expectations — Charles Dickens (1861)
An orphan's rise through society, funded by a mysterious benefactor, becomes a meditation on class, ambition, and the gap between who we want to be and who we actually become. Miss Havisham's decaying wedding feast is one of Dickens's most haunting images.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A grand, decaying dining room with a long-untouched wedding feast covered in dust and cobwebs, candles burned down to stubs, faded grandeur frozen in time.

### 55. A Tale of Two Cities — Charles Dickens (1859)
Set against the French Revolution, this is a story of sacrifice, doubling, and redemption, opening with one of literature's most famous lines about it being the best and worst of times. The guillotine looms over the entire second half.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Paris street during the Revolution, a crowd gathered near a barricade under a stormy sky, dramatic light, tension between order and chaos.

### 56. David Copperfield — Charles Dickens (1850)
A semi-autobiographical bildungsroman following its narrator from a difficult childhood through to adulthood, populated by some of Dickens's most memorable characters, from the cruel Mr. Murdstone to the endlessly hopeful Mr. Micawber.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A modest English cottage by the sea, fishing boats nearby, warm domestic light against a grey coastal sky, humble and hopeful.

### 57. Vanity Fair — William Makepeace Thackeray (1848)
A satirical panorama of early 19th-century English society, following the scheming Becky Sharp as she climbs the social ladder by any means available. Subtitled "A Novel Without a Hero," it's unsparing about everyone in it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A crowded ballroom seen slightly from above, glittering chandeliers and a sea of fine dresses, opulent but with a faint satirical edge in the framing.

### 58. The Count of Monte Cristo — Alexandre Dumas (1844)
A man wrongly imprisoned for years escapes, acquires a vast fortune, and methodically takes revenge on everyone who wronged him. One of the great adventure novels, equal parts thriller and meditation on the cost of obsession.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A rocky Mediterranean island fortress at dawn, a small boat approaching, dramatic cliffs and pale early light, suggestive of escape and transformation.

### 59. Les Misérables — Victor Hugo (1862)
An ex-convict's quest for redemption unfolds against the backdrop of post-revolutionary France, encompassing poverty, justice, love, and an uprising on the streets of Paris. Its moral scope is matched by its sheer momentum.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Paris street barricade at dawn, makeshift and improvised, early light breaking over rooftops, a sense of fragile hope before a storm.

### 60. The Hunchback of Notre-Dame — Victor Hugo (1831)
Set in medieval Paris, this is the story of a deformed bell-ringer, a beautiful dancer, and the cathedral that looms over both their fates. Hugo's love letter to Gothic architecture is as much about the building as the people in it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Notre-Dame's towers at dusk, gargoyles silhouetted against an orange sky, gothic and atmospheric, the cathedral itself as the central character.

### 61. The Three Musketeers — Alexandre Dumas (1844)
A young man arrives in Paris and falls in with three swordsmen whose loyalty to each other becomes the engine of a swashbuckling adventure through court intrigue. "All for one and one for all" has outlived the book itself.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A duel in a Parisian courtyard at dawn, cloaks and rapiers, warm stone walls and long shadows, dynamic and theatrical.

### 62. Treasure Island — Robert Louis Stevenson (1883)
A young boy's map to buried pirate treasure sets off one of the most influential adventure stories ever written, giving the world Long John Silver and the template for nearly every pirate story that followed.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A tropical island cove seen from a ship's deck, palm trees and white sand, golden light, adventurous and inviting.

### 63. Robinson Crusoe — Daniel Defoe (1719)
Often called the first English novel, this is the account of a man shipwrecked alone on an island for decades, forced to rebuild civilization from scratch. Equal parts survival story and meditation on self-reliance and faith.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A solitary figure on a beach looking out at an empty horizon, a simple shelter visible behind him, warm tones, quiet and self-contained.

### 64. Gulliver's Travels — Jonathan Swift (1726)
A satirical travelogue in which a ship's surgeon visits lands of tiny people, giants, and floating islands, using each absurd society to skewer the politics and pretensions of Swift's own era. Funnier and angrier than its reputation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A giant figure lying on a beach, bound by countless tiny ropes, tiny figures swarming around him, a striking contrast of scale, warm and slightly whimsical.

### 65. Candide — Voltaire (1759)
A young man is taught that this is "the best of all possible worlds," then subjected to an escalating series of disasters that thoroughly demolish the idea. A short, viciously funny satire of optimism, religion, and philosophy.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small garden plot being tended amid a chaotic, war-torn landscape in the background, a quiet visual nod to the novel's famous closing image of cultivating one's own garden.

### 66. The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn — Mark Twain (1884)
A boy and an escaped enslaved man raft down the Mississippi together, and their friendship becomes Twain's vehicle for a sharp moral critique of the society both of them are trying to escape. Hemingway called it the source of all modern American literature.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A wooden raft on the wide Mississippi at sunset, two figures aboard, warm orange light on the water, expansive and free.

### 67. The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — Arthur Conan Doyle (1892)
The collection that turned a Baker Street detective into one of fiction's most enduring characters, establishing the template for the modern detective story through Holmes's deductive method and his partnership with the steady Dr. Watson.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A foggy London street at night, a single gas lamp illuminating a figure in a deerstalker and coat, atmospheric Victorian noir.

### 68. The Hobbit — J.R.R. Tolkien (1937)
A comfortable, home-loving hobbit is swept into a quest with dwarves and a wizard to reclaim a stolen treasure from a dragon. Originally a children's book, it laid the groundwork for the entire modern fantasy genre.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A round green door set into a hillside, warm light spilling out, a path winding away toward distant blue mountains, cozy and inviting.

### 69. The Lord of the Rings — J.R.R. Tolkien (1954)
A small hobbit carries a corrupting ring across a vast continent to destroy it, in a story that defined epic fantasy as a genre and remains its most influential single work. Tolkien built an entire world, languages and history included, to support it.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A vast mountain range with a winding road below, small figures dwarfed by the landscape, epic scale, dramatic light breaking through clouds.

### 70. Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Lewis Carroll (1865)
A young girl falls down a rabbit hole into a world of nonsensical logic, talking animals, and increasingly strange characters. Its surreal imagery has been reinterpreted endlessly, from psychedelia to philosophy.
**Deckoviz inspo:** An oversized table set for tea in an overgrown garden, mismatched chairs and teacups, whimsical and slightly disorienting in scale.

### 71. Peter Pan — J.M. Barrie (1911)
A boy who refuses to grow up leads a group of children to a magical island, in a story that's far darker and stranger than its pantomime reputation suggests, dealing openly with mortality, loss, and the cost of never changing.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A nursery window open to a night sky full of stars, a small figure silhouetted against the moon, dreamy blues and silvers.

### 72. The Wind in the Willows — Kenneth Grahame (1908)
Animal friends along an English riverbank navigate friendship, home, and the occasional reckless adventure, in a gentle pastoral story that's as much about the English countryside as its characters.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A riverbank in late spring, willows trailing into the water, a small boat tied to the bank, soft green light, peaceful and idyllic.

### 73. The Little Prince — Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (1943)
A pilot stranded in the desert meets a young prince from a tiny asteroid, and their conversations become a gentle, melancholic meditation on love, loss, and what adults forget as they grow up.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small figure standing on a tiny planet against a star-filled sky, a single rose nearby, minimalist and quietly poignant.

### 74. Watership Down — Richard Adams (1972)
A group of rabbits flees the destruction of their warren and undertakes an epic journey to find a new home, in a novel that uses its animal characters to explore leadership, mythology, and survival with surprising depth.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Rolling English downland at golden hour, a line of small figures crossing an open field toward distant hills, warm and expansive.

### 75. Love in the Time of Cholera — Gabriel García Márquez (1985)
A man waits over fifty years for the woman he loved in his youth, while both their lives unfold separately in a Caribbean city marked by cholera epidemics and civil unrest. A love story stretched across an entire lifetime.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A Caribbean port town at sunset, colorful colonial buildings along a waterfront, riverboats in the harbor, warm and nostalgic.

### 76. The House of the Spirits — Isabel Allende (1982)
A multigenerational Chilean family saga blending political history with magical realism, following women whose clairvoyance and strength run through decades of upheaval. Often read as a counterpart to One Hundred Years of Solitude.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A large blue-painted hacienda surrounded by gardens, slightly surreal lighting, warm earth tones with a hint of the otherworldly.

### 77. Like Water for Chocolate — Laura Esquivel (1989)
A young Mexican woman forbidden from marrying pours her emotions into the food she cooks, which then affects everyone who eats it. Magical realism filtered through the kitchen, structured around recipes for each chapter.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A rustic Mexican kitchen filled with steam and warm light, dried chilies and herbs hanging from the ceiling, rich and sensory.

### 78. The Kite Runner — Khaled Hosseini (2003)
A friendship between two boys in Kabul is shattered by an act of betrayal, and the novel follows the guilt that follows one of them across decades and continents, against the backdrop of Afghanistan's transformation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Two kites tangled together against a clear blue sky over a hillside city, distant and small, capturing both freedom and fragility.

### 79. Midnight's Children — Salman Rushdie (1981)
A man born at the exact moment of India's independence finds his life mysteriously intertwined with the nation's history, in a sprawling, magical realist epic that won the Booker Prize and later the "Booker of Bookers."
**Deckoviz inspo:** A crowded Indian city street at midnight, fireworks lighting the sky above rooftops, vibrant colors against deep blue night, celebratory and chaotic.

### 80. The God of Small Things — Arundhati Roy (1997)
Twin siblings in Kerala grow up in the shadow of a family tragedy shaped by caste, class, and forbidden love. Roy's prose is dense and lyrical, circling the central event of the novel from multiple angles before revealing it fully.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A lush Kerala backwater at dusk, a small wooden boat among water lilies, deep greens and golden light, beautiful and quietly sorrowful.

### 81. Norwegian Wood — Haruki Murakami (1987)
A young man in 1960s Tokyo navigates grief, love, and mental illness through his relationships with two very different women. Quiet, melancholic, and full of small, specific details that give it an almost diary-like intimacy.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A snow-covered Japanese countryside path leading toward a distant farmhouse, soft grey light, isolated and contemplative.

### 82. The Remains of the Day — Kazuo Ishiguro (1989)
An English butler's road trip across the countryside becomes a reckoning with a lifetime of suppressed feeling and misplaced loyalty, told in a voice so restrained that everything important is said by what's left unsaid.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A grand English country house seen from across a misty lawn at dawn, formal and slightly distant, conveying restraint and quiet grandeur.

### 83. Never Let Me Go — Kazuo Ishiguro (2005)
Set in a quietly dystopian England, this is the story of young people raised at a boarding school whose true purpose is revealed slowly and devastatingly. A science fiction premise handled with the emotional texture of a literary novel.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A boarding school playing field under an overcast sky, figures in the distance, muted greens and greys, ordinary on the surface with something unsettling beneath.

### 84. The Handmaid's Tale — Margaret Atwood (1985)
In a near-future theocracy, fertile women are forced into reproductive servitude, narrated by one woman trying to hold onto her own identity under total control. Atwood's warning about the fragility of rights has only grown more discussed with time.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A row of figures in red cloaks and white bonnets walking through a quiet suburban street, stark color contrast against muted surroundings, unsettling and precise.

### 85. Persuasion — Jane Austen (1817)
Austen's last completed novel, a quieter and more melancholic story of second chances, following a woman who turns down a proposal out of social pressure and spends years regretting it. Often considered her most emotionally mature work.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A windswept seaside town, figures walking along a promenade with the sea beyond, soft autumnal light, wistful and reflective.

### 86. Catch-22 — Joseph Heller (1961)
A satirical, circular take on World War II bureaucracy, where the only way to be excused from dangerous missions is to be declared insane, but requesting the evaluation proves you're sane enough to fly. The phrase entered the language directly from this book.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A row of bomber planes on an airfield at dawn, repetitive and orderly, slightly absurdist in its uniformity, muted military tones.

### 87. On the Road — Jack Kerouac (1957)
A semi-autobiographical account of cross-country trips across America, written in a breathless, improvisational style that became the defining text of the Beat Generation. Less a plot than a mood, restless and hungry for experience.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A long open highway at night, headlights cutting through darkness, distant neon diner sign, a sense of motion and possibility.

### 88. A Clockwork Orange — Anthony Burgess (1962)
A violent teenager in a near-future Britain undergoes a brutal experimental treatment meant to cure him of his impulses, raising uncomfortable questions about free will and the ethics of forced rehabilitation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A stark, brutalist urban courtyard at night, harsh artificial lighting, geometric shadows, cold and clinical in tone.

### 89. Fahrenheit 451 — Ray Bradbury (1953)
In a future where books are illegal and firemen burn them, one fireman begins to question everything he's been trained to do. Bradbury wrote it in a rented typewriter room at a library, which feels appropriate.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A pile of books glowing with firelight against a dark background, warm orange light, dramatic and elegiac rather than purely destructive.

### 90. The Shadow of the Wind — Carlos Ruiz Zafón (2001)
A boy in post-war Barcelona discovers a mysterious novel and sets out to find its author, uncovering a decades-old story of love and tragedy along the way. A love letter to books themselves, wrapped in a gothic mystery.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A hidden labyrinthine library of forgotten books, towering shelves disappearing into shadow, a single lantern held by a small figure, atmospheric and mysterious.

### 91. The Goldfinch — Donna Tartt (2013)
A boy survives a museum bombing that kills his mother, and the small painting he takes from the wreckage becomes the thread that ties together decades of grief, art, and crime. Sprawling and immersive, it won the Pulitzer Prize.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A small, exquisitely lit painting of a bird on a wall, displayed alone in an otherwise dark room, intimate and slightly haunted.

### 92. A Suitable Boy — Vikram Seth (1993)
One of the longest novels ever published in English, following a mother's search for a husband for her daughter against the backdrop of post-independence India. Vast in scope but intimate in its portrayal of family life.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A bustling Indian wedding courtyard strung with lights and marigolds, warm golden tones, joyful and richly detailed.

### 93. Wide Sargasso Sea — Jean Rhys (1966)
A prequel of sorts to Jane Eyre, telling the story of the "madwoman in the attic" from her own perspective, a Creole heiress whose life in the Caribbean and later England is shaped by colonialism and isolation. A powerful act of literary reclamation.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A lush Caribbean estate garden in vivid color, slightly overgrown, contrasted with a distant cold grey English house in the background, two worlds in one frame.

### 94. Cry, the Beloved Country — Alan Paton (1948)
A Zulu pastor travels to Johannesburg to find his son, encountering the full weight of South Africa's racial injustice along the way. Written before apartheid was formally established, it reads as both prophecy and lament.
**Deckoviz inspo:** Rolling hills of rural South Africa at dawn, a single figure on a dirt road walking toward distant hills, warm earth tones, quiet and dignified.

### 95. The Joy Luck Club — Amy Tan (1989)
Four Chinese immigrant mothers and their American-born daughters navigate the gap between their cultures and generations, told through interwoven stories that move between San Francisco and pre-revolutionary China.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A mahjong table set with tea, soft lamplight, a window showing a San Francisco street outside, warm domestic interior bridging two worlds.

### 96. The Bell Jar — Sylvia Plath (1963)
A young woman's descent into depression during a summer internship in New York, written with unflinching clarity by a poet who would die by suicide a month after its publication. Raw, precise, and often devastating.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A New York apartment window looking out over a hazy summer skyline, glass slightly distorted, a sense of distance between the viewer and the world outside.

### 97. Rebecca — Daphne du Maurier (1938)
A young second wife arrives at her new husband's estate to find it still haunted by the presence of his first wife, who died under mysterious circumstances. A Gothic masterpiece of atmosphere and slow-building dread.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A grand English manor seen from a long driveway through overgrown rhododendrons, mist clinging to the grounds, beautiful and faintly ominous.

### 98. The Secret History — Donna Tartt (1992)
A group of classics students at an elite New England college commit a murder, and the novel works backward and forward from that act, exploring obsession, elitism, and the seductive danger of believing yourself above ordinary morality.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A New England campus quad in autumn, fallen leaves and old stone buildings, golden afternoon light with long shadows, beautiful but with an undercurrent of unease.

### 99. The Power and the Glory — Graham Greene (1940)
A fugitive priest in a Mexican state that's outlawed the Catholic Church tries to carry out his duties while evading capture, becoming Greene's deepest exploration of faith, failure, and grace under pressure.
**Deckoviz inspo:** A dusty Mexican village church, half in ruins, a single figure walking toward it under a vast sky, warm and desolate at once.

### 100. A Passage to India — E.M. Forster (1924)
An incident during a trip to the Marabar Caves exposes the deep racial and cultural fault lines of British colonial India, told with Forster's characteristic interest in whether genuine connection across difference is even possible.
**Deckoviz inspo:** The entrance to a rock-cut cave against a stark, sun-bleached landscape, small figures dwarfed by the scale of the rock, vast and ambiguous.

## Closing thoughts
A hundred books is not a finish line, it's a starting point. Every one of these worlds took its author years, sometimes decades, to build, and reading even one of them properly is its own kind of journey. What we've tried to do here is offer a second life for that journey, a way for the atmosphere of a book to linger in a room long after you've put it back on the shelf.

That's the idea behind DASPort and Vizzy: not replacing the book, but extending it. The windmills of La Mancha, the green light across Gatsby's bay, the rabbits crossing the downs at dusk, all of these can exist as ambient art that changes with your mood and your space, a quiet reminder of where a story took you.

So pick one. Read it, or reread it, then bring its world home. A hundred books, a hundred rooms you could live in. Start with whichever one is already calling to you.
