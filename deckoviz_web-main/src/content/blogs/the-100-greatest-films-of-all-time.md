## Introduction

Cinema is the art form that contains all other art forms.

Within a single film you will find painting (every frame is a composition), music (the score, the silence, the sound design), literature (the screenplay, the narrative architecture), theatre (the performance), photography (the cinematography), and architecture (the production design).

It is the most total sensory experience that art has produced, and the one that has reached the most people.

This guide gathers 100 of the greatest films ever made - across genres, across decades, across cultures and continents.

It is not a list of the most commercially successful films, or the most technically innovative, or the most critically celebrated by any single institution.

It is a curated selection of the films that have mattered most - to cinema as an art form, to the culture that produced them, and to the audiences who have carried them across time.

Every film here is a visual world.

A distinct palette, a signature way of moving through space, a specific quality of light, a set of images so memorable they have entered the permanent stock of human visual memory.

The shower in *Psycho*.

The bicycle chase in *Bicycle Thieves*.

HAL's red eye in *2001*.

The green light at the end of Daisy's dock in *The Great Gatsby*.

The briefcase glow in *Pulp Fiction*.

These are images that live in the mind independently of the films they came from.

### How to use this guide with your Deckoviz DASPort:

Every film here is a source of material for stunning poster art on your frame.

Use this guide as a creative brief for Vizzy sessions - generating posters, character art, scene studies, typographic poster designs, and colour palette explorations drawn from the visual world of each film.

There are several distinct directions you can take:

*   **Classic film poster art** - the tradition of hand-painted and graphic-designed movie poster art, from Saul Bass's iconic minimalist designs to the lush painted posters of Hollywood's golden age, is one of the richest in the history of graphic design. Use the films here as subjects for poster art in that tradition.
*   **Scene and frame art** - the most visually iconic scenes in these films make powerful standalone visual art. A still from *Vertigo*, rendered as a painterly poster. A Kubrick corridor from *The Shining*. The opening shot of *Touch of Evil*. Great cinematography is great visual art by definition.
*   **Character and portrait art** - the most memorable characters in cinema make extraordinary portrait art subjects. Chaplin's Tramp. Travis Bickle in his mohawk. Citizen Kane at the podium. These are visual archetypes that resonate beyond their films.
*   **Mood and colour palette art** - every great film has a signature colour world. Wes Anderson's symmetrical pastels. Kubrick's clinical whites and deep blacks. Wong Kar-wai's neon-soaked Hong Kong. These colour palettes, abstracted into pure visual art, create atmosphere on your frame that is immediately recognisable to those who know the films and beautiful to those who do not.
*   **Typography and title art** - the typographic language of cinema title design is one of the richest in graphic design history. The font choices, the colour relationships, the compositional decisions of great film title sequences make stunning typographic art for your frame.

Come back to this guide whenever you want to create film-inspired art. Use it as a reference for the visual worlds, the colour languages, and the iconic imagery of the greatest films ever made.

## The 100 Films

### 1. Citizen Kane (1941) 
**Director:** Orson Welles
Orson Welles was 25 when he made the film that most critics, for most of the 20th century, called the greatest ever made.

A fictionalised portrait of a newspaper magnate (based on William Randolph Hearst) told in reverse.
* **Visual world:** High-contrast black and white, dramatic low-angle compositions, cavernous spaces dwarfing their inhabitants, the snow globe, Xanadu in shadow.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Saul Bass-style minimalist title art. Kane at the podium in a vast space. The snow globe.

### 2. Vertigo (1958) 
**Director:** Alfred Hitchcock
Hitchcock's darkest and most personal film. The famous 'dolly zoom' was invented for this film.
* **Visual world:** San Francisco in cold greens and greys, Kim Novak's grey suit, the spiral motif recurring everywhere, red and green neon.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The spiral motif as typographic/graphic art. Madeleine in grey against green. The tower at night. 

### 3. Tokyo Story (1953) 
**Director:** Yasujirō Ozu
An elderly couple travel from their provincial home to Tokyo to visit their grown children. Ozu's camera sits at floor level - the perspective of someone sitting on a tatami mat. 
* **Visual world:** Post-war Tokyo and provincial Japan, low camera angle, tatami rooms, grey streets, the sea at Atami.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Japanese minimalist poster design. Low-angle corridor and room compositions. The elderly couple at the sea.

### 4. 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) 
**Director:** Stanley Kubrick
The most visually ambitious film ever made - a four-movement meditation on human evolution. 
* **Visual world:** The black monolith, clinical white spacecraft interiors, HAL's red eye, the African savanna at dawn, the cosmic light show, the Louis XVI room.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** HAL's red eye as a minimal art concept. The monolith in landscape. The star child. Space station exterior.

### 5. Bicycle Thieves (1948) 
**Director:** Vittorio De Sica
A man in post-war Rome has his bicycle stolen. Italian Neorealism at its peak - shot on the streets of Rome with non-professional actors, natural light.
* **Visual world:** Post-war Rome, crowded street scenes, grey stone, the bicycle itself as a symbol of hope and dignity.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Father and son walking through Roman streets. The bicycle as a graphic symbol. Italian neorealist poster aesthetic.

### 6. Persona (1966) 
**Director:** Ingmar Bergman
A nurse is assigned to care for an actress who has stopped speaking. Bergman strips film to its essential mechanics.
* **Visual world:** High-contrast Sven Nykvist black and white, close-up faces merging, the seashore, the hospital corridor, the frame burning.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Two female faces merging into one. The divided face composition - Bergman's most iconic image. 

### 7. 8½ (1963) 
**Director:** Federico Fellini
A film director, overwhelmed by creative block and personal crisis, retreats into memory, fantasy, and dream. 
* **Visual world:** Black and white Italy - spa towns, film sets, the director's imagination rendered as physical space.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Circular composition of figures in white. The director floating above a crowd. Film set art.

### 8. Sunrise: A Song of Two Humans (1927) 
**Director:** F.W. Murnau
Made in Hollywood but entirely European in spirit. The way it is told - the visual richness, the emotional expressiveness - has never been surpassed.
* **Visual world:** Expressionist shadow and light, the fog over the lake, the city at night in blazing light, moonlight over water.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Expressionist shadow art. The couple in moonlight on the water. City-at-night art in 1920s aesthetic.

### 9. The Rules of the Game (1939) 
**Director:** Jean Renoir
A comedy of manners among the French aristocracy and their servants at a country estate.
* **Visual world:** The French country estate, theatrical costumes and masks, the game hunt, the costume ball.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** French château interior art with theatrical warmth. Masked ball compositions. Aristocratic portrait art.

### 10. Rashomon (1950) 
**Director:** Akira Kurosawa
Four witnesses give four contradictory accounts of a samurai's death in a grove.
* **Visual world:** Dappled forest light, the ruined gate in rain, samurai costumes.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Japanese forest light art. Samurai composition art. The ruined gate in rain.

### 11. Jeanne Dielman, 23 Commerce Quay, 1080 Brussels (1975) 
**Director:** Chantal Akerman
Three days in the life of a widowed Belgian woman who maintains a rigid domestic routine - in real time, without judgment.
* **Visual world:** The beige apartment, the kitchen table, the precise geometry of domestic space, slow natural light.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Domestic still life art in Akerman's flat, precise aesthetic. Kitchen and interior compositions.

### 12. Apocalypse Now (1979) 
**Director:** Francis Ford Coppola
A journey up a fictional river into the jungle to find a rogue colonel named Kurtz who has gone mad with truth.
* **Visual world:** Napalm sunsets, green jungle, the neon nightmare of Saigon, Kurtz in shadow, the river at night.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Vietnam War napalm sunset art. Helicopter silhouette compositions. Kurtz in shadow.

### 13. Mirror (1975) 
**Director:** Andrei Tarkovsky
Tarkovsky's most personal film - fragmentary memories, dreams, and historical documentary footage.
* **Visual world:** The dacha in the Russian countryside, children running through fields, fire, wind through grass.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Wind through grass - a Tarkovsky visual signature. Fire and water abstract art. Russian countryside.

### 14. Metropolis (1927) 
**Director:** Fritz Lang
The most ambitious silent film ever made - a vision of a future city divided between workers underground and the elite above.
* **Visual world:** Art Deco city towers, the underground worker city, the robot Maria, the clock face, the machine heart.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Metropolis city skyline. The robot Maria. Art Deco sci-fi poster design.

### 15. Stalker (1979) 
**Director:** Andrei Tarkovsky
A guide takes a writer and a scientist through the Zone, cinema's most sustained meditation on faith, desire, and the limits of human knowledge.
* **Visual world:** The sepia of the outside world, the green of the Zone, the long grass in water, the abandoned railway.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The Zone's green world - industrial ruin in water and grass. Sepia-to-colour transition art. 

### 16. The Godfather (1972) 
**Director:** Francis Ford Coppola
The film that elevated American popular cinema to art in a single stroke. 
* **Visual world:** Gordon Willis's dark photography (the 'Prince of Darkness') - half the frame in shadow. Candlelit rooms. Sun-bleached Sicily. 
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The Godfather marionette poster - one of the great graphic designs in cinema history. Candlelit portrait art.

### 17. Breathless (À Bout de Souffle) (1960) 
**Director:** Jean-Luc Godard
The film that started the French New Wave - shot on handheld camera on the streets of Paris.
* **Visual world:** Black-and-white Paris streets, jump cuts that create a perpetual present tense.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** French New Wave graphic design aesthetic. Black-and-white Paris street photography art. 

### 18. Singin' in the Rain (1952) 
**Director:** Stanley Donen & Gene Kelly
The most joyful film ever made. Gene Kelly in a yellow raincoat dancing in the rain.
* **Visual world:** Technicolor Hollywood, the rain-soaked street, the yellow raincoat, the pink and purple fantasy sequence.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The rain dance as a poster composition - one of cinema's most iconic images. 

### 19. Sunset Boulevard (1950) 
**Director:** Billy Wilder
A failed screenwriter is hired by a faded silent film star to work on her comeback.
* **Visual world:** The decaying mansion in Sunset Boulevard, the swimming pool, Norma Desmond's grand entrance.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The decaying Hollywood mansion at night. Norma Desmond portrait. The close-up motif.

### 20. The Seventh Seal (1957) 
**Director:** Ingmar Bergman

* **Visual world:** Medieval Sweden, the chess game on the beach, the dance of death silhouette against the sky.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** The chess game with Death - one of cinema's greatest visual concepts for poster art.

### 21. Chinatown (1974) 
**Director:** Roman Polanski
The greatest American film of the 1970s - a private detective in 1930s Los Angeles investigates a water rights scandal.
* **Visual world:** 1930s Los Angeles in dusty golds and browns, Jack Nicholson with the bandaged nose, the drought-parched hillsides.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Film noir Los Angeles art in warm dusty palette. Jack Nicholson's bandaged face. Water and drought concept art.

### 22. Do the Right Thing (1989) 
**Director:** Spike Lee

### 23. Nashville (1975) 
**Director:** Robert Altman
Twenty-four characters over five days in Nashville, Tennessee.

### 24. Mulholland Drive (2001)

### 25. There Will Be Blood (2007) 
**Director:** Paul Thomas Anderson

### 26. In the Mood for Love (2000)

### 27. Andrei Rublev (1966) 
**Director:** Andrei Tarkovsky

* **Visual world:** Medieval Russia in black-and-white, snow and mud and fire, the final revelation of Rublev's golden icons in colour.
* **Deckoviz poster art:** Russian icon gold art. The transition from black-and-white to colour as a visual concept.

### 28. Pulp Fiction (1994) 
**Director:** Quentin Tarantino
Three interlinked stories of Los Angeles crime culture - told out of sequence.
* **Visual world:** 1990s Los Angeles diners and motel rooms, the briefcase glow, the dance contest, the neon.

### 29. Dr. Strangelove (1964)

### 30. Wild Strawberries (1957)

An elderly professor, driving to receive an honorary doctorate, remembers his life.

### 31. Schindler's List (1993)

### 32. Parasite (2019)

## Outro

One hundred films. One hundred ways of seeing the world, living in time, and asking what it means to be human.

What is remarkable about cinema, compared to the other art forms in the Deckoviz library, is how specific it is.

A painting can exist in multiple contexts - a reproduction, a postcard, a projection on a wall.

A piece of music can fill a room, a car, a pair of headphones.

But a film is always a film - it demands time, it demands attention, it demands that you enter its world and stay there for the duration.

In that quality of demand lies its unique power: of all the art forms, cinema asks the most and gives the most in return.

The images from these films have outlasted their original contexts.

You do not need to have seen *Vertigo* to feel the power of the spiral.

You do not need to have seen *Blade Runner* to respond to neon rain on a dark city.

You do not need to have seen *Days of Heaven* to be moved by the light of magic hour on a wheat field.

These images have become part of the shared visual vocabulary of anyone who has grown up in a culture saturated with cinema - which is, at this point, almost everyone on earth.

On your Deckoviz DASPort, you can bring these visual worlds home.

Use Vizzy to generate film-inspired poster art across the five directions outlined in the introduction: the classic film poster tradition, the iconic scene or frame, the character portrait, the signature colour palette, the typographic title art.

Mix approaches - a Godfather portrait in the chiaroscuro of Gordon Willis, or a Blade Runner colour palette applied to your own city.

Take the visual language of cinema and use it as raw material for art that is entirely your own.

The 100 films in this guide represent roughly 120 years of cinema - from the Lumière brothers to Charlotte Wells, from the first flickering images of a train arriving at a station to the degraded Super 8 footage of a father and daughter at a Turkish beach resort.

Every one of them shows you something about how life looks from a specific vantage point, in a specific light, at a specific moment.

Every one of them asks, in some way, the only question that art ever asks: what does it mean to be here, alive, now?

Use them as your archive. Use your frame as your cinema.
