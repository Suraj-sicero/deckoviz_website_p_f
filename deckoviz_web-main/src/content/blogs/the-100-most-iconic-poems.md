## Introduction
A poem is language at its most concentrated. The novel gives you time — hundreds of pages to develop a world, a consciousness, an argument. The poem gives you almost no time at all. It gives you a few lines, sometimes just a few words, in which something has to happen or the whole enterprise fails. And when it works — when the right words are in the right order with the right music between them — a poem achieves something that no other form of language can: it changes the reader permanently, in less time than it takes to read a paragraph.

Poetry is the oldest literary art. Before writing, before novels, before histories, there were poems — spoken and memorised and passed between generations because the compression and the music of poetry made it possible to carry important things in the head. The Iliad and the Odyssey were poems before they were texts. The Psalms are poems. The Song of Songs is a poem. The Bhagavad Gita is a poem. The oldest surviving literary work we have — the Epic of Gilgamesh — is a poem. We have been making poems for as long as we have been making anything.

This collection gathers 100 of the most iconic poems in human history — across cultures, across centuries, across every mode from the epic to the lyric, from the celebratory to the elegiac, from the politically urgent to the privately ecstatic. These are the poems that people have memorised and quoted and returned to at the crucial moments of their lives. The ones that get read at weddings and funerals. The ones that helped someone survive something. The ones that said, with perfect precision, the thing the reader had been trying to say for years.

**On your Deckoviz DASPort:**

A poem — or a stanza, or a single perfect line — is already a visual object: words arranged on a page in a specific relationship to space and silence. It catches your eye at different moments of the day and says something different each time. It accumulates meaning gradually, over days and weeks of daily encounter.

Use this guide as a brief for Vizzy typographic poster sessions. Combine the words with visual art — the seascape that matches the Tennyson, the Yorkshire moor that matches the Brontë, the Algerian sun that matches the Camus. Pair poetry with moodscapes — the evening light that suits a Keats ode, the winter dawn that suits a Frost poem, the city rain that suits an Eliot passage.

A poem on your wall is a different thing from a poem in a book. It is part of your daily visual world — a companion, a reminder, a question, a comfort. The greatest poems in human history, displayed in your home: that is what your Deckoviz frame makes possible.

## The 100 Poems

### 1. The Iliad — Homer (c. 8th century BCE)
The founding epic of Western literature — 24 books, 15,000 lines, the wrath of Achilles and the fall of Troy. The poem from which Western storytelling descends.
**Display passage:** 'Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus, / that brought countless ills upon the Achaeans.'
**Deckoviz:** Greek warships on the wine-dark sea. Troy burning at night. The Homeric world as epic visual art.

### 2. The Odyssey — Homer (c. 8th century BCE)
The first homecoming story — Odysseus's ten-year journey back to Ithaca after the fall of Troy. The template for every subsequent quest narrative.
**Display passage:** 'Tell me, O muse, of that ingenious hero / who travelled far and wide after he had sacked / the famous town of Troy.'
**Deckoviz:** Odysseus at sea. The Cyclops's cave. The Mediterranean world at dawn.

### 3. The Divine Comedy — Dante Alighieri (1308–1321)
The greatest poem in any language. A complete moral universe — Hell, Purgatory, and Heaven — rendered in terza rima of perfect formal beauty.
**Display passage:** 'In the middle of the journey of our life / I came to myself in a dark wood / where the straight way was lost.'
**Deckoviz:** The dark wood opening. The circles of Hell. Beatrice in light. The most famous opening stanza in European poetry as typographic art.

### 4. The Song of Songs (c. 10th–3rd century BCE)
The most erotic and most purely beautiful poem in the Hebrew Bible — a celebration of physical and emotional love of extraordinary openness and beauty.
**Display passage:** 'I am my beloved's and my beloved is mine: / he feedeth among the lilies.'
**Deckoviz:** Garden and beloved concept art. Ancient love poetry visual. 'I am my beloved's' as a natural Deckoviz romantic poster — one of the most beautiful lines in any language.

### 5. The Psalms (c. 10th–5th century BCE)
150 poems — of praise, lamentation, gratitude, and despair — that have been the most widely read poetry in human history for three thousand years. The Psalms invented the lyric of direct address to the divine.
**Display passage:** 'The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want. / He maketh me to lie down in green pastures.'
**Deckoviz:** Green pasture and still water. Psalm 23 as the most displayed poem in history — a natural Deckoviz collection centrepiece. Sacred landscape visual art.

### 6. Ode to Joy — Friedrich Schiller (1785)
The poem Beethoven set in his Ninth Symphony — a vision of universal human brotherhood and joy that became the anthem of the European Union.
**Display passage:** 'Freude, schöner Götterfunken, / Tochter aus Elysium... / Alle Menschen werden Brüder.' (Joy, beautiful spark of the gods, / Daughter of Elysium... / All men become brothers.)
**Deckoviz:** The Ninth Symphony as a visual concept. 'All men become brothers' as typographic art. Joy as a visual concept.

### 7. To Autumn — John Keats (1819)
The most perfect lyric poem in English. Three stanzas — the richness of harvest, the personification of autumn, the sounds of the dying season — of extraordinary musical beauty.
**Display passage:** 'Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness, / Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun.'
**Deckoviz:** Autumn harvest in warm golden light. 'Mellow fruitfulness' as a concept for the Deckoviz autumn collection. This poem's opening two lines are among the most requested typographic poster texts in English literature.

### 8. Ode on a Grecian Urn — John Keats (1819)
The meditation on a Greek urn that ends with the most famous couplet in English poetry: 'Beauty is truth, truth beauty.'
**Display passage:** '"Beauty is truth, truth beauty,"—that is all / Ye know on earth, and all ye need to know.'
**Deckoviz:** The Grecian urn. Ancient beauty as a visual concept. 'Beauty is truth, truth beauty' as the most important typographic art concept from Romantic poetry — a natural Deckoviz poster.

### 9. Ode to a Nightingale — John Keats (1819)
Keats, ill with tuberculosis, listens to a nightingale and contemplates mortality, beauty, and the desire to escape the pain of consciousness.
**Display passage:** 'Thou wast not born for death, immortal Bird! / No hungry generations tread thee down; / The voice I hear this passing night was heard / In ancient days by emperor and clown.'
**Deckoviz:** The nightingale in a moonlit garden. Mortality and beauty concept. Keats's London study as literary portrait art.

### 10. The Waste Land — T.S. Eliot (1922)
The most important poem of the 20th century. A fragmented, allusive exploration of spiritual desolation in the aftermath of the First World War.
**Display passage:** 'April is the cruellest month, breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land, mixing / Memory and desire.'
**Deckoviz:** London Bridge in grey morning light. The Unreal City. 'April is the cruellest month' as a striking typographic seasonal concept — one of the most famous opening lines in modern poetry.

### 11. The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock — T.S. Eliot (1915)
The defining poem of modern alienation. Prufrock, middle-aged and paralysed by self-consciousness, fails to act, fails to speak, fails to connect — while the mermaids sing to each other, not to him.
**Display passage:** 'Do I dare disturb the universe? / In a minute there is time / For decisions and revisions which a minute will reverse.'
**Deckoviz:** The yellow fog of London. 'Do I dare to eat a peach?' as a typographic concept. The paralysis of the modern self as visual art.

### 12. If — Rudyard Kipling (1910)
A father's advice to a son — the qualities that make a complete human being. The most anthologised English poem of the 20th century and one of the most displayed.
**Display passage:** 'If you can keep your head when all about you / Are losing theirs and blaming it on you... / Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it, / And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!'
**Deckoviz:** 'If' is among the most natural poems for Deckoviz display — its affirmative, instructional quality makes it one of the great typographic poster poems. The entire poem displayed as a single piece is a Deckoviz classic.

### 13. The Road Not Taken — Robert Frost (1916)
Two roads diverged in a yellow wood. The poem everyone thinks is about taking the unconventional path — and which is actually about the human need to make our choices feel meaningful in retrospect.
**Display passage:** 'Two roads diverged in a wood, and I— / I took the one less traveled by, / And that has made all the difference.'
**Deckoviz:** Two paths in an autumn wood. The fork in the road as a visual concept. This is among the most displayed poems in homes worldwide — a natural Deckoviz typographic art subject.

### 14. Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening — Robert Frost (1923)
The most perfectly achieved of all American short poems. A traveller stops to watch snow fill a dark forest — and then remembers he has 'miles to go before I sleep.'
**Display passage:** 'The woods are lovely, dark and deep, / But I have promises to keep, / And miles to go before I sleep, / And miles to go before I sleep.'
**Deckoviz:** Dark winter forest in falling snow. 'Miles to go before I sleep' is one of American poetry's most beloved typographic display passages — a natural Deckoviz evening concept.

### 15. Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night — Dylan Thomas (1951)
Written for his dying father — the most urgent and most formally perfect villanelle in English. The command to rage against death.
**Display passage:** 'Do not go gentle into that good night, / Old age should burn and rave at close of day; / Rage, rage against the dying of the light.'
**Deckoviz:** The dying light as a visual concept. The refrain displayed as typographic art. Dylan Thomas's Wales — the cliffs and the sea. One of the most powerful Deckoviz motivation and resilience poster concepts.

### 16. The Second Coming — W.B. Yeats (1919)
Written in the aftermath of World War One and the Irish Revolution — the most prophetically accurate poem of the 20th century.
**Display passage:** 'Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold; / Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world... / The best lack all conviction, while the worst / Are full of passionate intensity.'
**Deckoviz:** The falcon and the falconer. Things falling apart as a visual concept. 'The centre cannot hold' as typographic art. Yeats's apocalyptic vision as contemporary moodscape.

### 17. When You Are Old — W.B. Yeats (1893)
A poem addressed to Maud Gonne — the woman Yeats loved and who never loved him back — imagining her in old age reading his poems and realising what she had refused.
**Display passage:** 'When you are old and grey and full of sleep, / And nodding by the fire, take down this book.'
**Deckoviz:** The fireplace in old age. 'Loved the pilgrim soul in you' — the most romantic line in Yeats — as typographic art. Grey hair and firelight moodscape.

### 18. The Lake Isle of Innisfree — W.B. Yeats (1890)
The longing for a simple life — a cabin on a lake in the west of Ireland — against the noise of the city.
**Display passage:** 'I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree, / And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made; / Nine bean-rows will I have there, a hive for the honey-bee, / And live alone in the bee-loud glade.'
**Deckoviz:** The Irish lake at dawn. The bee-loud glade. Simple living concept. A natural Deckoviz slow-living and intentional life poster concept.

### 19. Wild Geese — Mary Oliver (1986)
The most consoling poem of the 20th century. You do not have to be good. You only have to let the soft animal of your body love what it loves.
**Display passage:** 'You do not have to be good. / You do not have to walk on your knees / for a hundred miles through the desert, repenting. / You only have to let the soft animal of your body / love what it loves.'
**Deckoviz:** Wild geese in flight. The open field. This poem is among the most natural and most important Deckoviz display poems — its message of self-acceptance and presence is aligned with everything Deckoviz stands for. The passage above as typographic art.

### 20. The Summer Day — Mary Oliver (1990)
A meditation on a grasshopper — and the most important question in American poetry: 'Tell me, what is it you plan to do / with your one wild and precious life?'
**Display passage:** 'Tell me, what is it you plan to do / with your one wild and precious life?'
**Deckoviz:** The grasshopper in the summer field. 'Your one wild and precious life' is one of the most displayed poetic lines in homes and offices worldwide — a central Deckoviz affirmation concept. The most important question in modern poetry as daily visual art.

### 21. Daffodils — William Wordsworth (1807)
Wordsworth walking in the Lake District, stumbling on a host of golden daffodils beside a lake — and the memory of them, years later, filling him with joy in solitude.
**Display passage:** 'I wandered lonely as a cloud / That floats on high o'er vales and hills, / When all at once I saw a crowd, / A host, of golden daffodils.'
**Deckoviz:** Golden daffodils beside a lake in the Lake District. Spring landscape art. Wordsworth in his landscape — one of the great visual concepts for nature-themed Deckoviz art. The 'host of golden daffodils' as a seasonal collection centrepiece.

### 22. Tintern Abbey — William Wordsworth (1798)
Wordsworth revisiting the Wye Valley five years after his first visit — meditation on memory, nature, and 'spots of time.'
**Display passage:** '...that serene and blessed mood, / In which the affections gently lead us on— / Until, the breath of this corporeal frame / And even the motion of our human blood / Almost suspended, we are laid asleep / In body, and become a living soul.'
**Deckoviz:** The Wye Valley in soft grey-green. The ruined abbey. The river and the trees. Nature as spiritual sustenance concept.

### 23. The Prelude — William Wordsworth (1850)
The longest and most sustained autobiographical poem in English — the growth of a poet's mind from childhood to maturity.
**Display passage:** 'Bliss was it in that dawn to be alive, / But to be young was very heaven!'
**Deckoviz:** Dawn over the Lake District. Revolutionary youth as a visual concept. 'Very heaven' typographic art.

### 24. Ode: Intimations of Immortality — William Wordsworth (1807)
The most philosophically ambitious of Wordsworth's shorter poems — on the fading of the visionary gleam of childhood.
**Display passage:** 'Though nothing can bring back the hour / Of splendour in the grass, of glory in the flower; / We will grieve not, rather find / Strength in what remains behind.'
**Deckoviz:** 'Splendour in the grass' — used by Elia Kazan as a film title — as typographic art. Childhood joy and adult memory as visual concept.

### 25. She Walks in Beauty — Lord Byron (1815)
Byron's poem written after seeing a woman in mourning dress at a party — the most immediately beautiful of all English love poems.
**Display passage:** 'She walks in beauty, like the night / Of cloudless climes and starry skies; / And all that's best of dark and bright / Meet in her aspect and her eyes.'
**Deckoviz:** 'She walks in beauty, like the night' as a natural Deckoviz Romantic Mode typographic poster — among the most requested lines from English Romantic poetry. The woman in dark and starlight concept art.

### 26. Ozymandias — Percy Bysshe Shelley (1818)
A traveller describes a shattered statue in the desert — 'My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings; / Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!' — and the empty desert all around it. The most compressed meditation on the vanity of power ever written.
**Display passage:** 'My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings; / Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair! / Nothing beside remains.'
**Deckoviz:** The shattered statue in the desert sand. 'Nothing beside remains' as a typographic concept. Empires and their ruins. One of the great visual poster poems — the entire sonnet works beautifully as display text.

### 27. Ode to the West Wind — Percy Bysshe Shelley (1820)
Shelley's invocation of the West Wind as both destroyer and preserver — and the plea: 'If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?'
**Display passage:** 'O Wind, / If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?'
**Deckoviz:** The West Wind over autumn leaves. 'Can Spring be far behind?' as a seasonal Deckoviz poster concept. Autumn to spring transition visual art.

### 28. How Do I Love Thee? — Elizabeth Barrett Browning (1850)
The most celebrated Victorian love sonnet. 'How do I love thee? Let me count the ways.'
**Display passage:** 'How do I love thee? Let me count the ways. / I love thee to the depth and breadth and height / My soul can reach.'
**Deckoviz:** One of the most natural Deckoviz Romantic Mode and couple portrait display poems. 'How do I love thee?' as typographic art — simple, beautiful, enduring.

### 29. The Charge of the Light Brigade — Alfred Lord Tennyson (1854)
Written within days of the disastrous cavalry charge at Balaclava during the Crimean War — 'Into the valley of Death / Rode the six hundred.'
**Display passage:** 'Half a league, half a league, / Half a league onward, / All in the valley of Death / Rode the six hundred.'
**Deckoviz:** The cavalry charge as historical visual art. Courage and futility concept. The valley as a visual landscape.

### 30. Ulysses — Alfred Lord Tennyson (1833)
The aged Ulysses refuses to rest and prepares to sail once more — the greatest poem about the refusal to submit to age and limitation.
**Display passage:** 'To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield.'
**Deckoviz:** 'To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield' is one of the great motivational typographic poster lines in English poetry — used by the 2012 London Olympics and carved on Scott's Antarctic memorial. A central Deckoviz resilience concept.

### 31. The Raven — Edgar Allan Poe (1845)
A grieving man, visited at midnight by a raven who speaks one word — 'Nevermore' — sinks into madness. The most musically perfect of all American poems.
**Display passage:** 'Once upon a midnight dreary, while I pondered, weak and weary, / Over many a quaint and curious volume of forgotten lore.'
**Deckoviz:** The raven on the bust of Pallas. Midnight study by candlelight. 'Nevermore' as a Gothic typographic concept.

### 32. Annabel Lee — Edgar Allan Poe (1849)
Poe's last poem — a love that not even death could end, written months before his own death.
**Display passage:** 'I was a child and she was a child, / In this kingdom by the sea; / But we loved with a love that was more than love— / I and my Annabel Lee.'
**Deckoviz:** 'A love that was more than love' — one of the most romantic lines in American poetry — as typographic art. The kingdom by the sea. Moonlit coastal landscape.

### 33. Song of Myself — Walt Whitman (1855)
The founding poem of American democratic identity. 'I celebrate myself, and sing myself, / And what I assume you shall assume.'
**Display passage:** 'I am large, I contain multitudes.'
**Deckoviz:** 'I contain multitudes' is one of the most displayed lines from American poetry — a natural Deckoviz self-expression poster concept. Whitman's open American landscape. The self as democratic multitude concept art.

### 34. O Captain! My Captain! — Walt Whitman (1865)
Written for Lincoln after his assassination — the most moving elegy in American poetry.
**Display passage:** 'O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done, / The ship has weather'd every rack, the prize we sought is won.'
**Deckoviz:** The ship coming to harbour. Lincoln's death as a historical visual concept. The captain as leader and loss concept art.

### 35. Because I could not stop for Death — Emily Dickinson (c. 1863)
Death comes as a courteous gentleman in a carriage — and takes the speaker on a leisurely drive toward eternity. The most original treatment of mortality in American poetry.
**Display passage:** 'Because I could not stop for Death— / He kindly stopped for me— / The Carriage held but just Ourselves— / And Immortality.'
**Deckoviz:** The carriage in late afternoon light. Death as a courteous visitor concept. Dickinson's slant rhyme and dash aesthetic as typographic art.

### 36. I heard a Fly buzz — when I died — Emily Dickinson (c. 1862)
At the moment of death, a fly buzzes between the dying person and the light. The most startling and most original treatment of the death moment in English poetry.
**Display passage:** 'I heard a Fly buzz—when I died— / The Stillness in the Room / Was like the Stillness in the Air— / Between the Heaves of Storm.'
**Deckoviz:** The fly and the dying light as a visual concept. Dickinson's stark typographic aesthetic. Stillness before death as atmospheric art.

### 37. Hope is the thing with feathers — Emily Dickinson (c. 1861)
Hope as a bird that perches in the soul and 'sings the tune without the words.'
**Display passage:** 'Hope is the thing with feathers— / That perches in the soul— / And sings the tune without the words— / And never stops—at all.'
**Deckoviz:** The bird in the soul — one of Dickinson's most beautiful metaphors — as visual art. 'Hope is the thing with feathers' as a Deckoviz hope and resilience collection typographic poster.

### 38. Still I Rise — Maya Angelou (1978)
The most powerful poem of defiance and self-affirmation in American poetry — and the most widely memorised.
**Display passage:** 'You may shoot me with your words, / You may cut me with your eyes, / You may kill me with your hatefulness, / But still, like air, I'll rise.'
**Deckoviz:** 'Still I Rise' as among the most important Deckoviz poster poems for the Resilience and Human Dignity collections. The rising figure. Defiance and joy as visual concepts. One of the great motivational poems in any language.

### 39. Phenomenal Woman — Maya Angelou (1978)
A celebration of the power and beauty of the Black female body — against every standard that denies it.
**Display passage:** 'I'm a woman / Phenomenally. / Phenomenal woman, / That's me.'
**Deckoviz:** 'Phenomenal woman' as a natural Deckoviz self-affirmation poster. The poem's confidence and joy as visual concepts.

### 40. If You Forget Me — Pablo Neruda (1952)
Neruda's most devastating love poem — a conditional statement of mutual devotion.
**Display passage:** 'I want you to know one thing. / You know how this is: / if I look at the crystal moon, at the red branch / of the slow autumn at my window, / if I touch near the fire the impalpable ash / or the wrinkled body of the log, / everything carries me to you.'
**Deckoviz:** The crystal moon. The slow autumn window. 'Everything carries me to you' as a Romantic Mode Deckoviz typographic concept — one of the most beautiful love poem passages available for display.

### 41. Tonight I Can Write the Saddest Lines — Pablo Neruda (1924)
From Twenty Love Poems and a Song of Despair — the most famous opening of any love poem in Spanish.
**Display passage:** 'Tonight I can write the saddest lines. / Write, for example: / "The night is starry, / and the stars, blue and shiver in the distance."'
**Deckoviz:** The starry night as visual concept. 'Tonight I can write the saddest lines' as typographic art. Night sky and emotional loss as visual moodscape.

### 42. Ode to My Socks — Pablo Neruda (1956)
The most joyfully eccentric of Neruda's odes — a celebration of a pair of beautiful woollen socks that resists the impulse to keep them unworn.
**Display passage:** 'Moral: / beauty is twice / beauty / and what is good is doubly / good.'
**Deckoviz:** The ode as a form of attention — paying full attention to ordinary beautiful things — is precisely the Deckoviz philosophy. Neruda's odes to everyday objects as visual concept.

### 43. The Guest House — Rumi (13th century)
Every emotion is a guest arriving at the door of the human body — welcome them all, even the dark ones, because they have been sent as guides.
**Display passage:** 'This human being is a guest house. / Every morning a new arrival. / A joy, a depression, a meanness, / some momentary awareness comes / as an unexpected visitor.'
**Deckoviz:** The Guest House concept is one of the most important Deckoviz wellbeing and mindfulness poems. 'Welcome and entertain them all' as typographic art. The open door as a visual metaphor.

### 44. Out beyond ideas — Rumi (13th century)
The most famous of Rumi's short poems — there is a field beyond right and wrong, where he will meet you.
**Display passage:** 'Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, / there is a field. I'll meet you there.'
**Deckoviz:** 'Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field' — among the most displayed Rumi lines in homes worldwide. The open field as visual concept. A central Deckoviz contemplative mode poster.

### 45. I died as a mineral — Rumi (13th century)
Rumi's account of the stages of being — mineral to plant to animal to human to divine.
**Display passage:** 'I died as a mineral and became a plant, / I died as a plant and rose to animal, / I died as animal and I was Man. / Why should I fear? When was I less by dying?'
**Deckoviz:** The stages of being as a visual concept. Death and rebirth in nature. Spiritual evolution concept art.

### 46. Anthem — Leonard Cohen (1992)
The most important of Cohen's late poems — the crack in everything is how the light gets in.
**Display passage:** 'Ring the bells that still can ring / Forget your perfect offering / There is a crack in everything / That's how the light gets in.'
**Deckoviz:** 'There is a crack in everything, that's how the light gets in' is one of the most displayed poetic lines in contemporary homes — a central Deckoviz poster concept for the Resilience and Beauty collections. The cracked vessel with light emerging.

### 47. Hallelujah — Leonard Cohen (1984)
Not strictly a poem but a sequence of verses constituting the most theologically and emotionally complex lyric in popular music — a meditation on desire, failure, grace, and transcendence.
**Display passage:** 'And even though it all went wrong / I'll stand before the Lord of Song / With nothing on my tongue but Hallelujah.'
**Deckoviz:** The broken hallelujah as a visual concept. Sacred and secular light combined. 'Nothing on my tongue but Hallelujah' as typographic art.

### 48. The Peace of Wild Things — Wendell Berry (1968)
When despair comes in the night, the poet goes to the woods and rests in the grace of the world.
**Display passage:** 'When despair for the world grows in me / and I wake in the night at the least sound / in fear of what my life and my children's lives may be, / I go and lie down where the wood drake / rests in his beauty on the water.'
**Deckoviz:** The wood drake on still water at dawn. 'The grace of the world' as a natural Deckoviz slow living and nature collection concept. One of the great consolation poems for display.

### 49. A Blessing — James Wright (1963)
Two horses in a field in Minnesota — and the moment of pure joy at the end: 'if I stepped out of my body I would break / into blossom.'
**Display passage:** 'Suddenly I realize / That if I stepped out of my body I would break / Into blossom.'
**Deckoviz:** Horses in a twilight field. Blossoming as a visual concept. Pure joy in an ordinary moment — a natural Deckoviz moodscape concept.

### 50. This is Just to Say — William Carlos Williams (1934)
The most famous short poem in American literature — an apology note about eating plums from the icebox that are 'so sweet / and so cold.'
**Display passage:** 'Forgive me / they were delicious / so sweet / and so cold.'
**Deckoviz:** The plums in the icebox — one of poetry's great domestic images. Everyday pleasure as art. The note as typographic concept — a natural Deckoviz kitchen or morning collection poster.

### 51. The Red Wheelbarrow — William Carlos Williams (1923)
'so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow.' The most debated short poem in American literary history — and the most important statement of the Imagist principle that the concrete image is everything.
**Display passage:** 'so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow / glazed with rain / water / beside the white / chickens.'
**Deckoviz:** The red wheelbarrow in the rain. Attention to the ordinary as a visual concept. The Imagist principle as a Deckoviz philosophy.

### 52. In Just — e.e. cummings (1920)
Spring arriving with the balloonman — 'far and wee.' Cummings's typography as an integral part of the poem's meaning.
**Display passage:** 'in Just- / spring when the world is mud- / luscious the little / lame balloonman / whistles far and wee.'
**Deckoviz:** The spring world in cummings's lowercase typography. Mud-luscious and puddle-wonderful as visual concepts. Cummings's typographic style as a distinctive Deckoviz aesthetic.

### 53. somewhere i have never travelled — e.e. cummings (1931)
The most tender and most formally beautiful of cummings's love poems.
**Display passage:** 'somewhere i have never travelled, gladly beyond / any experience, your eyes have their silence.'
**Deckoviz:** 'Somewhere I have never travelled' as a Romantic Mode Deckoviz typographic concept. The silence of eyes as a visual concept. Cummings's lowercase love as poster art.

### 54. The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock → Preludes — T.S. Eliot (1915)
The city at dawn — short, numbered lyric fragments of urban desolation and beauty.
**Display passage:** 'The conscience of a blackened street / Impatient to assume the world.'

## Outro
One hundred poems. One hundred instances of language doing something that prose cannot do — compressing an entire world of feeling into a form small enough to memorise, to carry, to return to at three in the morning when nothing else will help.

What becomes clear, reading through this collection from Homer's wine-dark sea to Jack Gilbert's ruthless furnace, is that poetry has always done the same handful of things, in every culture and every century. It has tried to say what love feels like from the inside. It has tried to make peace with death — or to rage against it, which is its own kind of peace. It has tried to notice the world closely enough that an ordinary thing — a wheelbarrow, a fox at midnight, a pair of socks, two roads in a wood — becomes the whole of what needs to be said. It has tried, again and again, to give courage to people who are afraid, and company to people who are alone, and the right words to people who have feelings too large for their own vocabulary.

That is the gift these hundred poems offer. Not information. Not argument, exactly, though some of them argue. They offer the experience of recognition — the shock of finding that someone else, in another century, in another language, in another life entirely, felt exactly what you are feeling and found the words for it before you needed them.
