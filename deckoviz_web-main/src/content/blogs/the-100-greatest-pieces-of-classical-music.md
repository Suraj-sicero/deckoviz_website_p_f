## The 100 Pieces

### 1. Ludwig van Beethoven - Symphony No. 9 in D minor, Op. 125 ('Choral')
**Composed:** 1824

Beethoven's final symphony, completed when he was entirely deaf, remains one of the most extraordinary acts of creative will in human history. The fourth movement introduces the human voice into the symphony for the first time, with the famous 'Ode to Joy' setting Schiller's poem as a soaring choral finale.

**History & Context:** Premiered in Vienna in 1824, the audience gave it five standing ovations - Beethoven had to be turned around to see the applause he could not hear. It has since become a cultural monument, adopted as the European Union's anthem and performed at pivotal moments of historical significance the world over.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Ninth begins in barely audible trembling and builds toward one of the most overwhelming climaxes in all of music. It is about endurance, about triumph over darkness and deafness and despair. The final movement's message - that all humanity is united in joy - has never lost its force.

**Moods & States:** Triumph, awe, transcendence, unity, joy, catharsis

**On Deckoviz:** Perfect for Celebration Mode, landmark moments, and the Sublime collection. The Ode to Joy finale is one of the great pieces for marking life's biggest milestones.

### 2. Johann Sebastian Bach - Mass in B minor, BWV 232
**Composed:** 1749

Bach's crowning choral achievement, assembling a lifetime of sacred music into a single, towering work. The Mass in B minor spans the full arc of Christian liturgy but transcends any single faith - it is an act of devotion to the full range of human feeling.

**History & Context:** Bach compiled and revised the Mass throughout his life, completing it in the final years before his death in 1750. It was never performed in full during his lifetime. Today it is considered one of the supreme achievements of Western music.

**Why It Moves Us:** The sheer architecture of the piece - its vast choral movements, its delicate arias, its moments of shattering grief and boundless joy - creates an experience of immense spiritual weight. The Kyrie and the Crucifixus, in particular, reach depths of sorrow rarely touched in music.

**Moods & States:** Reverence, spiritual depth, solemnity, transcendence, inner quiet

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Deep Focus Mode and the Sacred & Sublime collection. Works with meditative visual art, stone textures, cathedral imagery.

### 3. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Requiem in D minor, K. 626
**Composed:** 1791

Mozart's final work, left unfinished at his death at 35 and completed by his student Franz Xaver Süssmayr. The Requiem is music written at the threshold of death - and it sounds exactly like that.

**History & Context:** Commissioned anonymously (later revealed to be Count Franz von Walsegg), the Requiem became legend when Mozart died before finishing it. The Lacrimosa breaks off at just eight bars - the last notes Mozart ever wrote.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Requiem moves between terror and consolation with almost unbearable intimacy. The Lacrimosa is perhaps the most beautiful piece of music ever written about grief. The Dies Irae terrifies. The final Lux Aeterna offers peace that feels hard-won and genuine.

**Moods & States:** Grief, solemnity, consolation, mortality, beauty in darkness

**On Deckoviz:** For Contemplative Mode, the Memory & Loss collection. Pairs with autumnal visual art, candlelight moodscapes, monochromatic palettes.

### 4. Ludwig van Beethoven - Piano Sonata No. 14 in C-sharp minor, Op. 27 No. 2 ('Moonlight')
**Composed:** 1801

The most beloved piano sonata ever written, and one of the most immediately recognizable openings in all of classical music. The first movement's hypnotic triplet arpeggios beneath a melody of quiet anguish have moved listeners for over two centuries.

**History & Context:** Written during Beethoven's early onset of deafness, a period of profound personal crisis. The nickname 'Moonlight' was coined by critic Ludwig Rellstab years after Beethoven's death, who described the first movement as moonlight reflected on Lake Lucerne.

**Why It Moves Us:** The sonata moves from nocturnal melancholy through a gentle middle movement into a ferocious, storm-like finale. It captures something true about the inner life - the way darkness gives way, eventually, to motion and force.

**Moods & States:** Melancholy, nocturnal beauty, introspection, yearning, eventual fire

**On Deckoviz:** Perfect for Evening Wind-Down Mode and the Night & Stillness collection. The first movement pairs naturally with moonlit visuals, deep blues, and slow-moving atmospheric art.

### 5. Frédéric Chopin - Nocturne in E-flat major, Op. 9 No. 2
**Composed:** 1832

Chopin's most famous nocturne, and the piece that essentially defined the genre. A singing melody over a gentle, rocking accompaniment - intimate, tender, and achingly beautiful.

**History & Context:** Chopin composed his nocturnes largely under the influence of John Field, the Irish composer who invented the form. But Chopin transformed the nocturne into something incomparably more personal and psychologically complex.

**Why It Moves Us:** There is a quality of private feeling in this piece that makes it feel like listening to someone's inner monologue. The ornamental variations of the melody on each return give it an improvisatory warmth, as if it is being invented in the moment.

**Moods & States:** Tenderness, intimacy, wistfulness, quiet love, nocturnal peace

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Romantic Mode and Late Night collections. Works beautifully with soft candlelight art, impressionist florals, warm amber tones.

### 6. Johann Sebastian Bach - Cello Suite No. 1 in G major, BWV 1007
**Composed:** c. 1720

Six suites for solo cello, of which the first is the most frequently performed and perhaps the most instantly recognizable. The Prelude - a cascade of arpeggios - is one of the most perfect pieces of music ever written for a single instrument.

**History & Context:** Bach composed the suites in Köthen, where he worked for Prince Leopold. They were largely forgotten until Pablo Casals discovered a copy in a Barcelona second-hand bookshop in 1889 as a teenager and devoted twelve years to learning them before performing them publicly.

**Why It Moves Us:** Solo Bach strips music to its essence. There is no accompaniment, no harmony - only a single instrument and the architecture of counterpoint. The result is music that feels both ancient and inexhaustibly modern. The G major Suite radiates warmth and clarity.

**Moods & States:** Clarity, groundedness, quiet joy, focus, timeless calm

**On Deckoviz:** Excellent for Morning Mode and the Focus & Flow collection. Works with minimalist art, clean geometric forms, natural textures.

### 7. Claude Debussy - Clair de lune (Suite bergamasque)
**Composed:** 1905

The third movement of Debussy's Suite bergamasque, and one of the most beloved piano pieces ever written. Impressionist in the fullest sense - music that evokes light on water, the texture of moonlit air, the feeling of a night that opens into something infinite.

**History & Context:** Debussy composed the suite in 1890 but did not publish it until 1905. Clair de lune - 'moonlight' - takes its title from a poem by Paul Verlaine, which evokes masked figures dancing in moonlight with a sadness they are barely aware of.

**Why It Moves Us:** Clair de lune is one of those rare pieces where the gap between musical technique and emotional effect virtually disappears. It sounds like what moonlight feels like - diffuse, shimmering, expansive, melancholy in the most beautiful way.

**Moods & States:** Dreamy, melancholic beauty, wonder, romantic longing, spaciousness

**On Deckoviz:** A signature piece for Romantic and Evening modes. Perfect with impressionist art, water and light imagery, deep blue and silver palettes.

### 8. Antonio Vivaldi - The Four Seasons (Le quattro stagioni)
**Composed:** 1725

Four violin concertos each depicting a season of the year, accompanied by sonnets (likely written by Vivaldi himself) describing scenes of pastoral life. The most programmatic and immediately evocative of all Baroque compositions.

**History & Context:** Published in 1725 as part of a collection of twelve concertos called Il cimento dell'armonia e dell'inventione (The Contest Between Harmony and Invention). They were enormously popular in Vivaldi's lifetime, largely forgotten after his death, and then rediscovered in the 20th century to become among the most recorded pieces in classical history.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Four Seasons is music that conjures physical sensation with extraordinary vividness - the bite of winter frost, the drowsy heat of summer, the melancholy of autumn rains. Each concerto is a complete world.

**Moods & States:** Vitality (Spring), heat and storm (Summer), wistful beauty (Autumn), crisp intensity (Winter)

**On Deckoviz:** Perfect for seasonal collections - Spring Mode, Autumn Mode, Winter Mode. One of the most versatile pieces in the Deckoviz musicscape library.

### 9. Franz Schubert - Symphony No. 8 in B minor, D. 759 ('Unfinished')
**Composed:** 1822

Two movements - and only two - of what was to be a full symphony. The Unfinished is one of the great mysteries and one of the great masterpieces of Romantic music. Why Schubert stopped, no one knows.

**History & Context:** Schubert composed the two movements in 1822 and then set the work aside, never completing it. It was only performed for the first time in 1865, 37 years after his death. The two movements are so self-contained and so perfect that many argue the symphony is complete as it stands.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Unfinished opens with a quiet, dark theme in the low strings that feels like a question without an answer. The music reaches moments of great lyrical warmth before returning to shadow. It is music about incompleteness - and about the beauty of what is left unsaid.

**Moods & States:** Melancholy, quiet longing, emotional depth, Romantic beauty, unresolved feeling

**On Deckoviz:** For Contemplative and Evening modes. Pairs with twilight imagery, incomplete forms, misty landscapes.

### 10. Johann Sebastian Bach - Goldberg Variations, BWV 988
**Composed:** 1741

An aria and 30 variations for harpsichord (or piano), one of the supreme achievements of contrapuntal writing in Western music. The variations range from the most intricate intellectual puzzles to moments of disarming simplicity.

**History & Context:** Supposedly commissioned by the Russian ambassador Count Keyserlingk for his insomniac nights - though this story may be apocryphal. Brought to wide public attention in 1955 by Glenn Gould's debut recording, one of the most influential classical recordings ever made.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Goldbergs open and close with the same aria - but the same music sounds entirely different at the end. Having been through thirty variations, the listener has changed. It is one of the most profound structural ideas in all of music.

**Moods & States:** Intellectual depth, focus, serenity, wonder, meditative clarity

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Deep Focus and Late Night modes. Works with architectural art, geometric abstractions, black and white palettes.

### 11. Gustav Mahler - Symphony No. 5 in C-sharp minor
**Composed:** 1902

A vast, turbulent, and ultimately triumphant symphony that spans the full range of Mahler's emotional universe. The Adagietto - the fourth movement - is perhaps the most achingly tender music he ever wrote.

**History & Context:** Composed at a peak of personal happiness - Mahler had just met and married Alma Schindler. The Adagietto is widely believed to be a private love letter to her. Luchino Visconti used it in his 1971 film Death in Venice, introducing it to a new generation.

**Why It Moves Us:** The symphony's journey from a funeral march to blazing D major triumph feels genuinely earned. The Adagietto, floating between strings and harp with no other instruments, is music of almost unbearable beauty and vulnerability.

**Moods & States:** Romantic love (Adagietto), turbulence, triumph, grandeur, vulnerability

**On Deckoviz:** The Adagietto is a centrepiece of the Romantic Mode collection. The finale works for Celebration and Epic modes.

### 12. Sergei Rachmaninoff - Piano Concerto No. 2 in C minor, Op. 18
**Composed:** 1901

The most beloved of all piano concertos, and the work that rescued Rachmaninoff from a three-year creative paralysis following the disastrous premiere of his First Symphony. Rich, sweeping, melodically inexhaustible.

**History & Context:** After the Symphony No. 1 disaster, Rachmaninoff could not compose for three years. He underwent hypnotherapy with Dr. Nikolai Dahl, who repeated 'You will compose again' until Rachmaninoff believed it. The Second Concerto was the result, and Rachmaninoff dedicated it to Dahl.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto's opening - solo piano chords rising from near-silence to a crashing declaration - is one of the great dramatic openings in music. The slow movement's central melody is one of the most beautiful ever written. The finale blazes with optimism.

**Moods & States:** Romantic passion, hope, grandeur, emotional warmth, triumph after struggle

**On Deckoviz:** Central to the Romantic Mode and the Great Love collection. Pairs with lush painterly landscapes, warm golds and crimsons.

### 13. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Piano Concerto No. 21 in C major, K. 467
**Composed:** 1785

The most performed of Mozart's 27 piano concertos, celebrated especially for its Andante movement - a melody of such sustained, almost impossibly perfect beauty that it became known worldwide after featuring in the 1967 Swedish film Elvira Madigan.

**History & Context:** Composed at the height of Mozart's popularity in Vienna, when he was at his most confident and prolific. The concerto was premiered by Mozart himself on the day after it was completed.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Andante floats above a pizzicato accompaniment like something half-dreamed. It is music that seems to exist slightly outside of time - serene, luminous, achingly graceful.

**Moods & States:** Serenity, graceful beauty, dreamlike stillness, emotional clarity

**On Deckoviz:** For Romantic and Morning modes. Works with soft impressionist art, water reflections, pale morning light palettes.

### 14. Johannes Brahms - Symphony No. 4 in E minor, Op. 98
**Composed:** 1885

Brahms's final symphony, and for many his greatest. Autumnal, deeply felt, architecturally immaculate. The finale - a passacaglia on a repeated bass line - is one of the most emotionally overwhelming movements in the symphonic repertoire.

**History & Context:** Brahms feared the symphony would not be understood - its structure is deliberately archaic, reaching back to Bach's time for the passacaglia form. His fears were unfounded. The premiere was met with rapturous applause.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Fourth Symphony has the weight of a life fully lived and looked at clearly. There is a quality of late-autumn light to the whole work - warm but fading. The finale, with its thirty variations on an unchanging bass, builds to a conclusion of devastating force.

**Moods & States:** Autumnal depth, emotional richness, gravitas, maturity, quiet grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Autumn Mode, the Late Light collection. Works with warm amber visual palettes, aged textures, forest imagery.

### 15. Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky - Piano Concerto No. 1 in B-flat minor, Op. 23
**Composed:** 1875

One of the most dramatically thrilling openings in the entire orchestral repertoire - a crashing horn fanfare answered by great sweeping piano chords. The concerto that established Tchaikovsky as a composer of international stature.

**History & Context:** When Tchaikovsky played the concerto for his mentor Anton Rubinstein, Rubinstein declared it unplayable and worthless. Tchaikovsky refused all changes. Hans von Bulow premiered it in Boston in 1875 to enormous success. Rubinstein eventually performed it himself.

**Why It Moves Us:** The first movement alone contains more memorable melody than most composers produced in a lifetime. The slow movement has a delicate central section of childlike charm. The finale erupts with Slavic exuberance and irresistible energy.

**Moods & States:** Heroic drama, passion, exuberance, warmth, triumphant energy

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Epic, Motivation, and Celebration modes. Works with bold visual art, dramatic landscapes, rich jewel-tone palettes.

### 16. Franz Schubert - String Quintet in C major, D. 956
**Composed:** 1828

Schubert's final chamber work, composed two months before his death at 31. By almost universal agreement, the slow movement - Adagio - is the most beautiful thing he ever wrote, and one of the most beautiful things in all of music.

**History & Context:** Schubert never heard it performed. It remained unpublished for 23 years after his death. The quintet uses an unusual scoring - two cellos instead of two violas - which gives the harmony an added warmth and depth from the lower register.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Adagio seems to exist in its own suspended time. It is music of such transparent tenderness that it is almost physically felt rather than merely heard. Knowing Schubert composed it near death gives it an almost unbearable poignancy - but the music itself transcends biography.

**Moods & States:** Transcendent beauty, tenderness, stillness, mortality and peace, deep emotional resonance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sublime and Sacred collections. Pairs with soft natural light visuals, serene abstract art, quiet evening palettes.

### 17. Dmitri Shostakovich - Symphony No. 5 in D minor, Op. 47
**Composed:** 1937

A symphony composed under extraordinary political pressure, after Stalin's regime had condemned Shostakovich's music as 'formalism.' Its triumphant finale was received as submission - or resistance. The ambiguity remains unresolved, and it is part of what makes it great.

**History & Context:** After the denunciation of his opera Lady Macbeth of the Mtsensk District in Pravda in 1936, Shostakovich feared for his life. The Fifth Symphony was his answer - officially a 'creative reply to just criticism.' The audience wept at the premiere.

**Why It Moves Us:** The first movement's anguish is unmistakable. The finale's apparent triumph has been read as sincere, as forced, as sardonic - and all three interpretations are plausible. Music that carries political and personal weight without ever losing its purely musical force.

**Moods & States:** Anguish, defiance, ambiguous triumph, human resilience, emotional complexity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Resilience and Human Stories collections. Works with stark, dramatic visual art, high contrast imagery.

### 18. Johann Sebastian Bach - St Matthew Passion, BWV 244
**Composed:** 1727

The greatest choral work ever composed, by virtually universal agreement. A setting of the Passion narrative from the Gospel of Matthew, for double choir, double orchestra, and soloists. It runs to over three hours and does not have a single weak moment.

**History & Context:** First performed on Good Friday 1727 in Leipzig's Thomaskirche. Largely forgotten after Bach's death, it was revived by the young Felix Mendelssohn in 1829, a performance that is credited with igniting the Bach revival and modern interest in historical performance.

**Why It Moves Us:** The St Matthew Passion contains some of the deepest expressions of grief in human art - the aria 'Erbarme dich' (Have mercy, Lord) for alto, violin, and sobbing orchestra is devastating. The final chorus, 'Wir setzen uns mit Tranen nieder' (We sit down in tears), is among the most moving things ever written.

**Moods & States:** Profound grief, compassion, spiritual depth, consolation, transcendence

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sacred, Deep Grief, and Contemplative collections. Pairs with dark, rich visual palettes, religious or meditative imagery.

### 19. Maurice Ravel - Bolero
**Composed:** 1928

Seventeen minutes of the same melody, repeated over and over, growing incrementally louder as instruments are added one by one. An obsessive, hypnotic, relentlessly building study in orchestral colour and cumulative power.

**History & Context:** Composed as a ballet for the dancer Ida Rubinstein, who commissioned it. Ravel described it as 'an experiment in a very special and limited direction' with 'no form properly speaking, no development, no or almost no modulation.' He thought audiences would hate it. They have adored it ever since.

**Why It Moves Us:** Bolero is unlike almost anything else in music. Its power is entirely architectural - the same melody and the same rhythm, given entirely new character by new instrumentation at every repetition. By the end, what began as a quiet snare drum whisper is a screaming orchestral climax.

**Moods & States:** Hypnotic focus, building energy, sensual intensity, inevitable momentum

**On Deckoviz:** For Focus Mode, the Build & Create collection, and work sessions that require sustained energy. Also works for Romantic Mode in the right context.

### 20. Samuel Barber - Adagio for Strings, Op. 11
**Composed:** 1936

The most famous piece of American classical music, and among the most performed pieces of any classical music in the world. A single, sustained, climbing melody for string orchestra that reaches an almost unbearable emotional peak before collapsing into silence.

**History & Context:** Barber sent the score to Arturo Toscanini, who returned it without comment - only for Toscanini's son to explain that the conductor had memorized it and did not need the score. Its NBC broadcast premiere in 1938 made it famous overnight. It was played at the funerals of FDR, Albert Einstein, and Princess Grace of Monaco, and has been used to mark countless moments of national mourning.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Adagio builds so slowly and so inevitably that the emotional release when it finally arrives feels physiological. It is one of the few pieces of music that can produce tears in people who rarely cry at music.

**Moods & States:** Grief, longing, cathartic sorrow, solemnity, profound beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For the Memory, Loss & Beauty and Contemplative collections. Works with monochromatic visual art, fading light, empty landscapes.

### 21. Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky - Swan Lake, Op. 20
**Composed:** 1876

The greatest of all classical ballets, and music of extraordinary range - from the lush romanticism of the White Swan theme to the fierce drama of the Black Swan. The score alone, independent of any staging, is a complete emotional world.

**History & Context:** The first production in 1877 was considered a failure. It was only after Tchaikovsky's death, in Petipa and Ivanov's 1895 revival, that Swan Lake became the definitive ballet experience it is today.

**Why It Moves Us:** The White Swan Adagio is one of the most heartbreakingly beautiful melodies in existence - pure yearning, pure grace. The Black Swan pas de deux is erotic, cunning, and irresistible. The tragedy of the ending carries genuine dramatic weight.

**Moods & States:** Romantic longing, graceful beauty, drama, passion, melancholy

**On Deckoviz:** White Swan themes for Romantic Mode; Black Swan for Seductive and Mysterious collections. Works with swan imagery, moonlit water, silver and midnight blue palettes.

### 22. Edward Elgar - Cello Concerto in E minor, Op. 85
**Composed:** 1919

Elgar's farewell to the Romantic era, composed after the First World War had shattered the world he knew. A work of immense personal loss and autumnal beauty, performed by the cellist with an intimacy that feels like private confession.

**History & Context:** The premiere was a disaster - the orchestra was under-rehearsed - and Elgar barely composed again. He died in 1934 thinking the concerto forgotten. Jacqueline du Pre's 1965 recording with Barbirolli changed everything, bringing it back to the fore with a performance of such raw emotional power that it became a modern classic.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto carries the grief of an era - the world before the war, lost forever. The slow movement is music of extraordinary tenderness. The finale, for all its energy, ends in a kind of exhausted acceptance. It feels like the end of something.

**Moods & States:** Elegiac grief, autumnal beauty, nostalgia, tenderness, quiet resignation

**On Deckoviz:** For the Autumn and Loss collections. Pairs with sepia-toned imagery, falling leaves, fading light.

### 23. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Symphony No. 41 in C major, K. 551 ('Jupiter')
**Composed:** 1788

Mozart's final and greatest symphony, and one of the supreme achievements of the Classical era. The finale - a fugue of five subjects woven together with breathtaking skill - is among the most astonishing displays of compositional mastery in all music.

**History & Context:** Composed in a six-week burst along with the two preceding symphonies (No. 39 and No. 40) in the summer of 1788, with no certain commission and no certain performance in mind. No one is entirely sure why Mozart wrote them. The nickname 'Jupiter' was given by the London impresario Johann Peter Salomon.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Jupiter combines physical energy, wit, lyrical grace, and intellectual power in a way that still feels miraculous. The coda of the finale, where all five fugue themes sound simultaneously, is a moment of pure exhilaration.

**Moods & States:** Joy, intellectual exhilaration, elegance, vitality, grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** For Celebration, Focus, and Morning modes. Works with luminous, classical visual palettes - blue sky, white marble, golden light.

### 24. Sergei Rachmaninoff - Symphony No. 2 in E minor, Op. 27
**Composed:** 1908

The most lushly melodic of all Romantic symphonies, and the most purely pleasurable. The third movement Adagio contains a theme so beautiful it has been used in popular music, films, and countless arrangements - and still has not been exhausted.

**History & Context:** After the catastrophe of Symphony No. 1, Rachmaninoff was terrified to write another. He composed it secretly in Dresden, away from Moscow and its judgements. The premiere in St Petersburg in 1908 was a triumph, and Rachmaninoff was awarded the Glinka Prize.

**Why It Moves Us:** The slow movement's clarinet theme - the most famous moment in the symphony - arrives with the quality of something inevitable, as if it had always existed and was just waiting to be written down. The symphony as a whole has a warmth and generosity almost unmatched in the repertoire.

**Moods & States:** Romantic warmth, melancholic beauty, longing, emotional richness, lush grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** Central to the Romantic and Great Love collections. Works with lush painted landscapes, warm evening light, deep crimson and gold palettes.

### 25. Franz Schubert - 'Winterreise' (Winter Journey), D. 911
**Composed:** 1828

A song cycle of 24 songs for voice and piano, setting poems by Wilhelm Muller about a man wandering alone through winter after losing the woman he loves. The most searching, the most desolate, and the most psychologically penetrating work in the entire song repertoire.

**History & Context:** Schubert composed it in the final year of his life, as his health was failing. He gathered his friends to hear the songs and reportedly said 'These songs have depressed me more than all the others.' His friends were baffled; they thought the songs too dark. Time has proved Schubert right.

**Why It Moves Us:** Winterreise goes further into loneliness and despair than almost any other piece of music - and somehow, because it renders those feelings with such precision and beauty, it becomes consoling. The final song, 'Der Leiermann' (The Hurdy-Gurdy Man), is one of the most devastating in music history.

**Moods & States:** Loneliness, desolation, existential wandering, acceptance, bittersweet beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For the Solitude, Winter, and Late Night collections. Works with snow imagery, grey palettes, empty roads, minimalist winter art.

### 26. Ludwig van Beethoven - String Quartet No. 14 in C-sharp minor, Op. 131
**Composed:** 1826

Seven movements played without a break, beginning with a fugue and ending with a tempestuous finale. Beethoven's most searching late string quartet, and the work he is said to have considered his greatest.

**History & Context:** One of the last works Beethoven completed. Franz Schubert heard it in a private performance shortly before his own death and said, 'After this, what is left for us to write?' The opening fugue was performed at Beethoven's funeral.

**Why It Moves Us:** The C-sharp minor Quartet exists at the very edge of human expression. Its seven movements form a continuous arc from dark, mysterious counterpoint through lyrical meditation, playful scherzo movements, and a finale of fierce, almost violent energy. It is music that feels like the inside of a mind working at the limits of its own capacity.

**Moods & States:** Searching depth, intellectual intensity, emotional complexity, transcendence, restless energy

**On Deckoviz:** For the Deep Thought and Inner World collections. Works with abstract visual art, dense textures, complex geometric palettes.

### 27. Richard Wagner - Tristan und Isolde: Prelude and Liebestod
**Composed:** 1865

The Prelude to Wagner's opera of obsessive love is the most harmonically revolutionary piece of music of the 19th century - the so-called 'Tristan chord' opened the door to atonality and changed Western music forever. The Liebestod (Love-Death) that closes the opera is the fullest expression of Romantic longing ever written.

**History & Context:** Wagner composed Tristan und Isolde under the influence of his own obsessive, unconsummated love affair with Mathilde Wesendonck. It premiered in Munich in 1865 under Hans von Bulow - whose wife Cosima later left him for Wagner.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Prelude and Liebestod together describe the complete arc of obsessive love - the yearning that cannot be satisfied, the death that is the only consummation. Isolde's final aria, rising through the orchestra to dissolution, is one of the most overwhelming experiences in all of opera.

**Moods & States:** Obsessive longing, ecstatic love, dissolution, transcendence, erotic intensity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Great Love and Dissolution collections. Works with deep red and twilight visual palettes, ocean imagery, dark romantic art.

### 28. Johannes Brahms - Intermezzo in A major, Op. 118 No. 2
**Composed:** 1893

One of the most intimate and heartfelt pieces in the piano literature. Among the last works Brahms wrote, it has the quality of a man looking back on a life and summing it up in seven minutes of extraordinary music.

**History & Context:** Composed in the last creative phase of Brahms's life, these late piano pieces were a departure - introspective, compressed, without any of the public grandeur of the symphonies. Clara Schumann, the love of Brahms's life, said of them: 'Each piece is a jewel.'

**Why It Moves Us:** The Intermezzo opens with a melody of such warm simplicity that it feels immediately familiar - as if you have heard it before in a dream. The contrasting middle section is more agitated, but the opening returns and closes the piece in a mood of the tenderest acceptance.

**Moods & States:** Warmth, tender nostalgia, acceptance, inner peace, late-evening beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For Evening Wind-Down, the Late Light, and Memory collections. Pairs with warm-toned intimate interiors, soft amber palettes, firelight imagery.

### 29. Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky - Violin Concerto in D major, Op. 35
**Composed:** 1878

The most exuberant and openly joyful of the great violin concertos - a work that sings, dances, and blazes with a Russian energy that is entirely irresistible.

**History & Context:** Composed in three and a half weeks while Tchaikovsky was recovering from his disastrous marriage. He was staying in Clarens, Switzerland, and the sunshine and the company of his student Joseph Kotek seemed to free him from depression. The violin part required a violinist of virtuoso calibre, and its first intended performer refused it as 'unplayable.'

**Why It Moves Us:** The slow movement - the Canzonetta - is one of the most purely beautiful melodies Tchaikovsky ever wrote. The finale is a Russian folk-dance romp of irresistible energy. The whole concerto feels like joy rediscovered after pain.

**Moods & States:** Joy, energy, warmth, exuberance, summer vitality

**On Deckoviz:** For Summer, Celebration, and Morning modes. Works with bright landscapes, warm golden palettes, open sky imagery.

### 30. Gustav Mahler - Symphony No. 9 in D major
**Composed:** 1909

Mahler's final completed symphony, and his farewell to the world - composed after his heart diagnosis and the death of his daughter. The last movement, Adagio, is music of a beauty so quiet and so final that it almost defies description.

**History & Context:** Mahler died before conducting a performance of the Ninth. Bruno Walter gave the premiere in 1912. Mahler had a famous superstition about ninth symphonies - Beethoven, Schubert, and Bruckner all died after their Ninths - and he knew this was likely his last.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Ninth begins with a broken, hesitating heartbeat rhythm in the cellos - an irregular pulse, widely believed to represent Mahler's own diseased heart. The final Adagio, dissolving into silence, is among the most moving pieces of music ever written. It is music of total acceptance.

**Moods & States:** Transcendence, farewell, acceptance, love for the world, profound peace

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sublime, Sacred, and Late Night collections. Works with dawn and dusk imagery, dissolving light, serene abstract art.

### 31. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Don Giovanni, K. 527: Overture and Selected Arias
**Composed:** 1787

Mozart's greatest opera, and one of the most perfect musical dramas ever created. The story of the seducer Don Giovanni blends comedy and terror with a moral seriousness that anticipates the 19th century. The overture alone is one of the most dramatically charged pieces in the repertoire.

**History & Context:** Premiered in Prague in 1787 to enormous success, shortly before the Vienna premiere. The overture was reputedly written the night before the premiere. Goethe called Don Giovanni 'a masterpiece' and Kierkegaard wrote a famous philosophical essay on its subject.

**Why It Moves Us:** The juxtaposition of comedy and dark inevitability in Don Giovanni is uniquely Mozart. The Commendatore's final scene - when the statue comes to drag Don Giovanni to hell - is one of the most terrifying passages in opera history.

**Moods & States:** Drama, seduction, dark comedy, moral gravity, theatrical intensity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Drama and Storytelling collections. Works with baroque architecture imagery, candlelit interiors, deep dramatic palettes.

### 32. Franz Liszt - Piano Sonata in B minor, S. 178
**Composed:** 1853

The most ambitious and technically demanding piano sonata since Beethoven, and a work that condenses the full range of Romantic expression into a single, continuous 30-minute movement. Heroic, demonic, lyrical, and profoundly architectural.

**History & Context:** Liszt dedicated it to Robert Schumann in return for Schumann's dedication of his Fantasie, Op. 17. It was premiered in 1857. Brahms reportedly fell asleep during the premiere (or pretended to), while Hans von Bulow called it 'the greatest musical work of the century.'

**Why It Moves Us:** The Sonata's single-movement form contains, in miniature, all four movements of a conventional sonata - a formal idea as audacious as it is brilliantly executed. It moves from mystery and violence to some of the most tenderly lyrical writing Liszt ever produced.

**Moods & States:** Heroic intensity, tenderness, demonic energy, Romantic passion, architectural grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** For the Epic and Power collections. Works with volcanic landscape imagery, bold visual art, deep black and crimson palettes.

### 33. Edvard Grieg - Piano Concerto in A minor, Op. 16
**Composed:** 1868

The most beloved Scandinavian concerto and one of the great Romantic piano works. Its opening - a crashing piano descent answered by the orchestra - is among the most familiar entrances in the repertoire.

**History & Context:** Composed when Grieg was just 24, during his honeymoon. He was inspired by Schumann's Piano Concerto, which has a similar spirit. The concerto was premiered by Edmund Neupert in Copenhagen in 1869, with Grieg conducting.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto breathes Scandinavian fresh air - there is something in its harmonies that evokes mountain landscapes and fjords, long northern light, the particular melancholy and exhilaration of those latitudes. The Adagio slow movement is of great lyrical warmth.

**Moods & States:** Romantic exhilaration, Nordic beauty, youthful passion, warmth, landscape feeling

**On Deckoviz:** For the Nordic and Nature collections. Works with fjord and mountain imagery, cool blue and grey palettes, natural light.

### 34. Johann Sebastian Bach - Violin Partita No. 2 in D minor, BWV 1004: Chaconne
**Composed:** c. 1720

The final movement of Bach's Second Violin Partita - a chaconne of fifteen minutes for solo violin alone, which is by universal consensus one of the greatest single movements in the entire musical canon.

**History & Context:** Johannes Brahms, in a letter to Clara Schumann, described it: 'On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings. If I imagined that I could have created, even conceived the piece, I am quite certain that the excess of excitement and earth-shattering experience would have driven me out of my mind.'

**Why It Moves Us:** The Chaconne is music on an almost impossibly grand scale compressed into a single instrument. It moves through darkness, struggle, radiance, and grief, ending with a final statement of the theme in a major key that feels like something hard-won. To hear it played by a great violinist is a transformative experience.

**Moods & States:** Struggle, transcendence, grandeur, grief, ultimate radiance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sublime and Human Triumph collections. Works with bold abstract art, stark landscapes, high-contrast monochromatic palettes.

### 35. Antonin Dvorak - Symphony No. 9 in E minor, Op. 95 ('From the New World')
**Composed:** 1893

Composed during Dvorak's time in America, the New World Symphony is a love letter to the American landscape filtered through Czech musical sensibility. The Largo slow movement - its cor anglais melody - is one of the most beautiful in all music.

**History & Context:** Dvorak was director of the National Conservatory of Music in New York from 1892 to 1895. He was deeply moved by African-American spirituals and Native American music, and wove their influence into this symphony. The Largo melody was later set to words as 'Goin' Home,' a beloved American spiritual.

**Why It Moves Us:** The New World Symphony feels like wide-open spaces - prairies, vast skies, the sweep of a new continent. The Largo has a quality of homesickness, of beauty tinged with longing for somewhere distant. The finale blazes with energy and joy.

**Moods & States:** Vastness, homesickness, landscape wonder, vitality, warm longing

**On Deckoviz:** For the Open World and Journey collections. Works with landscape photography, prairie and sky imagery, warm earth tone palettes.

### 36. Igor Stravinsky - The Rite of Spring (Le Sacre du Printemps)
**Composed:** 1913

The most revolutionary piece of music of the 20th century, and perhaps of all time. A violent, ecstatic, rhythmically brutal portrayal of pagan spring rituals culminating in a young woman dancing herself to death as a sacrifice.

**History & Context:** The premiere at the Theatre des Champs-Elysees in Paris on 29 May 1913 caused one of the most famous riots in musical history. The audience hissed, shouted, and fought - whether over the music, Nijinsky's choreography, or both. Within a decade it was recognized as a masterpiece.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Rite of Spring sounds like nothing that preceded it - its rhythms are unpredictable, its harmonies deliberately harsh, its energy almost animalistic. It captures something primal about human experience: the desire for sacrifice, for renewal, for spring at any cost.

**Moods & States:** Primordial energy, violence, ecstasy, primal fear, raw vitality

**On Deckoviz:** For the Primal Energy and Power collections. Works with earth-toned abstract art, fire imagery, raw organic textures.

### 37. Felix Mendelssohn - Violin Concerto in E minor, Op. 64
**Composed:** 1844

The most structurally innovative and melodically perfect of all violin concertos - Mendelssohn solved formal problems that had defeated other composers and produced something that sounds as fresh and as moving today as the day it was written.

**History & Context:** Mendelssohn worked on the concerto for six years, in constant consultation with his friend Ferdinand David, who gave the premiere in Leipzig in 1845. It was an instant success. Yehudi Menuhin called it 'one of the most intimate and sensitive' of all concertos.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto begins without orchestral introduction - the soloist enters immediately, with one of the most beautiful themes in the violin literature. The lyrical slow movement has a quality of private radiance. The finale dances with pure, uncomplicated joy.

**Moods & States:** Lyrical beauty, intimacy, radiance, joy, youthful warmth

**On Deckoviz:** For Morning and Romantic modes. Works with spring imagery, soft green and gold palettes, gentle light.

### 38. Franz Schubert - Piano Sonata in B-flat major, D. 960
**Composed:** 1828

Schubert's final piano sonata, composed in the last weeks of his life. Vast, unhurried, and of a lyrical beauty so sustained it has the quality of someone taking long breaths knowing they may be among the last.

**History & Context:** Written in September 1828, along with two other late sonatas. Schubert died in November at 31. The sonata was not published until 1838. When Alfred Brendel first recorded it, he wrote that it was among the three or four most important works he had encountered.

**Why It Moves Us:** The first movement's unusual length and refusal to hurry give the sonata a spacious, otherworldly quality. The slow movement, with a low trill that persists through the texture like a heartbeat, is among the most searching things Schubert wrote. It is music at the edge of something.

**Moods & States:** Profound stillness, valedictory beauty, spaciousness, existential depth, tender acceptance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Late Light and End of Day collections. Works with dusk imagery, open space art, soft blues and greys.

### 39. Johannes Brahms - Piano Concerto No. 2 in B-flat major, Op. 83
**Composed:** 1881

The most symphonic of all piano concertos, in four movements rather than the usual three, with a cello solo in the slow movement of heartbreaking beauty. A work of immense physical and intellectual scale.

**History & Context:** Brahms worked on it intermittently for 22 years. He described it to a friend with characteristic self-deprecation: 'I must tell you that I have written a tiny, tiny piano concerto with a tiny, tiny wisp of a scherzo.' It is, in fact, nearly 50 minutes long.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Andante slow movement, with its long cello solo, is the emotional heart - music of great lyrical depth and warmth. The finale has a playful lightness that provides relief from the weight of what has come before. The whole concerto feels like a full life in four movements.

**Moods & States:** Grandeur, depth, warmth, intellectual richness, emotional range

**On Deckoviz:** For the Epic and Deep Thought collections. Works with monumental natural imagery, rich warm palettes, late-afternoon light.

### 40. Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky - The Nutcracker, Op. 71
**Composed:** 1892

The most beloved Christmas ballet, and music of extraordinary range - from the magical Waltz of the Snowflakes to the delicate Waltz of the Flowers, from the whirling character dances of the Kingdom of Sweets to the soaring Grand Pas de Deux.

**History & Context:** The Nutcracker was not a success at its 1892 premiere; the ballet was considered too expensive and the choreography uninspired. It is now the financial backbone of ballet companies worldwide, performed hundreds of times each December.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Nutcracker contains some of Tchaikovsky's most inventive orchestration - the celesta, introduced here for the first time in Russian music, gives the Sugar Plum Fairy her shimmering, crystalline quality. The score is full of delights, surprises, and pure musical joy.

**Moods & States:** Wonder, festive joy, childhood magic, warmth, celebration

**On Deckoviz:** Central to the Winter and Celebration collections. Works with snow imagery, warm interior palettes, festive golden light.

### 41. Richard Strauss - Also sprach Zarathustra, Op. 30
**Composed:** 1896

A tone poem based on Nietzsche's philosophical novel, most famous for its opening fanfare - 'Sunrise' - which was used in Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey and became one of the most recognized passages of classical music in the world.

**History & Context:** Strauss was deeply attracted to Nietzsche's ideas about the Ubermensch and human self-transcendence, though he later admitted the music was less a philosophical argument than a musical impression of the book's imagery. The work premiered in Frankfurt in 1896.

**Why It Moves Us:** The opening three-note fanfare - quiet, then exploding into an overwhelming brass and organ crescendo - is perhaps the single most awe-inspiring moment in orchestral music. The rest of the work sustains a sense of vast philosophical and cosmic scale.

**Moods & States:** Awe, cosmic vastness, dawn, transcendence, heroic grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** Perfect for the Sunrise collection, Epic Mode, and Space-themed moodscapes. Works with dawn imagery, vast open landscapes, cosmic art.

### 42. Dmitri Shostakovich - String Quartet No. 8 in C minor, Op. 110
**Composed:** 1960

Written in three days in Dresden, reputedly after Shostakovich saw the ruins of the bombed city. A work of devastating personal intensity, saturated with self-quotation and musical signatures - Shostakovich described it as his own requiem.

**History & Context:** The quartet is permeated with Shostakovich's musical monogram (D-S-C-H, from his name in German notation) and quotes from his earlier works. He wrote it, he said, 'for myself' - as if no one else was meant to hear it.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Quartet begins and ends with a slow, aching threnody. In between, it quotes from Shostakovich's own earlier music as if reviewing his life, then breaks into a savage waltz, then crashes into silence. It is one of the most nakedly emotional works in the chamber repertoire.

**Moods & States:** Grief, self-examination, devastation, inner darkness, personal intensity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Memory and Deep Grief collections. Works with stark, monochromatic visual art, broken forms, grey winter imagery.

### 43. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - String Quartet No. 19 in C major, K. 465 ('Dissonance')
**Composed:** 1785

The last of Mozart's six 'Haydn' quartets, named for the extraordinary slow introduction to the first movement - 24 bars of chromatic dissonance so strange that contemporary musicians assumed the printed parts were full of errors.

**History & Context:** Mozart dedicated six string quartets to Joseph Haydn, who responded by telling Mozart's father: 'Before God and as an honest man I tell you that your son is the greatest composer known to me.' The Dissonance Quartet demonstrates exactly why.

**Why It Moves Us:** The introduction to the first movement sounds centuries ahead of its time - dark, chromatic, almost atonal. Then the Allegro begins and Mozart is immediately, irrepressibly himself. The contrast is electrifying.

**Moods & States:** Tension dissolving into grace, intellectual brilliance, elegant wit, musical surprise

**On Deckoviz:** For the Deep Thought and Classical Elegance collections. Works with marble and architecture imagery, classical palettes.

### 44. Bela Bartok - Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta
**Composed:** 1936

One of the most perfectly constructed works of the 20th century - a four-movement piece for divided string orchestra with piano, harp, celesta, and percussion. Its architecture is mathematical, its emotional range enormous.

**History & Context:** Commissioned by Paul Sacher and the Basel Chamber Orchestra. The first movement, a fugue beginning on a single note and expanding outward symmetrically, exemplifies Bartok's synthesis of folk music and modernist technique.

**Why It Moves Us:** The third movement - marked Adagio - is one of the most atmospheric and haunting pieces of the 20th century. Bartok called for 'night music,' and that is exactly what it sounds like: the sounds of the dark, alive with invisible creatures and restless energy.

**Moods & States:** Mystery, night, tension, architectural precision, haunting beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For the Night and Mystery collections. Works with dark, atmospheric visual art, forest imagery, deep black and silver palettes.

### 45. Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky - Symphony No. 6 in B minor, Op. 74 ('Pathetique')
**Composed:** 1893

Tchaikovsky's final symphony, premiered nine days before his death. A work of shattering emotional directness, ending not in triumph but in a slow, dying Adagio lamentoso - the only major symphony in the repertoire to end in this way.

**History & Context:** Tchaikovsky conducted the premiere himself. Nine days later he was dead - officially of cholera, though the circumstances have always been disputed. Whatever the cause, the Sixth Symphony sounds like a farewell from its first note to its last.

**Why It Moves Us:** The first movement's development builds to one of the most overwhelming climaxes in symphonic music before collapsing entirely. The beautiful second movement (in 5/4 - a limping waltz) has a quality of bittersweet charm. The final movement dissolves into darkness. There is nothing like it.

**Moods & States:** Grief, farewell, dark beauty, fateful inevitability, emotional devastation

**On Deckoviz:** For the Memory, Loss, and Late Night collections. Works with dark, spare visual art, winter imagery, deep indigo palettes.

### 46. Johann Pachelbel - Canon in D major
**Composed:** c. 1694

The most performed Baroque piece in history, built on an endlessly repeated eight-bar bass line over which three violins weave increasingly elaborate canonic variations. Simple, perfect, and inexhaustibly beautiful.

**History & Context:** Pachelbel's Canon was completely forgotten for centuries and only rediscovered in the 1960s. Jean-Francois Paillard's 1968 recording turned it into one of the most recognizable pieces of music in the world. It has been performed at more weddings than any other piece of classical music.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Canon's genius is in its simplicity - the ground bass is so stable that anything can happen above it, and everything sounds inevitable. By the end, the violins are ornamenting with pure melodic freedom, having earned it through thirty-some variations.

**Moods & States:** Serene joy, romantic warmth, timeless beauty, ceremony, gentle celebration

**On Deckoviz:** For Romantic, Morning, and Celebration modes. One of the most universally beloved moodscape pieces in the library.

### 47. Frederic Chopin - Ballade No. 1 in G minor, Op. 23
**Composed:** 1836

The first and greatest of Chopin's four ballades - a piece that tells a story without words, moving from a calm, questioning opening through lyrical warmth to one of the most dramatic and violent endings in the piano literature.

**History & Context:** Chopin was the first composer to apply the term 'ballade' to a solo piano piece. The precise story these ballades tell has never been confirmed, though it is widely believed they were inspired by the narrative poems of Adam Mickiewicz, which Chopin admired deeply.

**Why It Moves Us:** The coda of the G minor Ballade is one of the most astonishing moments in all piano music - it arrives with almost no warning and erupts into a torrent of notes of overwhelming force. The contrast between the preceding lyricism and the final violence is shattering.

**Moods & States:** Romantic storytelling, lyrical beauty, building tension, unleashed power

**On Deckoviz:** For the Drama and Storytelling collections. Works with dark narrative visual art, bold contrasts, stormy landscapes.

### 48. Hector Berlioz - Symphonie fantastique, Op. 14
**Composed:** 1830

The most explicitly autobiographical symphony ever written - Berlioz's fevered self-portrait of an artist who, consumed by unrequited love, takes opium, dreams, and descends into nightmare. The first great Romantic program symphony.

**History & Context:** Berlioz composed it in the grip of his obsession with the Irish actress Harriet Smithson, who did not know he existed. The symphony describes his alter ego's opium dream: pastoral idyll, ball scene, march to execution, and finally a Witches' Sabbath where his beloved is mocked.

**Why It Moves Us:** The final movement - the Witches' Sabbath - is one of the most original and terrifying pieces of music ever written. The distortion of the beloved's theme into something grotesque, the medieval Dies irae chant, the col legno strings - it sounds like a nightmare and feels like one.

**Moods & States:** Obsession, romantic ecstasy, dark imagination, horror, hallucinatory intensity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Dark Dreams and Drama collections. Works with surreal visual art, dark colour palettes, macabre or symbolic imagery.

### 49. Sergei Prokofiev - Piano Concerto No. 3 in C major, Op. 26
**Composed:** 1921

The most purely enjoyable modern piano concerto - brilliant, playful, rhythmically electrifying, and full of the dry wit and percussive energy that made Prokofiev one of the 20th century's great originals.

**History & Context:** Prokofiev assembled the concerto from sketches spanning a decade, completing it while working with Diaghilev and the Ballets Russes in Paris. He premiered it himself in Chicago in 1921. It was an immediate hit.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto's wit is as important as its brilliance - there is an ironic detachment in Prokofiev that is entirely his own. The second movement's theme-and-variations game between piano and orchestra is one of the most inventive passages in the 20th century concerto.

**Moods & States:** Wit, playful energy, rhythmic brilliance, controlled exuberance, cool intelligence

**On Deckoviz:** For the Focus and Playful Energy collections. Works with geometric modern art, cool urban palettes, precise visual design.

### 50. Johann Sebastian Bach - The Well-Tempered Clavier, Books I and II, BWV 846-893
**Composed:** 1722 & 1742

48 preludes and fugues - two for each major and minor key - composed as both a technical demonstration of equal temperament and a supreme collection of contrapuntal art. Every prelude and fugue is a world in miniature.

**History & Context:** The first book was completed in 1722; the second in 1742. Together they are considered the 'Old Testament' of keyboard music (the Beethoven sonatas being the 'New Testament'). Virtually every major composer who followed - Haydn, Mozart, Beethoven, Chopin, Brahms - studied them rigorously.

**Why It Moves Us:** The variety within the collection is extraordinary - from the luminous C major Prelude that opens Book I to the tragic chromatic Prelude in C minor, from the ferociously complex five-voice fugues to the simplest two-voice explorations. Each piece is perfectly crafted. Together, they are inexhaustible.

**Moods & States:** Focus, intellectual clarity, meditative depth, the full emotional spectrum

**On Deckoviz:** Ideal for Deep Focus and Flow modes. Works across multiple visual contexts - the sheer variety of the collection matches almost any art in the library.

### 51. George Frideric Handel - Messiah, HWV 56
**Composed:** 1741

The most performed large-scale choral work in history - an oratorio in three parts covering the prophecy, birth, death, and resurrection of Christ, culminating in the Hallelujah chorus that audiences have stood for since the 18th century.

**History & Context:** Handel composed the Messiah in an extraordinary burst of creativity - the entire work in 24 days. The tradition of standing for the Hallelujah chorus is said to originate from King George II rising in awe at a 1743 performance.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Hallelujah chorus is among the most thrilling pieces of choral music ever written - a great, blazing edifice of sound. But the quieter arias - 'He Was Despised,' 'I Know That My Redeemer Liveth' - carry equal weight and perhaps more enduring power.

**Moods & States:** Jubilation, spiritual awe, triumph, consolation, reverential grandeur

**On Deckoviz:** Central to the Sacred and Celebration collections. The Hallelujah chorus is ideal for milestone and landmark visual moments.

### 52. Gabriel Faure - Requiem in D minor, Op. 48
**Composed:** 1888

The most consoling of all Requiems - Faure deliberately excluded the Dies Irae (Day of Wrath) and focused on the promise of rest and peace. It is music of extraordinary gentleness and otherworldly beauty.

**History & Context:** Faure began it while his mother was ill and completed it shortly after his father's death. He said: 'My Requiem does not express the fear of death. I see death as a happy deliverance, an aspiration toward a happiness beyond the grave.' The In Paradisum for solo soprano is music of transcendent serenity.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Faure Requiem offers what no other does: the experience of death as something not terrible but beautiful. The Agnus Dei and In Paradisum have a quality of floating lightness, as if the body has been released. It is music that comforts at the deepest level.

**Moods & States:** Gentle consolation, spiritual peace, transcendent beauty, acceptance, otherworldly calm

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sacred, Memory, and Healing collections. Works with white and pale gold palettes, angelic or luminous visual art.

### 53. Cesar Franck - Symphony in D minor
**Composed:** 1889

The only symphony Franck completed, and one of the most organically unified large-scale works in the French repertoire - a single, vast cyclic structure in which themes from the first movement return, transformed, throughout all three.

**History & Context:** Premiered by the Paris Conservatoire Orchestra in 1889, it was poorly received by the academic establishment. Gounod dismissed it as 'the affirmation of incompetence.' Today it is recognized as a cornerstone of the symphonic repertoire.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Symphony's famous cor anglais theme in the slow movement - haunting, medieval, timeless - is one of the great original melodies of the 19th century. The finale's transformation of all the earlier material into triumphant D major has enormous satisfying force.

**Moods & States:** Spiritual grandeur, cyclic inevitability, warm emotional richness, triumphant resolution

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sacred Architecture and Journey collections. Works with cathedral imagery, deep jewel-tone palettes.

### 54. Camille Saint-Saens - Symphony No. 3 in C minor, Op. 78 ('Organ')
**Composed:** 1886

Saint-Saens's masterpiece, and the only 19th-century symphony to use the pipe organ to full transformative effect. The slow movement, in C major, with full organ, is one of the most overwhelming passages in the orchestral repertoire.

**History & Context:** Commissioned by and dedicated to the Royal Philharmonic Society of London. Saint-Saens described it as his 'complete effort' - a statement of everything he knew.

**Why It Moves Us:** When the organ enters in the slow movement, the entire acoustic universe of the concert hall changes. The effect is not just musical - it is almost physical. The final pages of the symphony, with organ, brass, and full orchestra, are among the most viscerally thrilling in all music.

**Moods & States:** Awe, spiritual grandeur, overwhelming power, triumph, sacred monumentality

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sacred, Epic, and Power collections. Works with cathedral and monumental architecture imagery, golden and deep blue palettes.

### 55. Modest Mussorgsky - Pictures at an Exhibition (orchestrated by Ravel)
**Composed:** 1874 / 1922

A piano suite describing a walk through an art exhibition, transformed by Ravel's orchestration into one of the most vividly coloured and imaginative orchestral showpieces in the repertoire. Each movement is a musical portrait of a different artwork.

**History & Context:** Mussorgsky composed the original piano suite in memory of his friend, the artist Viktor Hartmann. It sat largely unperformed until Ravel orchestrated it in 1922 at the request of conductor Sergei Koussevitzky. Ravel's orchestration is now considered a masterpiece in its own right.

**Why It Moves Us:** The 'Promenade' theme - the listener walking from picture to picture - is one of the most memorable brass calls in the repertoire. The suite culminates in 'The Great Gate of Kiev,' a finale of extraordinary grandeur and architectural sweep.

**Moods & States:** Wonder, visual imagination, grandeur, drama, playful wit

**On Deckoviz:** Perfect for the Art & Creativity collection. One of the most natural pairings for a visual art platform - music literally about looking at art.

### 56. Ludwig van Beethoven - Symphony No. 5 in C minor, Op. 67
**Composed:** 1808

The most famous symphony ever written, beginning with the most famous four notes in music - short-short-short-long, the rhythmic cell that Beethoven said represented 'Fate knocking at the door.' A 35-minute arc from darkness to blazing triumph.

**History & Context:** Premiered on 22 December 1808 in Vienna - on the same programme as the Sixth Symphony, the Fourth Piano Concerto, and the Choral Fantasy, in a concert lasting over four hours in an unheated theatre in the dead of winter. The Fifth was largely ignored by critics at the time.

**Why It Moves Us:** Few pieces of music tell a clearer story: darkness, struggle, and the ultimate emergence into light. The transition from the third movement into the finale - where the full orchestra, with trombones, piccolo, and contrafagotto entering for the first time - is one of the greatest dramatic coups in music.

**Moods & States:** Struggle, determination, overcoming, triumph, heroic energy

**On Deckoviz:** For Motivation, Resilience, and Epic modes. Works with bold visual art, dramatic contrast imagery, dark-to-light visual narratives.

### 57. Jean Sibelius - Symphony No. 2 in D major, Op. 43
**Composed:** 1902

Sibelius's most popular symphony, and one of the most purely emotionally satisfying in the repertoire. Its great D major finale, with its broad, singing melody over pizzicato strings, is music of triumphant, open-air beauty.

**History & Context:** Premiered in Helsinki in 1902 to an ecstatic response. Finns interpreted it as a patriotic response to Russian imperial oppression - Sibelius denied this, but said he understood why.

**Why It Moves Us:** The symphony builds across four movements with an organic naturalness that feels completely inevitable. The finale's great peroration is music of expansive, outdoor grandeur - it sounds like a Finnish landscape coming into summer light.

**Moods & States:** Triumphant beauty, landscape grandeur, open-air vitality, warm exhilaration

### 59. Robert Schumann - Piano Concerto in A minor, Op. 54

### 60. Olivier Messiaen - Quartet for the End of Time (Quatuor pour la fin du temps)
**Composed:** 1941

### 61. Arvo Part - Spiegel im Spiegel

### 63. Gustav Holst - The Planets, Op. 32
**Composed:** 1917

Seven movements, each depicting one of the planets of the solar system in astrological rather than astronomical terms - Mars the Bringer of War, Venus the Bringer of Peace, Jupiter the Bringer of Jollity, and so on.

**History & Context:** Holst composed it between 1914 and 1916, inspired partly by astrology and partly by theosophical ideas. It was premiered privately in 1918 and publicly in 1920. **Why It Moves Us:** Mars, the Bringer of War - the opening movement - invented the sound of modern cinematic war music. Its relentless 5/4 meter and grinding brass have influenced virtually every film score written since. Saturn, the Bringer of Old Age, is music of terrifying slow majesty.

### 64. Sergei Rachmaninoff - Vespers (All-Night Vigil), Op. 37
**Composed:** 1915

### 90. Johannes Brahms - A German Requiem, Op. 45
**Composed:** 1868

Unlike the Latin Catholic Requiem, which dwells on the terror of the Last Judgment, Brahms selected his own texts from the Luther Bible to create a Requiem intended entirely to comfort the living who mourn.

**History & Context:** Written partly in response to the death of Brahms's mother, and partly mourning his mentor Robert Schumann. The premiere established Brahms as one of the great composers of his age.

**Why It Moves Us:** 'Blessed are they that mourn, for they shall be comforted.' That is the opening line, and it sets the tone for a work of immense choral warmth and humanity. The second movement, 'For all flesh is as grass,' is a slow, darkly majestic march detailing human mortality, resolving into glorious major-key hope.

**Moods & States:** Comfort to the living, choral majesty, humanistic warmth, somber acceptance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Memory and Consolation collections. Works with autumnal imagery, soft enveloping art, warm brown and gold palettes.

### 91. Richard Strauss - Four Last Songs (Vier letzte Lieder)
**Composed:** 1948

The ultimate autumnal masterpiece of romantic music. Written when Strauss was 84, these four songs for soprano and orchestra are a serene, glowing farewell to life, art, and the world.

**History & Context:** Strauss set three poems by Hermann Hesse and one by Joseph von Eichendorff ('Im Abendrot' - 'At Sunset'). He died before hearing them performed. The premiere was given in London in 1950 by Kirsten Flagstad, conducted by Wilhelm Furtwangler.

**Why It Moves Us:** The final song, 'Im Abendrot,' depicts an old couple watching the sunset together. Two larks (represented by two trilling flutes) rise into the sky. The voice asks, 'O vast, tranquil peace... is this perhaps death?' It is music of accepting, golden, twilight beauty.

**Moods & States:** Twilight beauty, farewell, peace, golden nostalgia, finality

**On Deckoviz:** For the Sunset and Late Light collections. Works perfectly with twilight imagery, golden hour photography, soft fading light.

### 92. Igor Stravinsky - The Firebird (L'Oiseau de feu)
**Composed:** 1910

Stravinsky's breakthrough work, written for Diaghilev's Ballets Russes in Paris when the composer was mostly unknown. It is a brilliant, glittering orchestral showcase based on Russian fairy tales.

**History & Context:** The commission was initially offered to Anatoly Lyadov, who procrastinated too long. The young Stravinsky got the job and changed the course of 20th-century music. The premiere in Paris was a triumph.

**Why It Moves Us:** The 'Infernal Dance of King Kashchei' is thrillingly violent and rhythmic, prefiguring The Rite of Spring. But the 'Berceuse' (Lullaby) and the final 'Finale' are completely different - luminous, magical, and ending with a majestic brass hymn that feels like a sun rising over the world.

**Moods & States:** Magic, brilliance, orchestral colour, fairytale triumph

**On Deckoviz:** For the Magic and Fire collections. Works with glittering visual art, vibrant reds and golds, fire and dawn imagery.

### 93. Johann Sebastian Bach - Toccata and Fugue in D minor, BWV 565
**Composed:** c. 1704

The most famous piece of organ music ever written. From the opening thunderous flourish down to the massive dramatic chords at the end, it is the ultimate expression of the pipe organ's power.

**History & Context:** Passed down only in a single manuscript copy made by a student, it is generally believed to belong to Bach's youth. Due to its theatricality (partly thanks to its use in countless horror films and Disney's Fantasia), it is one of the most universally recognized themes in Western music.

**Why It Moves Us:** It is music of absolute architectural drama. It does not comfort; it awes. The organ is capable of sustaining sound endlessly, and the Toccata uses that capability to create walls of sound that command total attention.

**Moods & States:** Gothic drama, architectural power, thunderous energy, Halloween macabre

**On Deckoviz:** For the Gothic and Power collections. Works with dark cathedral imagery, dramatic shadow art, intense organ pipes.

### 94. Ludwig van Beethoven - Piano Concerto No. 5 in E-flat major, Op. 73 ('Emperor')
**Composed:** 1809

Beethoven's final piano concerto, composed while Vienna was under bombardment by Napoleon's army. It is a work of unshakeable heroic grandeur, earning its (non-Beethoven) nickname.

**History & Context:** Beethoven spent the bombardment hiding in his brother's basement with pillows over his ears to protect his failing hearing. Yet the music he wrote is confidently, majestically victorious. The premiere was given in Leipzig in 1811; Beethoven's deafness prevented him from performing it himself.

**Why It Moves Us:** The concerto begins with the soloist immediately asserting dominance through massive cadenzas. It is music that refuses to be defeated. The slow movement, however, is a hymn of suspended, lyrical beauty that provides an emotional haven before the galloping finale.

**Moods & States:** Heroic grandeur, victory, unshakeable confidence, lyrical peace

**On Deckoviz:** For the Epic and Motivation modes. Works with monumental imagery, soaring eagles, bright confident golds and blues.

### 95. Bela Bartok - Concerto for Orchestra
**Composed:** 1943

Written while Bartok was struggling with leukemia and financial hardship in American exile. It is an orchestral masterpiece where every section of the orchestra is treated as a virtuoso soloist.

**History & Context:** Commissioned by the conductor Serge Koussevitzky for the Boston Symphony Orchestra, it gave the dying Bartok a final creative surge. It was an immediate success and secured a measure of financial security for his final days.

**Why It Moves Us:** The 'Elegia' third movement is music of profound homesickness and grief (Bartok called it 'a lugubrious death-song'). But the rest of the piece, particularly the finale, blazes with an unbreakable, life-affirming folk vitality. It is the sound of an artist refusing to go quietly.

**Moods & States:** Virtuosity, nostalgic grief, defiant vitality, orchestral brilliance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Resilience and Brilliant Colour collections. Works with fast-moving visual art, stark contrasts, Eastern European folk motifs.

### 96. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Le nozze di Figaro (The Marriage of Figaro), K. 492: Overture & Selected Arias
**Composed:** 1786

The perfect comic opera, which also happens to be a profound commentary on class, forgiveness, and human frailty. The Overture is a breathless, whispered opening that explodes into joyous energy perfectly mirroring the plot.

**History & Context:** Based on Beaumarchais's play, which had been banned in Vienna for its subversive political content (servants outwitting their masters). Mozart and his librettist Lorenzo Da Ponte convinced the Emperor to allow it by removing explicit politics, replacing it with something deeper: universal human truth.

**Why It Moves Us:** The Countess's aria 'Dove sono' (where she mourns her lost happiness) is incredibly moving. The finale, where the Count begs his wife's forgiveness and she grants it, is one of the most radiant moments of grace in all art.

**Moods & States:** Wit, breathless energy, profound forgiveness, humanity

**On Deckoviz:** For the Grace and Human Stories collections. Works with classical interiors, light swift visual motion, pastel palettes.

### 97. Antonin Dvorak - Cello Concerto in B minor, Op. 104
**Composed:** 1895

Universally regarded as the greatest of all cello concertos. Composed in America, it is tinged with homesickness and grief for his dying sister-in-law (who was also his early love).

**History & Context:** Dvorak originally felt the cello was not a good solo instrument ('it mumbles in the bass and squeaks in the treble'). He changed his mind after hearing a concerto by Victor Herbert. He continuously revised the ending of his own concerto as news of his sister-in-law's declining health reached him.

**Why It Moves Us:** The cello is arguably the instrument closest to the human voice, and here it sings with incredible nostalgic warmth. The final movement slowly dissolves into memory, recalling a song his sister-in-law loved, before a final burst of orchestral energy.

**Moods & States:** Deep nostalgia, noble sorrow, romantic sweep, singing beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For the Nostalgia and Deep Emotion collections. Works with sunset imagery, autumnal landscapes, deep wood tones.

### 98. Giacomo Puccini - La Boheme: Selected Arias ('Che gelida manina', 'Mi chiamano Mimi')
**Composed:** 1896

The most beloved of all romantic tragedy operas. A story of young impoverished artists in Paris, driven by melodies of immediate, heart-on-sleeve emotion.

**History & Context:** Premiered in Turin, conducted by a 28-year-old Arturo Toscanini. Initial critical reaction was mixed, but the public loved it instantly. It has remained one of the most performed operas worldwide ever since.

**Why It Moves Us:** Puccini was the master of manipulating audience emotion. In the first act, when the poet Rodolfo warms the seamstress Mimi's freezing hand ('Che gelida manina' - What a cold little hand), the music swells into a statement of young love so direct and overwhelming that it works every single time.

**Moods & States:** Youthful romance, tragic love, Parisian atmosphere, tearful beauty

**On Deckoviz:** For the Great Love and Tragedy collections. Works with Parisian winter imagery, intimate garret settings, bohemian aesthetics.

### 99. Dmitri Shostakovich - Symphony No. 10 in E minor, Op. 93
**Composed:** 1953

A massive, brooding symphony composed immediately following the death of Josef Stalin. It is widely interpreted as Shostakovich's depiction of the Stalin years, ending with a savage, blazing assertion of his own identity.

**History & Context:** Shostakovich had kept many of his true feelings hidden for years under threat of death. He premiered the Tenth Symphony mere months after Stalin died. The ferocious second movement is reputed to be a 'musical portrait of Stalin.'

**Why It Moves Us:** The third movement introduces Shostakovich's musical signature (D-S-C-H), which interacts with a theme representing a female student he was infatuated with. In the finale, the D-S-C-H signature is hammered out by the full orchestra, over and over, drowning out everything else - 'I am here, and I survived.'

**Moods & States:** Fear, brutality, survival, ultimate defiance

**On Deckoviz:** For the Resilience and Defiance collections. Works with stark industrial imagery, oppressive architecture breaking into individual light.

### 100. Camille Saint-Saens - The Carnival of the Animals: 'The Swan' (Le Cygne)
**Composed:** 1886

The 13th movement of a humorous musical suite about animals. It is the only movement Saint-Saens allowed to be published in his lifetime, and it became one of the most famous melodies ever written for the cello.

**History & Context:** Saint-Saens wrote the suite as a private joke for friends and forbade its publication because he thought its frivolity would damage his reputation as a serious composer. He exempted 'The Swan.' The choreographer Michel Fokine later created the famous solo dance 'The Dying Swan' to this music for Anna Pavlova.

**Why It Moves Us:** The cello glides smoothly over rippling pianos, creating a perfect musical analogue of a swan gliding gracefully over water while paddling furiously beneath. It is pure, serene, floating beauty.

**Moods & States:** Grace, serene flow, floating beauty, gentle peace

**On Deckoviz:** For the Serenity and Water collections. Works with slow motion water imagery, white and blue aesthetics, perfect calm.
